# Deliverable 3 — Vulnerability Assessment: Aperture

**Date**: 2026-05-12
**Facilitator**: Petrichor Projects

The bear case as a sophisticated short would write it, organized as five attacks. Each attack: the line of argument, the evidence behind it, Aperture's prepared counter, and the pivot move when the counter doesn't fully close the gap.

---

## Attack 1 — "The NRR is best-quarter cherry-picking; the business is a 117% NRR business, not a 130% NRR business."

**The attack**: The Series C deck leads with 130% NRR. A trailing four-quarter recompute returns 117%. Best-quarter selection is a tell — the team chose the most flattering data point and put it on the cover slide.

**The evidence**: Q2 2025 quarterly NRR = 130%. Trailing-four ending Q1 2026 = 117%. Cohort retention shows the 130 was driven by two large expansion deals that closed in a single quarter and have not repeated.

**Our prepared counter**: Restate the deck before the partner meeting. "Trailing four-quarter NRR is 117%. Best quarter was 130%, driven by [two expansion deals], which we expect to repeat at 2-3 per year at current ACV." Show the cohort chart with both numbers, both contexts.

**Our pivot move**: If the partner still pushes, pivot to gross retention (target: >95%) and net dollar retention by entry-year cohort. A 117% NRR with 96% gross retention is a healthier business than a 130% NRR with 88% gross retention — and Aperture's gross retention is 96%. Make the partner argue against the floor, not the ceiling.

---

## Attack 2 — "The 14 OEMs is historical. Three are churned or paused. The active count is 11, and the trajectory is down."

**The attack**: The 14-of-25 claim is anchored to ever-contracted, not currently active. Three OEMs have rolled off since Q2 2025 — one acquisition spin-down, one budget freeze, one competitive displacement. The deck does not disclose this.

**The evidence**: CRM contract-in-force filter returns 11 active OEM accounts. Three accounts (OEM-7, OEM-12, OEM-19, anonymized) are marked paused or churned with timestamps between Q3 2025 and Q1 2026.

**Our prepared counter**: Restate the slide as "11 active enterprise OEM customers; 14 historical, of which 3 rolled off in 2025-2026 for reasons we have documented." Pre-empt the question. Show the win-back motion in flight for the two non-competitive losses.

**Our pivot move**: Show net new OEM logo adds — 4 net new in 2025, 2 already signed in 2026. The active count is 11 today, will be 13+ by Series C close. Make the trajectory of the count, not the count, the story.

---

## Attack 3 — "The +95% growth claim is a single-quarter compare. Trailing is +68%. The deck pitches a J-curve that the trailing data does not support."

**The attack**: Headline growth of +95% Y/Y is Q1 2025 vs. Q1 2024 — the easiest comparison window in the company's history. Trailing four-quarter growth is +68%. The J-curve thesis (aerospace / pharma / semiconductor expansion) is the implied lever to reaccelerate, but the non-automotive pilot funnel is converting at 62% (38% churn) and represents only 6% of current revenue.

**The evidence**: Trailing-four ARR growth = +68%. Q1 2025 vs. Q1 2024 = +95% (single-quarter compare). Non-automotive ARR mix = 6%. Non-automotive pilot churn = 38% (last 18 months of pilot starts).

**Our prepared counter**: Restate growth as "+68% trailing four quarters." Reframe trajectory shape from J-curve to "linear-plus-optionality" — durable automotive core compounding at 60-70%, three vertical bets with named conversion milestones and dates. Publish the pilot funnel honestly: pilot starts, pilot-to-paid conversion rate, time-to-conversion by vertical, why-lost categorization.

**Our pivot move**: If the partner discounts the optionality, lean back into the automotive durability. The defensible story is "the operator-built inspection layer 11 of the top automotive OEMs run on, growing 60-70% on a base where 96% of revenue is automotive." That is a fundable business at $300M-$350M post on trailing. The aerospace / pharma / semiconductor optionality becomes a step-up trigger in the term sheet, not a load-bearing claim in the deck.

---

## Attack 4 — "The price ask is internally inconsistent. 10x forward on a growth rate that is decelerating."

**The attack**: $400M post on $42M trailing ARR = 9.5x trailing. The deck pitches "10x" with the multiple basis unstated. Partners will assume trailing and price accordingly. Even at 10x forward ($71M end-of-year ARR), the round requires sustained +69% growth — but the trailing-four rate is +68% and decelerating quarter over quarter.

**The evidence**: Q4 2024 ARR growth = +74%; Q1 2025 = +73%; Q2 2025 = +71%; Q3 2025 = +69%; Q4 2025 = +66%; Q1 2026 = +64%. Trailing-four ending Q1 2026 = +68%. The growth curve is rolling, not snapping back.

**Our prepared counter**: Restate the multiple basis explicitly: "$400M post = 10x forward ARR / 5.6x trailing ARR. Forward ARR assumes +69% growth in the Series C year, consistent with trailing." Add a step-up grid: $350M post at signing, step up to $400M on hitting [milestone 1: aerospace 3 paid customers, milestone 2: trailing-four NRR back to 122%].

**Our pivot move**: If the partner won't underwrite $400M, take $350M with a clean structure. The deck should not die on the valuation ask — it should die only if the business cannot defend $300M, which it can.

---

## Attack 5 — "Reference customers are not in shape. The deck names five; four carry diligence risk."

**The attack**: Of the five named reference customers in the deck, two have champion changes since Q4 2025 (one acquisition-driven), one is in active renewal negotiation asking for a 15% discount, and one has been paused since February 2026 on a budget freeze. Only one of five is a clean strong-reference call today.

**The evidence**: CS CRM relationship arcs for the five named accounts. Last meaningful champion touchpoint:
- Account A — 142 days ago, new VP unfamiliar with Aperture.
- Account B — 91 days ago, acquired by larger entity, integration in flight.
- Account C — 8 days ago, renewal in negotiation, discount ask on the table.
- Account D — 109 days ago, paused account, no path to re-start in next 90 days.
- Account E — 4 days ago, expansion deal closing, strong relationship.

**Our prepared counter**: Replace Account D before any reference list ships. Re-anchor Accounts A and B with new champions in the next 60 days — CEO-led, Head of CS-supported, executive sponsor outreach. Run a mock reference call with each before partner exposure. Document the relationship arc for the diligence binder so the partner sees the work, not just the result.

**Our pivot move**: If a partner pre-empts and contacts an unprepared reference, get ahead of the call within 24 hours. Brief the partner: "We're mid-transition with the buyer at Account A; the new VP is fully on the platform but hasn't been briefed for a reference call. Our Head of CS will introduce — give us 7 days." Diligence partners respect transparency; they do not respect surprise.

## Trajectory Shape Mismatch — Standalone Note

The J-curve claim and the linear-plus-optionality reality are the single most consequential mismatch in the deck. Five of the five attacks above touch this mismatch directly or indirectly. The Y/Y comparison cherry-picks (Attack 3), the price math depends on continued J-curve growth (Attack 4), the reference list cannot demonstrate vertical expansion success (Attack 5).

**Fix the trajectory shape first.** Recompute trailing-data slides (14 days). Reframe the deck around durable automotive + three explicit optionality bets. The other four attacks substantially weaken when the deck stops claiming a J-curve it cannot show.

## Pilot Conversion Risk — Standalone Note

**38% pilot churn in non-automotive verticals** is the leading indicator of the J-curve failure. The next 90 days should produce a documented pilot conversion playbook — pre-pilot qualification criteria, in-pilot success metrics, time-to-conversion targets, kill criteria. Without this, the next four quarters of non-automotive pilots churn at the same rate, and the J-curve evidence remains absent at Series C signing.
