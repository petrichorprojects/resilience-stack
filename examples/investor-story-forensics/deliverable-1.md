## Claim Class 1 — Why-We-Win

**Verbatim claim**: "Built by manufacturing operators, for manufacturing operators."

**Reality**: Three of four founders were line-level QA or plant-floor operators at industrial OEMs before founding Aperture. Defensible. Specificity to competitors (Cognex, Keyence, AI-native upstarts) is not articulated in the deck.

**Gap severity**: LOW. The claim is true; the deck under-uses it.

**What a sophisticated investor will model**: "Operator origin is real and rare in this category. Does it convert into faster sales cycles, lower CAC, or higher gross retention than competitors? If yes, this is a structural moat. If not, it is brand poetry."

**Remediation**: Add one slide quantifying the operator advantage — sales cycle days vs. category benchmark, top-of-funnel conversion from operator referrals, share of new logos sourced through operator networks. Convert the line from poetry to math.

**Verdict**: Defensible. Strengthen, don't rebuild.

## Claim Class 2 — Why-Now

**Verbatim claim**: "AI-native inspection is the inevitable next layer of factory ops."

**Reality**: The category thesis is correct and well-trafficked. Every AI-native inspection vendor in the space makes a version of this claim. Aperture's specific edge inside the thesis ("why us, given the thesis is real") is asserted at the operator-origin level but not connected to the Why-Now.

**Gap severity**: LOW-MEDIUM. The claim is true but generic.

**What a sophisticated investor will model**: "The thesis is uncontroversial. Twelve companies in my pipeline pitch this same Why-Now. What is Aperture's wedge inside it — buyer archetype, regulatory tailwind, capital-formation cycle? If the wedge is generic, this is one of twelve, and I'm pricing accordingly."

**Remediation**: Connect Why-Now to Why-Us in a single line. "AI-native inspection is the inevitable next layer — and the operator-built inspection layer wins the buyer who has to live with the false-positive rate." Anchor with two operator quotes.

**Verdict**: Defensible. Tighten coupling to Why-Us.

## Claim Class 3 — Why-Us

**Verbatim claim**: "Founder-led, $42M ARR, 130% NRR, 14 of 25 top global automotive OEMs."

**Reality**:
- $42M ARR: correct.
- 130% NRR: best-quarter (Q2 2025). **Trailing four quarters: 117%**.
- 14 of 25 OEMs: 14 ever-contracted, **11 currently active**. Three churned or paused since Q2 2025 (one acquisition spin-down, one budget freeze, one competitive displacement).

**Gap severity**: HIGH. Two of four headline numbers fail the trailing-data test. The deck will not survive the data room.

**What a sophisticated investor will model**: "NRR cherry-picked to best quarter. Logo count cherry-picked to peak. The trailing reality is 117% NRR and 11 active OEMs — a B+ business presented as an A+ business. Adjust valuation 1-2 turns down. Add a discount for diligence-discovered downside surprises."

**Remediation**:
1. Restate NRR slide: "Trailing four-quarter NRR: 117%. Best quarter (Q2 2025): 130%."
2. Restate logo slide: "11 active enterprise OEM customers; 14 historical."
3. Add cohort retention chart by entry year (proves the 117 is durable, not in decline).
4. Get ahead of the three churned OEMs with a one-line "lessons learned" footnote — diligence will find them; the question is whether Aperture explains them first.

**Verdict**: Vulnerable. Rebuild the slide before any partner meeting.

## Claim Class 4 — Why-This-Trajectory

**Verbatim claim**: "+95% Y/Y, expanding to aerospace/pharma/semiconductor."

**Reality**:
- +95% Y/Y was Q1 2025 vs. Q1 2024. **Trailing four-quarter growth: +68%.**
- Non-automotive verticals: **6% of revenue, 38% pilot churn.**
- Aerospace pilots stall on regulatory qualification. Pharma stalls on validation cycles. Semiconductor stalls on integration timeline.

**Gap severity**: HIGH. The headline growth number is best-period, not trailing. The expansion story is asserted, but the pilot funnel does not yet support it.

**What a sophisticated investor will model**: "Growth is +68%, not +95%. Expansion thesis is unproven — 38% pilot churn in the verticals being sold as the J-curve. This is not a multi-vertical company yet; it is a single-vertical company with three unproven optionalities. Price as single-vertical, give optionality credit only on milestones."

**Remediation**:
1. Restate growth slide: "+68% trailing four quarters, +95% best quarter (Q1 2025)."
2. Reframe trajectory shape from J-curve to "linear-plus-optionality" — durable automotive core, three stated vertical bets with conversion targets and dates.
3. Publish the pilot funnel honestly: pilot starts, pilot-to-paid conversion, time-to-conversion, why-lost categorization.
4. Set explicit milestones per vertical so the optionality is investable: e.g., "Aerospace: 5 paid customers by month 18 or vertical de-prioritized."

**Verdict**: Vulnerable. Rebuild trajectory shape; do not pitch J-curve without the data.

## Claim Class 5 — Why-This-Price

**Verbatim claim**: "Series C at $400M post (10x), $1.5B exit in 36 months."

**Reality**:
- $400M post = 10x. 10x of what? Forward ARR at end of Series C year, not trailing.
- Trailing growth is +68%, not +95%. The implied forward ARR (~$71M) requires +69% growth to hit; current trajectory supports it, but the multiple is being pitched off a number Aperture has not yet earned.
- $1.5B exit in 36 months implies ~$150M ARR with a 10x multiple, or ~$100M ARR with a 15x multiple. Either path requires continued +60-70% growth for three years, which has never been demonstrated in a four-year window.

**Gap severity**: HIGH. The price is internally inconsistent with the trailing data shown elsewhere in the same deck.

**What a sophisticated investor will model**: "10x forward is the ask. 10x trailing is $420M post on $42M ARR — they're asking for the same number but on a number they haven't earned. The exit math requires three more years at this growth rate without a deceleration quarter. I'm pricing this at $280M-$320M post on trailing, with a step-up tied to non-automotive proof points."

**Remediation**:
1. Restate the multiple basis: "Trailing ARR $42M; forward ARR $71M; pricing at 10x forward / 5.6x trailing."
2. Move the $1.5B exit number out of the deck. It is a board story, not an investor story. A partner cannot underwrite a 36-month exit — they can underwrite the next 18 months of milestones.
3. Add a milestones-to-valuation grid: what proof points unlock each step-up in the round.

**Verdict**: Vulnerable. The price ask is too far ahead of the trailing data shown.
