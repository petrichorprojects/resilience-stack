# Deliverable 2 — Competitive Pricing Map

**Company**: Helix
**Comparison set**: LegacyVendor (incumbent, 25-year market share leader), BookCal (horizontal scheduling tool repositioning into healthcare), ScrubsScheduler (vertical-native, growth-stage competitor)
**Audit date**: 2026-05-12

---

## 1. Tier-by-Tier Price Comparison

List prices, annual, per-clinic (not per-provider) for a normalized 50-provider mid-market reference account.

| Tier | Helix | LegacyVendor | BookCal | ScrubsScheduler |

| Mid / Pro | $45,000 | $78,000 | $32,000 | $52,000 |

## 2. Value-Per-Dollar Positioning (Helix by Tier)

| Helix tier | Position | Reading |
|------------|----------|---------|
| Starter | **Discount to LegacyVendor (-50%), parity-plus vs BookCal, discount to ScrubsScheduler (-17%)** | Helix is the value play. Feature parity with LegacyVendor at half price. Buyer interprets as "deal" not "premium." |
| Pro | **Discount to LegacyVendor (-42%), premium to BookCal (+41%), discount to ScrubsScheduler (-13%)** | Sitting below the vertical-native competitor while delivering equal capability. Pricing leaves authority on the table. |
| Enterprise | **Discount to LegacyVendor (-35%) but structurally compromised** | List price is competitive. Per-provider value metric breaks above 100 providers, so effective sold price falls to ~$95K — now -49% vs LegacyVendor. Helix is unintentionally the cheap option at the high end. |

## 3. Competitor Positioning Signals

How each competitor uses price as a signal.

**LegacyVendor — price-as-quality.** Highest list across all tiers. Their pricing page leads with "Trusted by 4,200 health systems." Price is part of the trust story. They discount in deals (estimated 12-18% based on win/loss intel) but never on the page. The high list price is the marketing.

**BookCal — price-as-access.** Lowest list. Pricing page leads with "Get started in minutes, no contract." Price is the on-ramp; the bet is land-and-expand. Their Enterprise tier is capped at $84K because their architecture cannot serve clinics above ~100 providers — they have made the structural limit a positioning choice.

**ScrubsScheduler — price-as-fit.** Mid-priced, vertical-native messaging. Pricing page leads with "Built for clinical operations from day one." Price signals serious tool for serious buyer. They discount narrowly (8-12% reported in win/loss) and refuse quarter-end concessions — that discipline is visible in the market.

**Helix — price-as-spreadsheet.** Pricing page shows three tiers with no narrative anchor. The 21% effective discount % broadcasts to the market that the list price is negotiable. Reps say "competitive" 41% of the time; the market hears "Helix discounts." This is the loudest signal Helix is sending right now.

## 4. Recommendation

Helix should **hold premium on Enterprise** (after fixing the value metric), **reposition Pro**, and **simplify Starter**.

**Enterprise**: keep list at $120K, add a $240K Enterprise+ tier as anchor (Section 4 of Deliverable 4), replace per-provider above 100 with a tiered provider-band structure. Once the architecture supports it, the list price is defensible against LegacyVendor at -35% and ScrubsScheduler at -17%.

**Pro**: hold list at $45K, kill the ad-hoc discount practice, introduce structured annual + multi-year discount tiers that buyers can actually plan against. The current 22% effective discount is destroying authority; a published 8-12% annual prepay discount preserves authority and converts the discount from a leak into a commitment lever.

**Starter**: increase list 8-10% to $13,000-$13,200. Renewal rate 91% and no price-sensitivity churn confirms WTP headroom. Use the increase to re-anchor BookCal as the discount option rather than Helix.

The strategic message Helix's pricing should send: **"We are the vertical-native premium under the legacy incumbent."** Today the pricing says: "We are negotiable." The architecture changes in Deliverable 4 are what make the strategic message true rather than aspirational.
