## Title: "Does your revenue story match your revenue mechanics?"

## Why this quiz exists

This quiz diagnoses how far a respondent's revenue narrative has drifted from how revenue actually works inside the business. Five questions map to the five load-bearing mechanics of the Petrichor Revenue Narrative Gap framework: forecast integrity, customer concentration, channel attribution truth, NRR evidence, and single-points-of-failure awareness. The composite score routes the respondent into one of four reconciliation tiers, and the tier determines what happens next: a manifesto read, the kit plus a five-day drip, the kit plus a Loom invite, or Phil personally reaching out inside 48 hours.

The quiz is a triage tool, not a content asset. Its only job is to put operators heading into a board reset, a raise, or a buyer diligence cycle into the right tier — fast enough that we stop wasting their time and ours.

## 5 Diagnostic Questions

1. **Forecast Truth — "How does your trailing 4-quarter growth compare to the forecast you presented to your board or investors?"**
   - They match (within 5%) — 0 pts (forecast integrity intact)
   - Within 20% — 1 pt (mild drift, defensible)
   - Off by 20–50% — 2 pts (material gap, diligence risk)
   - Off by 50% or more — 3 pts (forecast no longer load-bearing)

2. **Concentration — "What percentage of revenue comes from your single largest customer?"**
   - Under 5% — 0 pts (diversified)
   - 5–10% — 1 pt (manageable, still defensible)
   - 11–20% — 2 pts (single-point-of-failure forming)
   - 20% or more — 3 pts (will appear as a risk factor in any term sheet)

3. **Channel Attribution — "When you ask your sales leader how each channel performed last quarter, what do you hear back?"**
   - Hard numbers backed by source data — 0 pts (attribution is verifiable)
   - Rep-coded narrative with some data behind it — 1 pt (partial truth, partial story)
   - 'It's a mix — outbound, inbound, referral, all kind of working' — 2 pts (channel truth has collapsed)
   - 'I don't actually know — pipeline just shows up' — 3 pts (no defensible channel story)

4. **NRR Truth — "Pick the line that's truest about your current Net Revenue Retention."**
   - Trailing-quarter NRR backed by cohort data we publish internally — 0 pts (NRR is real)
   - Estimated NRR — no rigorous cohort breakdown — 1 pt (directionally correct, not defensible)
   - Bullish based on a few expanding accounts we point to in the deck — 2 pts (anecdote dressed as metric)
   - We have not measured NRR cleanly — 3 pts (NRR claim is hopeful, not evidenced)

5. **Single Points of Failure — "Could you name the three single-points-of-failure in your revenue right now — and the mitigation plan for each?"**
   - Yes, all three, with mitigation plans and named owners — 0 pts (resilient)
   - Yes, all three, but no mitigation plans yet — 1 pt (aware, exposed)
   - I can name one or two — 2 pts (partial map)
   - No — 3 pts (the gap is not yet visible to leadership)

## Scoring Rubric

### Tier 0 — Reconciled (0–5 points)

**Result page copy:**

Your revenue story and your revenue mechanics are doing the same job. The forecast tracks the trailing numbers. Concentration is inside the band a diligence team will accept without a risk-factor footnote. Your sales leader can defend channel attribution with data. NRR is measured against cohorts, not vibes. And you can name the failure points before someone else finds them in your data room.

You do not have a revenue-narrative problem right now. What you have is a maintenance problem. The Revenue Narrative Gap is not a one-time audit — it is a quarterly discipline. Story drifts from mechanics inside a single board cycle if nobody is watching. The companies that hold the line do it because someone runs the $1 Trace exercise every quarter, not because they got it right once.

Two actions:

1. Read the Resilience Stack manifesto. It explains why revenue narratives decay even when the business is healthy, and why the gap is invisible until it is expensive. Twenty minutes. No pitch at the end.
2. Bookmark the revenue-story-audit kit in the repo. Run the $1 Trace and the Board Deck Lie Detector as a 45-minute internal exercise every quarter. If two of the five signals in this quiz slip, that is your trigger to run the full audit.

You do not need a call. You need a calendar reminder.

### Tier 1 — Drifting (6–10 points)

**Result page copy:**

One or two of your signals have started slipping. The pattern is not yet visible in the boardroom — the deck still reads fine, the investors have not started asking pointed questions, the team has not raised a flag. But underneath, the trailing numbers and the forward narrative are no longer telling the same story. The forecast inherited an assumption nobody re-checked. One customer is creeping past 10% of revenue without a named mitigation. Your sales leader's channel breakdown is becoming a sentence instead of a spreadsheet.

This is what a Drifting revenue story looks like before it becomes a Series C diligence problem. The window to close it cheaply is short. Companies that catch it here usually need a focused 2.5-hour audit and a reconciled two-version artifact — not a re-platforming, not new tooling, not a CFO swap.

Two actions:

1. Download the revenue-story-audit kit. Run the full diagnostic against your last 4 quarters of revenue by customer, channel, product, geography, and cohort. Block 2.5 hours. Bring your last two board decks. Bring the CEO, the CFO, and the head of sales — the audit does not work without all three in the room.
2. Re-test the same five questions 30 days after you ship the reconciliation. If two scores improved, you caught it in time. If not, escalate.

You probably do not need a Petrichor engagement. You need a focused internal session with the right artifact at the end.

### Tier 2 — Inflated (11–15 points)

**Result page copy:**

Three or more of your signals are flashing. The forecast is materially off the trailing numbers. Concentration is past the line where diligence lawyers add a "key customer dependency" risk factor. Channel attribution is rep-coded narrative, not data. NRR is being defended with a few champion logos instead of cohort math. This is the stage where the deck still feels true to the people who wrote it and has stopped being defensible to anyone reading it cold.

At Tier 2, a kit alone is rarely enough. The audit will name the gap precisely — that is what it is built for. But the reconciliation usually requires uncomfortable conversations across leadership, and a self-serve kit cannot run those for you. The two-version reconciliation (Internal-Honest vs Externally-Defensible) is what makes the gap movable. Building it well requires a third party in the room.

Two actions:

1. Download the kit and run the diagnostic before any call. The scorecard tells you which of the nine segments are most decayed and what the composite Forecast Integrity Score is. Bring this to the conversation.
2. I am sending you a private Loom inside the welcome email. Six minutes. It walks through the NorthStack worked example — Tier 2, top-1 customer at 22%, forecast +120% claimed vs +47% actual. Watch it before you do anything else with this score.

The Loom is not a sales pitch. It is the same diagnostic vocabulary we will use if and when you book a call. If after watching it you think the kit alone is enough, run it yourself — that is the deal.

### Tier 3 — Critical (16–20 points)

**Result page copy:**

This is not drift and it is not inflation. The forecast has decoupled from the trailing data. A single customer is anchoring more than a fifth of revenue without a mitigation plan. Channel attribution is not measurable. NRR is anecdotal. Nobody in the building can name the three single-points-of-failure your revenue rides on. If you are anywhere near a raise, a board reset, or a strategic conversation in the next two quarters, the revenue story you are about to tell will not survive the room it is told in.

This is also the stage where the cost of waiting compounds the fastest. A "key customer dependency" risk factor on a term sheet costs one to two turns of valuation. An NRR claim that fails diligence kills the round entirely. The path out of Tier 3 is not a quiz, a kit, or a drip — it is a structured engagement that produces a reconciled two-version revenue narrative before the next external conversation.
