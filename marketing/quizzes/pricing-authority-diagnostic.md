## Title: "Do your prices signal authority — or just fit a spreadsheet?"

## Why this quiz exists

This quiz diagnoses whether a respondent's prices still signal authority in their category — or whether the pricing surface has decayed into a number that fits a spreadsheet and no longer carries the business. Five questions map to the five integrity points of the Petrichor Price-Value Integrity Map: discount trend, quarter-end approval pattern, NRR direction, value-metric defensibility, and price-increase readiness. The composite score routes the respondent into one of four authority tiers, and the tier determines what happens next: a manifesto read, the kit plus a five-day drip, the kit plus a Loom invite, or Phil personally reaching out inside 48 hours.

The quiz is a triage tool, not a content asset. Its only job is to put operators heading into a pricing reset, a renewal cycle, or a fundraise into the right tier — fast enough that we stop wasting their time and ours. Discounts tell the truth that decks do not. The quiz reads the bloodwork.

## 5 Diagnostic Questions

1. **Discount Trend — "How has your average discount % moved over the last 4 quarters?"**
   - Flat or down — 0 pts (authority intact)
   - Up 1–5% — 1 pt (mild erosion, defensible)
   - Up 6–15% — 2 pts (material drift, reps are negotiating against you)
   - Up 16% or more — 3 pts (authority broken, price has become a starting point)

2. **Quarter-End Pattern — "What percentage of last quarter's discounts were approved in the final 5 business days?"**
   - Under 10% — 0 pts (no calendar bias, discipline holds)
   - 10–25% — 1 pt (mild quarter-end pull, manageable)
   - 26–40% — 2 pts (manager approval has become a quarter-end ritual)
   - 41% or more — 3 pts (the discount is buying the quarter, not the deal)

3. **NRR Trend — "Where is your trailing-quarter Net Revenue Retention versus 4 quarters ago?"**
   - Up — 0 pts (expansion mechanics are working)
   - Flat — 1 pt (holding, but no expansion engine)
   - Down 5–10% — 2 pts (architectural failure forming, value metric or expansion path is broken)
   - Down 11% or more — 3 pts (pricing model is no longer load-bearing — board-flag territory)

4. **Value Metric Defensibility — "If a buyer asks 'why per X?' — can you defend it in 60 seconds without saying 'because spreadsheet'?"**
   - Yes, with a verifiable proof point the buyer can check — 0 pts (defensible)
   - Probably — we have a reason but it is internal, not buyer-facing — 1 pt (partial defense)
   - Not really — we have been winging it for a year — 2 pts (the metric is breaking under scrutiny)
   - No — the unit was chosen to match the comp set and we have never re-justified it — 3 pts (structural value-metric failure)

5. **Price-Increase Readiness — "Could you raise prices 10% next quarter without customer revolt?"**
   - Yes, confidently — across the board, with proof to cite — 0 pts (authority intact)
   - Maybe on Enterprise only — SMB and Mid-Market would push back — 1 pt (partial authority)
   - Probably not — the team would talk us out of it — 2 pts (authority eroded)
   - No — we have not raised in 24+ months and we are scared to test — 3 pts (authority lost; muscle has atrophied)

## Scoring Rubric

### Tier 0 — Holding (0–5 points)

**Result page copy:**

Your pricing surface is doing the job it was built to do. The discount curve is flat or trending down. Quarter-end is not buying your quarters for you. NRR is up or holding, which means expansion mechanics are working and the value metric the customer signed up for is still defensible at renewal. Your team would not flinch if you asked them to walk into next quarter with a 10% list-price lift on Enterprise.

You do not have a pricing-authority problem right now. What you have is a maintenance problem. The Price-Value Integrity Map is not a one-time audit — it is a quarterly discipline. Authority decays inside a single planning cycle if nobody is watching the discount curve and the manager-approval timestamps. The companies that hold the line do it because someone runs the Discount Autopsy every quarter, not because they got it right once.

Two actions:

1. Read the Resilience Stack manifesto. It explains why pricing surfaces decay even when the business is healthy, and why erosion is invisible until a board member asks why NRR slipped. Twenty minutes. No pitch at the end.
2. Bookmark the pricing-authority-diagnostic kit in the repo. Run the Discount Autopsy and the Price Increase Readiness Test as a 45-minute internal exercise every quarter. If two of the five signals in this quiz slip, that is your trigger to run the full diagnostic.

You do not need a call. You need a calendar reminder.

### Tier 1 — Eroding (6–10 points)

**Result page copy:**

One or two of your integrity points have started slipping. The pattern is not yet visible to your board — NRR is flat, not yet falling, and the discount creep has been absorbed into the "buyers are tighter this year" story your sales leader tells. But underneath, the price your reps quote and the price your customers pay have started to decouple. Quarter-end approvals are doing more work than your value proposition is. The list price has begun to function as a starting point in negotiation rather than a signal of position.

This is what an Eroding pricing authority looks like before NRR collapses below 100% and before a board member opens the discount report. The window to close it cheaply is short. Companies that catch it here usually need a focused 2.5-hour diagnostic and a structural recoding of discount reasons — not a pricing-page redesign, not new tooling, not a packaging overhaul.

Two actions:

1. Download the pricing-authority-diagnostic kit. Run the full Discount Autopsy against your last 4 quarters of closed-won deals — list price, sold price, discount %, recoded reason. Block 2.5 hours. Bring the CEO, the CFO, and the head of sales — the diagnostic does not work without all three in the room. Pull the 10 largest discounts last quarter and recode the reason from rep-claim to true root cause.
2. Re-test the same five questions 30 days after you ship the discount discipline fixes. If two scores improved, you caught it in time. If not, escalate.

You probably do not need a Petrichor engagement. You need a focused internal session with the right artifact at the end.

### Tier 2 — Eroding (advanced) (11–15 points)

**Result page copy:**

Three or four of your integrity points are flashing. Discount % has lifted into double-digit creep, quarter-end is doing real work, and NRR has started moving in the wrong direction. The value metric the company chose two or three years ago has begun to break at scale — buyers above a certain size are asking why-per-X and the team does not have a defense that holds for 60 seconds. The price-increase readiness answer was honest, and it was "maybe on Enterprise only" or worse. This is the stage where the pricing surface still feels right to the people who wrote it and has stopped being defensible to the people quoting it.

At Tier 2, a kit alone is rarely enough. The diagnostic will name the gap precisely — that is what it is built for. But the structural pricing fix usually requires uncomfortable conversations across leadership about value metric, expansion mechanics, and the discount-approval ladder, and a self-serve kit cannot run those for you. The Pricing Architecture Recommendations deliverable is what makes the gap movable. Building it well requires a third party in the room.

Two actions:

The Loom is not a sales pitch. It is the same diagnostic vocabulary we will use if and when you book a call. If after watching it you think the kit alone is enough, run it yourself — that is the deal.

### Tier 3 — Lost (urgent) (16–20 points)

**Result page copy:**

This is not erosion. The pricing surface has decoupled from anything that resembles authority. Discounts are up 16% or more year over year. Manager approval has become a quarter-end ritual that closes 41% or more of the quarter's discount volume in the last week. NRR is down double digits, expansion is well below 25%, and you have not raised prices in 24 months because the team would talk you out of trying. The value metric the company picked when it was small has structurally failed at the size you are now. The number on the website is whatever a rep can hold in the room — usually not much.

This is also the stage where the cost of waiting compounds the fastest. NRR below 100 with worsening discount discipline is the configuration that triggers board flags, ends careers in the CFO chair, and forces panic-mode pricing resets that produce customer churn spikes six months later. The path out of Tier 3 is not a quiz, a kit, or a drip — it is a structured engagement that produces a reconciled pricing architecture and a 90-day rollout plan before the next board cycle.
