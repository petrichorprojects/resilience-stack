## Title: "Is your positioning still relevant — or solving yesterday's problem?"

## Why this quiz exists

This quiz diagnoses how far a respondent's positioning has decayed against the market it is supposed to be selling into. Five questions map to the five measurable signals from the Petrichor Relevancy Decay framework: win rate trend, sales cycle length, buyer language match, competitor surprise frequency, and category share of voice. The composite score routes the respondent into one of four decay stages, and the stage determines the follow-up: nothing, the kit, the kit plus a call, or direct outreach within 48 hours.

The quiz is a triage tool, not a content asset. Its only job is to put strategy operators into the right tier so we stop wasting their time and ours.

## 5 Diagnostic Questions

1. **Win Rate — "How has your close-win rate trended over the last 2 quarters?"**
   - Improving — 0 pts (healthy baseline)
   - Flat — 1 pt (mild drift)
   - Down 3–15% — 2 pts (clear disconnect)
   - Down 16% or more — 3 pts (full displacement)

2. **Sales Cycle — "How has your average sales cycle changed over the last 2 quarters?"**
   - Shorter — 0 pts
   - Same — 1 pt
   - Longer by 1–20% — 2 pts
   - Longer by 21% or more — 3 pts

3. **Buyer Language — "Pick the line that sounds most like a current buyer describing your product in their own words."**
   - "They use our positioning language word-for-word — same category name, same value phrasing." — 0 pts
   - "They describe us close to our positioning, with one or two of their own words." — 1 pt
   - "They describe a tool we recognise but use language we don't use internally." — 2 pts
   - "They describe something we'd struggle to identify as our product — language we have never heard." — 3 pts

4. **Competitor Surprise — "How often in the last quarter did a lost deal mention a competitor your sales team did not know existed?"**
   - Never — 0 pts
   - Once — 1 pt
   - 2–3 times — 2 pts
   - 4 or more times — 3 pts

5. **Share of Voice — "Where is your category share of voice over the last 18 months?"**
   - Up — 0 pts
   - Flat — 1 pt
   - Down 1–25% — 2 pts
   - Down 26% or more — 3 pts

## Scoring Rubric

### Stage 0 — Stable (0–5 points)

**Result page copy:**

Your positioning is still doing its job. Win rate is holding, the sales cycle has not lengthened in any meaningful way, and buyers are still describing your product in language close to your own. You do not have a relevancy problem right now.

What you have is a maintenance problem. Frameworks that work today decay quietly. The signals you scored on are early indicators — once two of them flip negative inside a single quarter, you are usually 6–12 months into Stage 1 Drift before anyone notices.

Two actions:

1. Read the Resilience Stack manifesto. It explains why frameworks decay even when nothing visible has changed. Twenty minutes. No pitch at the end.
2. Bookmark the relevancy-audit kit in the repo. Run a 30-minute internal version of the diagnostic every quarter against the same five signals you just answered. If two of them slip, that is your trigger to run the full audit.

You do not need a call. You need a calendar reminder.

### Stage 1 — Drift (6–10 points)

**Result page copy:**

One or two of your signals have started slipping. The pattern is not yet visible in the boardroom — revenue still works, the analyst report has not changed tone, the team has not raised a flag. But underneath, the market has begun describing the problem in language that does not match yours. Win rate is wobbling. Buyers are paraphrasing where they used to quote you. This is what Stage 1 Drift looks like before it becomes obvious.

The window to fix Drift cheaply is short. Companies that catch it here usually need a positioning refresh and a competitive intel update — a 2–4 hour internal exercise, not a re-platforming.

Two actions:

1. Download the relevancy-audit kit. Run the full diagnostic on your own business against the five signals plus the four it adds (buyer survey language match, analyst language delta, NPS verbatim review, competitor narrative pull). Block 3 hours. Bring win/loss data and your last customer survey.
2. Re-test the same five questions 30 days after you ship the refresh. If two scores improved, you caught it in time. If not, escalate.

You probably do not need a Petrichor engagement. You need a focused internal sprint with the right artifact at the end.

### Stage 2 — Disconnect (11–15 points)

**Result page copy:**

Three or more of your signals are flashing. Win rate is materially down, the sales cycle has stretched, and you are losing deals to a competitor your team did not have on the board. This is the stage where the framework still feels true to you but has stopped describing the market your buyers actually live in. The gap is no longer drift — it is a disconnect.

At Stage 2, a kit alone is rarely enough. The diagnostic will name the problem precisely, but the fix usually requires a positioning rewrite that touches the website, the sales deck, the analyst briefing, and the inbound content engine. That is a cross-functional decision, and a self-serve kit does not make cross-functional decisions for you.

Two actions:

1. Download the kit and run the diagnostic. Even before any call, the scorecard tells you exactly which of the nine segments are most decayed. Bring this to the conversation.
2. Book a 30-minute call with Phil at Petrichor. The call confirms whether the disconnect is at the positioning layer (cheap to fix), the category layer (medium), or the product layer (expensive). The diagnosis determines the engagement, not the other way around.

The call is not a sales call. It is a triage call. If the answer is "you can fix this internally," that is what you will hear.

### Stage 3 — Displacement (16–20 points)

**Result page copy:**

This is not drift and it is not disconnect. Win rate is in freefall, the sales cycle has stretched past the point where buyers are still actively comparing, your team is losing to competitors it cannot name, and share of voice has collapsed. Your positioning has stopped describing the market. Buyers have moved to a different conversation and you are still selling into the old one.

Stage 3 is not solvable by a quiz, a kit, or a 30-minute call. It is solvable, but the work is a 4–8 week engagement that re-anchors the company around what the market is now buying — not what it bought when you achieved PMF. The longer Stage 3 runs untreated, the more of the fix lives in the product roadmap rather than the marketing layer, which is the expensive version.

One action:

1. Book a call with Phil at Petrichor. The calendar link is in the welcome email. We will respond within 48 hours regardless of whether you book.

You do not need to download the kit first. You can — it gives you the same diagnostic vocabulary we will use on the call — but the kit is not the path out of Stage 3. The path out is a structured engagement, and the only question worth answering on the call is whether Petrichor is the right team to run it.

If we are not, the call ends with the names of two people who are. That is the deal.
