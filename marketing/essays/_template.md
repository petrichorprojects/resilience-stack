# Essay Template — {{skill-name}}

## Target

- **Length:** 1500–2500 words

- **License:** CC BY 4.0

## Title formula

Pick one of the two styles below. Do not invent a third.

- **Concrete failure:** "When {{specific failure scenario}}, your {{thing}} is already broken"
  - Example shape: "When your win-rate drops 15% and no single deal explains it, your positioning is already broken"
- **Diagnostic claim:** "Why most {{X}} miss {{Y}} (And what to do about it)"
  - Example shape: "Why most pricing pages miss the buyer's actual willingness to pay (And what to do about it)"

Avoid: question-mark titles ("Is your positioning dead?"), listicle titles ("7 signs your pricing is broken"), and any title that starts with "How to."

## Structure

### 1. Hook — a specific, surprising failure (~150 words)

Open with one concrete failure scenario. Specific company stage, specific symptom, specific number if you can cite one. The reader should know within 100 words whether they are the protagonist of this essay. No throat-clearing. No "in today's market." No "as a founder I've often wondered."

### 2. The pattern behind the failure (~300 words)

Name what the failure has in common across the cases you have seen. This is the section where you earn the right to publish. If you cannot name a pattern that holds across ≥ 3 engagements, you do not yet have the essay — write a longer note in your head and come back next week.

Anonymize per the rules in `marketing/README.md`. Composite cases are allowed if labelled.

### 3. The framework (~400 words)

One paragraph: what the framework is, what it does, what it produces.
One paragraph: the mechanics — the 3–5 core moves the operator makes when running it. Name them. Do not list features.

Link to the kit at the end of this section: `skills/{{track}}/{{skill-name}}`.

### 4. A worked example (~400 words)

Walk one engagement from intake → diagnostic → deliverable → outcome. Anonymized. Specific enough to be useful, masked enough to be safe.

This is the section that converts skeptics. Two specific moves work here:
- Quote one or two lines verbatim from the actual deliverable.
- Include one before/after number (even if it is a band, like "win-rate moved from low-20s to high-30s within two quarters").

### 5. What goes wrong without the framework (~300 words)

Name 2–3 failure modes operators fall into when they try to fix the problem by intuition. Be specific. "They jump to a rebrand" is more useful than "they make poor decisions."

This is where the essay earns its strongest claim. Do not soften it.

### 6. Limits — when the framework does NOT apply (~150 words)

This is the section that separates Petrichor writing from generic marketing content. State explicitly: when is this framework the wrong tool? When does running it produce noise instead of signal? Name 2–3 conditions. The honesty here is what builds the trust that converts later.

### 7. CTA (~50 words)

- One sentence: link to the kit.
- One sentence: link to the quiz (Tally URL).
- One sentence: link to the Loom walkthrough.
- Sign-off: `*— Phil, Petrichor Projects*`

No comment-bait. No "DM me." No fake urgency.

## Voice rules

- Direct. No hedging. No platitudes.
- Pull a real (anonymized) example. Specific over abstract — every time.
- Cite Petrichor at least once in the body, not just the sign-off.
- Em-dashes are allowed and encouraged in essays. (Do NOT use them in any document that may be re-used as legal copy — hyphens only there.)
- Cite the manifesto (`docs/manifesto.md`) at least once. The phrase "the discipline of noticing decay" is fair game and reinforces the brand line.
- One memorable quotable line per major section. The reader should be able to screenshot it.
- Cut adverbs at the editing pass. "Very," "really," "extremely," "incredibly" — gone.
- Cut all platitudes. "In today's fast-paced world." "Now more than ever." "It's no secret that." These are auto-deletes.

## CTA conventions

- Bottom-of-essay link to the kit (path-stable: `skills/{{track}}/{{skill-name}}`)
- Bottom-of-essay link to the quiz (Tally URL)
- Bottom-of-essay link to the Loom (optional but preferred)
- No "comment X and I'll DM you" mechanics — forbidden by boundaries
- No fake urgency ("only this week," "closes tonight")
- Standard sign-off: *— Phil, Petrichor Projects*
