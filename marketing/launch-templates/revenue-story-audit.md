## LinkedIn long-form (1500 words)

Your board deck says +120% Y/Y. Your trailing four quarters say +47%. Both can be true on paper. Only one survives diligence.

This is the gap that kills Series C rounds. Not in the meeting where the deck is presented. Six weeks later, in the data room, when a junior associate at the lead fund runs a cohort analysis and finds that the stated NRR of 115 is actually 96. That the top customer is 22% of revenue. That the partner referral channel the deck claims as a growth lever has gone silent for 90 days. The associate writes a memo. The memo costs one to two turns of valuation. Sometimes it costs the round.

I have run versions of this conversation more than twenty times across Petrichor pre-raise engagements. The cast changes — Series B fintech, growth-stage vertical SaaS, marketplace approaching IPO — but the shape is identical. A revenue narrative is written at a specific moment. A board deck, an investor update, a sales pitch. The narrative is calibrated to real trailing numbers, real channel mix, real cohort behavior. It works. The company grows on top of it.

Twelve months in, the team has rebuilt the deck three times but never re-derived the underlying assumptions. Outbound, which was 30% of pipeline at the time the narrative was written, is now claimed at 60% because the sales leader's gut says it is, and the dashboards are not granular enough to contradict the gut. Eighteen months in, the forecast on the board deck has been carried forward as a target, then defended as a baseline, then quoted as a fact. Twenty-four months in, the NRR number on slide twelve is whatever number "feels right" given the customers leadership has been talking about most often in the all-hands.

Nothing in the system fires an alarm. Revenue-narrative decay does not crash. The CFO's model still opens in Excel. The CRM still runs. The numbers still get reported in the board meeting. And nobody in the building has put the trailing data and the forward narrative side by side on the same page in nine months.

**The Revenue Narrative Gap — story vs mechanics**

The Revenue Story Audit skill in our open-source repo is built on a single load-bearing concept: the Revenue Narrative Gap. Distance between the story you tell — the deck, the investor update, the sales pitch — and the mechanics of how revenue actually works inside the business. The gap is measurable. It closes through a reconciled two-version artifact: the Internal-Honest version (full mechanics, including the parts that are uncomfortable) and the Externally-Defensible version (everything in the first version that survives diligence). The two versions are not aspirational and operational. They are honest and survivable.

The audit runs nine segments. Three are load-bearing.

**Move 1 — The $1 Trace.** Pick a single dollar from last quarter. Trace it from origin to recognition. Document every assumption along the way. Most narratives die here. The dollar you trace is "outbound" on the channel sheet and turns out to be a partner-sourced renewal where the partner stopped returning emails three months ago. One dollar surfaces a dead referral channel that the deck still claims as active.

**Move 2 — The Board Deck Lie Detector.** Line by line, classify each revenue claim in the deck as verified, extrapolated, hopeful, or wrong. No claim survives without a traceable dollar. Forecasts based on "the team is bullish" are downgraded to hopeful until evidence is supplied. The output is a marked-up deck where every line carries one of four labels.

**Move 3 — The Uncomfortable Metric Anonymous Reveal.** Each member of leadership writes, anonymously, the one metric they hope nobody asks about in the next investor conversation. The reveal is anonymous because the answer matters more than who said it. In three of every four NorthStack-shape engagements I run, three of four leaders write the same metric. That metric is the diagnosis.

**Sample deliverable — what the scorecard says verbatim**

A line lifted directly from the latest audit deliverable: *"Tier 2 — Inflated, borderline Tier 3 Critical. Composite Forecast Integrity Score 22/30. Top-1 customer concentration: 22%. Claimed NRR 115; cohort-derived NRR 96. Claimed forecast +120% Y/Y; trailing-4Q actual +47%."*

The scorecard is not a narrative. It is a diagnostic with a tier assignment, a numerical score, five measured mechanics, and three named reconciliation actions with owners and deadlines.

**A worked case — NorthStack, fintech ops layer, $20M ARR**

Anonymized composite. Fintech operations layer for mid-market neobanks. $20M ARR. Series B closed 18 months ago, prepping Series C in 90 days. In the room: Founder/CEO, CFO, Head of Sales, Head of Product. Pre-work: four quarters of revenue by customer, channel, product, geography, and cohort. Two most recent board decks. Current sales pitch. Top 5 expansion deals and top 5 churn losses with reasons. Engagement time: 2.5 hours.

The audit classified NorthStack at Tier 2 — Inflated, borderline Tier 3 Critical. Four findings were load-bearing.

The forecast was extrapolated, not derived. The deck claimed +120% Y/Y, anchored on one strong quarter. Trailing four quarters delivered +47%. The Lie Detector classified the claim as hopeful, not verified.

The top customer was 22% of revenue. Top ten customers were 64%. The deck described the customer base as "diversified across mid-market neobanks." Without a mitigation plan, this would appear as a key-customer-dependency risk factor in any term sheet.

Outbound was claimed at 60%, actual was 40%. When channel attribution was recoded against source data — actual call logs, inbound form submissions, partner-sourced opportunities — the other 20% the deck called "outbound" was partner referral and inbound miscoded by reps.

NRR claimed bullish at 115, actual cohort math was 96. Three champion accounts had expanded sharply. The deck pointed at them. The cohort told a different story. The Uncomfortable Metric reveal: three of four leadership members wrote "NRR" as the metric they hoped no one asked about.

The two-version reconciliation closed the gap. The Externally-Defensible version went into the deck with the forecast revised to a defensible +65%, the concentration risk surfaced with a named 12-month mitigation plan, the channel mix recoded against source data, and NRR re-stated against cohort math with a quarter-over-quarter trend line.

Series C closed at the target valuation. The associate's diligence memo flagged concentration and noted that the company had identified and mitigated it pre-diligence. The narrative that survived the room was the one that had already survived the audit.

**What goes wrong without this framework**

Three failure modes I have watched companies fall into when they sense something is off in the deck but never run the reconciliation.

One — they paper over the gap. The CFO knows. The CEO knows. Neither raises it because the deck is "locked" and changing it now would signal weakness to the board. The deck ships externally. Six weeks later the associate's memo arrives. Valuation drops. The cost of not raising it internally is paid externally by everyone in the cap table.

Two — they tighten the story without re-deriving the mechanics. A new VP Finance or fractional CFO is brought in to "clean up the metrics." The deck gets new charts. The same hopeful numbers get prettier visualizations. The $1 Trace is never run. The Lie Detector is never applied. The decay is re-skinned, not addressed.

Three — they raise on the inflated narrative and pay later. The round closes. Twelve months in, trailing data still does not match the forecast that closed the round. The next board meeting becomes a postmortem. The CEO spends two quarters defending instead of building.

**Why most CFOs miss this**

The CFO function is structurally biased against running this audit. The CFO owns the model. The model produced the deck. Running an audit that reclassifies the deck's claims as hopeful is, structurally, the CFO auditing the CFO. The work has to come from outside the function — either a board member willing to push, an investor doing pre-diligence on themselves, or an external facilitator whose only deliverable is the gap and the reconciliation. Self-audit is the version that does not work.

The Resilience Stack is the open-source operationalization of the opposite assumption. Every revenue narrative ships presumed to be drifting from the day it is delivered. The deliverable is not the deck. The deliverable is the discipline of running the $1 Trace and the Lie Detector on a quarterly cadence, before the gap becomes a diligence memo.

**When the framework does NOT apply**

Pre-revenue companies — no story-vs-mechanics gap yet. Without 4-quarter segmented revenue data — the $1 Trace cannot be run honestly; build the data layer first. Public companies bound by Reg FD — reconciliation work still gets done, but it is run by securities counsel, not by an open-source audit framework.
