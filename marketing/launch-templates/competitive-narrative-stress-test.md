## LinkedIn long-form (1500 words)

Three board members at a $14M ARR retail analytics company asked the same question, in the same Q1 board meeting, without coordinating: why aren't we agentic?

The CEO's instinct, the next morning, was to rewrite the narrative. The CFO's pushback, on a separate call thirty minutes later, was do not chase the language, fight on outcomes. Both were wrong. The narrative was not the problem. The narrative was the symptom. The problem was that nobody on the team had read the competitor's full pitch deck in 90 days, and nobody had stress-tested the company's own story against the most obvious counter-narrative the competitor could deploy with unlimited budget.

The win rate had already dropped from 52% in Q3 to 31% in Q1. Four of seven enterprise deals lost to a single new entrant — up from one of seven the prior year. Gartner had reframed the category in March to "Agentic Retail Intelligence." None of these signals had been ignored. They had been mis-attributed. Sales leadership was logging the losses as "lost on price" and asking for discounting authority. The CEO was defending the deck as "still right, just needs sharper delivery." The CMO was scoping a rebrand. Three different fixes for one underlying problem: the competitive narrative had decayed, and the room could not see where it was leaking.

I have run versions of this conversation more than fifteen times in the last two years. The cast changes — vertical SaaS facing a category re-framer, infrastructure player getting reclassified by Gartner, application company watching an AI-native entrant absorb their differentiation — but the shape is identical. A competitive narrative is written at a specific moment, against a specific set of competitors, in a specific buyer vocabulary. It works. The company wins. Twelve to eighteen months later, the same narrative is still being delivered against a competitive set that has fully turned over, in a buyer vocabulary that has migrated.

Three structural drifts produce the failure. Narratives drift from buyer language — buyers stop saying "operating system" and start saying "data layer for agents," and the deck does not update. Sales-team sanitization — lost-deal reports get optimized for sales-leader comfort instead of buyer truth. Future claims that do not survive "okay, but how" — every competitive narrative has a future-state claim, and most collapse under one follow-up question because nobody has rehearsed the answer.

**The Narrative Attack Surface — 5 layers, composite Durability score**

The Competitive Narrative Stress Test skill in our open-source repo is built on a single load-bearing concept: the Narrative Attack Surface. Every competitive story exposes five surfaces — Origin Claim, Differentiation Claim, Proof Claim, Outcome Claim, Future Claim. Each surface scores 1-5 for attack resistance. The composite Durability Score lands between 5 (a narrative that cannot survive the first follow-up question) and 25 (a narrative that holds under hostile delivery). The audit closes the gap through three pressure mechanisms run in sequence.

**Move 1 — The Blank Check Test.** If your strongest competitor had unlimited budget and the best agency in the world, what would they say about you? Write the attack ad. Three lines. Most differentiation claims die here, in a meeting you control, before they die in a meeting you don't. Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers framework names the structural assets that make competition unwinnable — Counter-Positioning, Cornered Resource, Process Power — but most companies run narratives assuming powers they have not actually built. The Blank Check Test forces the team to face the attack ad before a competitor writes it for them.

**Move 2 — Competitor's Best Day Simulation.** The facilitator delivers the competitor's strongest 60-second pitch — against you, in their voice, using their verbatim language pulled from their decks and webinars. You defend in real time. Both versions are recorded. April Dunford has written about positioning as the work of choosing the context buyers use to evaluate you. The Competitor's Best Day forces the team to hear the context the competitor is already building in the buyer's head, in the competitor's words, before the buyer brings it to a sales call.

**Move 3 — 60-Second Delivery Test.** Reconstructed narrative delivered orally, on a timer, no notes, against hostile follow-up. If a layer cannot survive 60 seconds with one piece of evidence, it is a crack. The skill enforces a rule: composite Durability must improve at least 30% post-rebuild, or the reconstruction is rejected and the team redoes it. Vibes rebuilds do not survive the rule.

**Sample deliverable — what the scorecard says verbatim**

A line lifted directly from the latest stress-test deliverable: *"Pre-rebuild Attack Surface Durability: 13/25. Fragile. Layer 5 (Future Claim) scored 2/5 — 'retail operating system' reads as defensive against NewEntrant's 'data layer agents need.' Post-rebuild: 19/25. Holding. +6 point lift exceeds the 30% rebuild threshold."*

The scorecard is not a narrative. It is a diagnostic with a tier assignment, a numerical score across five attack surfaces, a before/after Durability lift, and three named reconstruction actions with owners and deadlines.

**A worked case — Tessera, retail analytics, $14M ARR**

Anonymized composite. Retail analytics SaaS for mid-market omnichannel retailers. $14M ARR. Series B closed 18 months ago, prepping the next round in 6-8 months. Win rate against NewEntrant fell from 52% to 31% over two quarters. Gartner reframed the category in March. Three board members independently asked "why aren't we agentic?" in a single meeting.

In the room: Founder/CEO, Head of Product, Head of Sales, CMO. Pre-work: Tessera's verbatim current narrative, NewEntrant's full pitch deck pulled from their website and a recorded analyst webinar, five lost-deal post-mortems with buyer language quoted verbatim, and Gartner's March reframe note. Engagement time: 2.5 hours.

The Attack Surface scored 13/25. Fragile. Layer 2 (Differentiation) — 2/5, the "deepest integrations" claim was no longer differentiating because NewEntrant had matched the count. Layer 4 (Outcome) — 2/5, and this was the mid-session discovery. The strongest customer-outcome claim Tessera was using — a $12M savings number from a single anchor customer — had no published methodology. Internally the methodology was sketched on a Notion page the CMO had not opened in 8 months. The number had become folklore inside Tessera.

Layer 5 (Future Claim) — 2/5. "Tessera is the retail operating system for omnichannel commerce." Read cold against NewEntrant's verbatim deck, the line was now defensive. The reframe the room agreed to ship: "Tessera is the retail data layer that agents need to act — without it, every agentic retail tool is hallucinating against stale inventory." That reframe accepted the agentic frame the buyer was already in, positioned Tessera upstream of NewEntrant rather than in competition with it, and surfaced a real failure mode of NewEntrant — agents on stale data — that Tessera could prove with telemetry.

Post-rebuild Durability: 19/25. Holding. +6 lift, well above the 30% threshold. Six weeks after the rebuild shipped, win rate against NewEntrant started reversing. By Series B kickoff conversations, head-to-head was back above 40%. The three board members got their answer in a frame the CEO could defend cold for 60 seconds under hostile questioning.

**What goes wrong without this framework**

Three failure modes I have watched companies fall into when win rate slips and the team mis-diagnoses the cause.

One — they blame price. Lost-deal reports come back logged as "lost on price." Discounting authority expands. Margin compresses. The actual cause — a counter-narrative the rep could not defend — never surfaces. Discounting is the cost of a narrative loss that has been mis-diagnosed as a pricing loss.

Two — they rebrand without rebuilding the narrative. A new agency. New visuals. New hero line. The same hopeful claims get prettier typography. The Attack Surface is never mapped. The Blank Check Test is never run. Twelve months later the same competitor is winning the same deals against a better-looking deck.

Three — they let the Series B story become "incumbent losing to agentic upstart." This is the failure mode that ends companies. Once Gartner has reframed the category and slotted you on the wrong side, the cost of the rebuild stops being a 2.5-hour facilitation and becomes an 18-month brand-rebuild plus an analyst-relations campaign.

**Why most CMOs miss this**

The CMO function is structurally biased against running this audit. The CMO owns the narrative. The narrative produced the deck. Running a stress test that scores the deck's claims as Fragile is, structurally, the CMO auditing the CMO. The work has to come from outside the function — either a board member willing to push, a head of sales willing to surface candid lost-deal reasons, or an external facilitator whose only deliverable is the Attack Surface and the reconstruction. Self-audit is the version that does not work. Most rebrand cycles fail for exactly this reason. The agency redoes the visuals because that is what the CMO can scope without auditing the strategy underneath. The Resilience Stack is the open-source operationalization of the opposite assumption: every competitive narrative ships presumed to be drifting from the day it is delivered. The deliverable is not the deck. The deliverable is the discipline of running the Blank Check Test and the Competitor's Best Day Simulation on a quarterly cadence, before the gap becomes a board confrontation.

**When the framework does NOT apply**

Pre-PMF companies with no real competitors — use `customer-truth-extraction` instead. True blue-ocean categories — use `category-creation-pressure-test`. Teams that cannot collect competitor verbatim — paraphrased positioning is not enough; the skill is evidence-required. Recent positioning rewrites under 30 days — give the new narrative time to gather signal first.
