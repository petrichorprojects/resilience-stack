## LinkedIn long-form (1400 words)

Your win rate dropped 14 points in two quarters. No single deal explains it. Your product hasn't regressed. Your pricing is the same. Your team hasn't shrunk. Here is what is actually broken.

I have run versions of this conversation more than thirty times across Petrichor engagements. The cast changes — Series B SaaS, growth-stage marketplace, vertical fintech, services firm — but the shape is identical. A founding team writes a positioning artifact at a specific moment. A deck, a one-liner, a category narrative, a website hero. The artifact is calibrated to a real moment with real buyers, real competitors, real buyer-language. It works. The company grows on top of it.

Twelve months in, the team has internalized the artifact so completely that nobody re-reads it. Eighteen months in, three of the five "main competitors" the artifact named no longer matter and three new ones have appeared. Twenty-four months in, the verbatim words buyers use to describe the problem have drifted by 5-8 terms out of a vocabulary of roughly 12. The artifact is being defended internally and overridden externally — in the same building, on the same Monday.

Nothing in the system fires an alarm. Decay does not crash. The deck still opens in PowerPoint. The website still loads. The sales team still pitches it, most of the time. And the win rate moves on a slope shallow enough that no single deal explains it.

**The Relevancy Decay Model — three stages, measurable thresholds**

The Relevancy Audit skill in our open-source repo is built on a three-stage decay model: Drift → Disconnect → Displacement. Each stage has signals you can read off four quarters of pipeline data, win/loss reviews, and one round of buyer interviews. The diagnostic is not a workshop. It produces a stage assignment with evidence.

**Stage 1 — Drift.** The framework still mostly works, but the world has moved 5-15% off the assumptions baked into it. Win rate down 10-20% year-over-year with no single deal explaining it. 2-4 of the top 12 buyer-verbatim terms missing from your positioning. The competitive set in your internal battlecards differs from the competitive set named in 50%+ of recent lost-deal reviews. This stage is the most dangerous because it does not feel like a problem.

**Stage 2 — Disconnect.** 5-8 of the top 12 buyer-verbatim terms are missing. Sales is winning deals "off-script" — the wins no longer match the playbook. New hires read the strategy doc and quietly ignore it within 60 days. Each individual loss is explainable as a "weird deal." In aggregate they are a structural signal that the model has stopped being load-bearing.

**Stage 3 — Displacement.** 9+ of the top 12 buyer-verbatim terms are missing. Buyers refuse the category noun you use and substitute one of their own. Analysts make you "translate" before they understand you. Terminal if untreated. The companies that fail to refresh in time do not lose to a better product. They lose to a better description of the same problem.

**Sample deliverable — what the scorecard says verbatim**

A line lifted directly from the latest audit deliverable: *"Stage 2 — Disconnect, borderline Stage 3. Composite Relevancy Decay Score 4.2 / 10 (FRAGILE). Of the top 12 buyer-verbatim terms, zero appear in the current positioning statement."*

The scorecard is not a narrative. It is a diagnostic with a stage assignment, a numerical score, six measured signals, and three named refresh actions with owners and deadlines.

**A worked case — vertical SaaS, legal ops, $30M ARR**

Anonymized composite. Mid-market vertical SaaS, $30M ARR, legal operations. Founded 2018, hit PMF 2021, plateaued 2024. Win rate 38% → 24% over two quarters. Sales cycle 47 days → 62. Marketing spend doubled. Share of voice flat for 18 months. Three lost-deal reviews in one quarter named a competitor the sales team had never heard of.

In the room: Founder/CEO, VP Marketing, Head of Sales, Head of Product. Pre-work: four quarters of coded win/loss data, 18 customer interviews with verbatim NPS comments, three competitor positioning statements, the Q4 2025 analyst quadrant that had quietly moved them from "Leader" to "Established Player." Engagement time: 2.5 hours.

The audit classified them Stage 2 — Disconnect, borderline Stage 3. The single most load-bearing insight: *zero* of the top 12 buyer-verbatim terms from the survey appeared in the company's positioning statement. The statement read "the leading legal operations platform for modern firms." Buyers in interviews were saying "AI workflow," "matter intake automation," "compliance defensibility," "associate leverage." None of those nouns were on the website. None were in the sales deck.

Day 30: positioning rewrite using buyer-verbatim language. 6 of the top 12 terms now present in the deck and hero. Net-new pipeline +18% month-over-month. Day 60: sales playbook now references the new entrant explicitly. Win/loss data recoded retroactively for 12 months. Loss rate to that competitor cut from 60% to 35%. Day 90: re-score moved from Stage 2 back into mid-Stage 1. The diagnostic is on a quarterly cadence now.

**What goes wrong without this framework**

Three failure modes I have watched companies fall into when they notice the win-rate slide but never diagnose the stage:

One — they hire a new VP Marketing and call it "rebrand season." New colors, new typography, new website, same underlying positioning. The verbatim language buyers actually use never enters the artifact. Six months and roughly $200K later the win rate has continued sliding because the diagnosis was never made.

Two — they spend their way out of it. Marketing budget doubles. Paid acquisition triples. The funnel is louder; conversion does not move; CAC payback stretches. Spend amplifies whatever message is in the system. If the message has decayed, more spend is just a faster way to lose money.

Three — they wait for the next funding round to "re-do strategy." This is the most expensive choice and the most common one. The unstated assumption is that strategy work is a once-per-round activity. Three of every five companies I diagnose at Stage 2 are operating on a strategy doc written at the previous funding round — 18 to 24 months stale by definition.

**Why most consultants miss this**

The strategy industry is structurally biased against catching decay. A consultant who tells you your framework expires in 18 months sells one engagement. A consultant who sells the framework as "your strategic foundation for the next decade" sells the same engagement at 4x and gets re-hired to defend it instead of replace it. The longer a framework can be sold as timeless, the longer the keeper of its interpretation gets paid. This is the gravity of how the work has been priced and sold for forty years. It is not malicious. It is the incentive.

The Resilience Stack is the open-source operationalization of the opposite assumption. Every framework ships presumed to be decaying from the day it is delivered. The deliverable is not the artifact. The deliverable is the discipline of replacing the artifact on schedule.

**When the framework does NOT apply**

Pre-PMF — you have no positioning to decay yet, you have a hypothesis. The right skill is `customer-truth-extraction`, not `relevancy-audit`. Single-customer enterprise — if 60%+ of revenue is one buyer, the frame is account strategy, not positioning. True category creation — if the category genuinely does not exist yet, the verbatim-language test returns false negatives. Run the audit 12 months in, not at launch.

The honesty matters. A framework that pretends to apply universally is one you cannot trust on the cases where it does apply.
