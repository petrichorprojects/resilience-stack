## LinkedIn long-form (1500 words)

A CFO I work with pulled the manager-approval timestamps on his discount report last quarter. He had been hearing the same story in three consecutive monthly reviews — buyers are tighter this year, competitive pressure has stepped up, the market has reset. The discount curve had climbed from 8% to 21% in twelve months. He did not buy the story the first two times. He did not buy it the third. So he pulled the timestamps.

Thirty-eight percent of last quarter's discount volume was approved between 5pm and midnight on the final three business days of the quarter. Manager-signed. Rep-driven. Calendar-stamped. The buyers were not tighter this year. The reps were closer to their numbers, the managers were closer to the line, and the discount approval ladder had quietly become a quarter-end ritual that bought the quarter at the expense of next year's NRR.

NRR, of course, had already started slipping. 108 four quarters ago. 96 this quarter. Expansion stuck at 8% against a board-promised target of 25%. Thirty months without a price increase. The board had not yet asked. But the discount report and the NRR line were the same story, told twice, and nobody in the building had the muscle to push back.

I have run versions of this conversation more than a dozen times across Petrichor pricing engagements. The cast changes. The shape does not.

**Discount creep is almost never the buyer**

It is three things, in layered order. Rep behavior, plus manager-approval bias, plus a structural value-metric failure nobody has named.

Rep behavior is the easiest layer to read. Reps quote discount the same way poker players bet — they read the room, not the cards. When discount has been approved at 20% three quarters in a row, 20 becomes the new starting offer. The list price functions as a ceiling the buyer can negotiate down from rather than a signal of where the product sits in the category.

Manager-approval bias is the second layer, and it produces the quarter-end skew. Managers approve discounts at quarter-end that they would reject in week two of the quarter, because the deal that closes Q3 keeps the team on plan and the deal that closes in Q4 does not. The approval criteria are calendar-driven, not deal-driven. The rep learns this within two cycles. The buyer learns it within four.

Structural value-metric failure is the third layer, and it is the one that produces the NRR collapse. Most SaaS companies pick a value metric early — per seat, per provider, per device, per call — that maps cleanly to how small customers use the product. The metric works for two years. Then a customer crosses a size threshold where the per-unit math stops making sense to the buyer. Two hundred providers paying the same per-unit rate as twenty providers does not feel like value to the larger buyer. They negotiate. The rep gives. The manager approves. The discount the company books as "competitive pressure" is actually a confession that the value metric does not scale.

**The Price-Value Integrity Map**

The Pricing Authority Diagnostic in the Resilience Stack repo is built on a single load-bearing concept: the Price-Value Integrity Map. Five integrity points, each scored on a six-point scale, composited into a Pricing Authority Score out of thirty. Price Signal, Value Metric, Pricing Psychology, Expansion Economics, Increase Readiness. The score is the diagnostic. The 90-day action plan is the deliverable.

The diagnostic runs nine segments. Three are load-bearing.

**Move 1 — The Discount Autopsy.** Pull the ten largest discounts last quarter. Recode every reason from rep-claim to true root cause. Reps explain discounts as buyer stories. The recoded reasons are almost always different. Quarter-end manager bias. Structural value-metric failure. Approval-ladder slack. Actual competition is usually the smallest bucket. The Autopsy is the artifact that makes the buyer-pressure narrative impossible to maintain.

**Move 2 — The Value Metric Stress Test.** Take the per-unit metric the product is priced on. Three questions. Can the buyer verify the unit themselves without taking your word for it? Does the per-unit price still feel fair to a buyer at five times the median customer size? When the metric breaks at scale, is discount the workaround? If the answers are no, no, and yes, the value metric has structurally failed and discount discipline alone will not fix it.

**Move 3 — The Price Increase Readiness Test.** Ask the leadership team, out loud, whether the company could raise prices ten percent next quarter without customer revolt. Honest answers fall into four buckets — yes confidently, yes on Enterprise only, probably not, and no. The bucket the team lands in is the diagnosis.

**Sample deliverable — what the scorecard says verbatim**

A line lifted directly from the latest diagnostic deliverable: *"Pricing Authority Score 18/30 — Eroding (target 24/30). Average discount: 21% (4-quarter trend +13 pts). Q-end manager-approved discount volume: 38% (root-cause recoded). Structural value-metric failure: 31% (per-provider metric breaks at >100 providers). The scorecard is not a narrative. It is a diagnostic with a tier assignment, a numerical score, five measured integrity points, and three named architectural actions with owners and deadlines.

**A worked case — Helix, healthcare scheduling SaaS, $28M ARR**

Anonymized composite. Healthcare scheduling SaaS for outpatient clinics. $28M ARR. Series B closed 24 months ago. In the room: Founder/CEO, CFO, Head of Sales, Head of Product. Pre-work: four quarters of every closed-won deal with list price, sold price, discount %, and rep-coded discount reason. Competitor pricing pages. Five lost deals with the price-objection reason quoted by the buyer. Current NRR cohort math by customer size. Engagement time: 2.5 hours.

The diagnostic classified Helix at a Pricing Authority Score of 18/30 — Eroding. Four findings were load-bearing.

The discount root cause was internal, not external. Recoded against the manager-approval timestamps and the deal-level CRM notes, last quarter's discount volume broke down as 38% quarter-end manager-approved with no competitive pressure documented, 31% structural value-metric failure, 19% actual competition, 12% other.

The per-provider value metric broke at scale. Helix priced per provider, flat. Defensible up to 100 providers. Above that threshold, every customer was systematically discounted into the same effective per-provider rate that the median customer paid undiscounted. The discount was buying the deal, not the value.

NRR had moved from 108 to 96 in twelve months. Expansion was 8% against a target of 25%. The expansion gap was an architectural problem. Helix had no usage-based component, no expansion path beyond "add more providers," and the renewal motion had become a discount-defense exercise rather than an upsell motion.

Thirty months without a price increase. The team's honest answer to the Price Increase Readiness Test was "probably not — the team would talk us out of it." The muscle had atrophied.

The 90-day plan had three actions. Redesign the value metric — tiered usage-based component above 100 providers, 45 days. Tighten the approval ladder — manager cap from 25% to 15%, anything above goes to CFO with written competitive justification, 14 days. Run a 10% list-price lift on net-new Enterprise contracts, 60 days. Leading indicator: monthly average discount on net-new deals, target below 12% for three consecutive months.

By the second quarter post-diagnostic, average discount was at 14% and trending down, NRR had recovered to 102, and the first Enterprise contracts under the new list price had closed at full sticker.

**Why most sales leaders miss this**

The sales function is structurally biased against running this diagnostic. The sales leader owns the quota. The quota is hit, in part, by the discount pattern. Running a diagnostic that reclassifies the discount reasons as internal organizational behavior is the sales leader auditing the sales leader. The work has to come from outside the function — either a board member willing to push, a CFO who pulls the timestamps, or an external facilitator whose only deliverable is the gap and the 90-day plan. Self-audit is the version that does not work.

**When the framework does NOT apply**

Pre-product-market-fit companies — pricing experimentation, not audit. Regulatory-defined pricing — when the addressable price is set by a payer or procurement schedule, the integrity map's outputs do not produce actionable change. Single-tier flat pricing under 12 months of data — insufficient variance for the Discount Autopsy.
