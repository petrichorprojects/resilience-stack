## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Why investor narratives decay between rounds even when the numbers are healthy
**Preview:** The deck was honest the day it shipped. Then twelve months passed.

Hi {{first_name}},

You came back Tier 0 Defensible. Trailing-quarter numbers in the deck. Reference customers rehearsed and current. Painfully-honest bear case written and owned. Leadership team would tell the same story in a split-room test. Claimed trajectory shape matches the trailing shape.

So why am I writing to you about decay?

Because every Tier 2 Cracking scorecard I have ever read started with a Tier 0 leadership team that thought the investor narrative was a fixed asset. The deck was honest at the Series A close. The reference customers confirmed the story for the Series A diligence. The bear case got written for the board presentation that followed. Then twelve months passed. The trailing-data window rolled. The reference customers had champion changes nobody tracked. The bear case aged into a sanitized version because the leadership team got comfortable with the answers. The split-room test would now produce visible divergence on at least one major claim — and nobody has run the split-room test since the Series A.

The five fault lines in the diagnostic — trailing-data truth, reference-customer readiness, bear-case honesty, split-room consistency, trajectory-shape match — are the load-bearing surfaces of investor-grade narrative integrity. They decay at different speeds. Reference-customer readiness drifts fastest because it requires active maintenance — champions move, contracts pause, pilots churn, and the deck still names the customer. Trailing-data truth drifts second because the snapshot you pitched at the last round is no longer in the trailing window. Bear-case honesty drifts third because it ages into comfort. Split-room consistency and trajectory-shape match drift slowest because they are leadership disciplines, not metrics — and the disciplines are the last things to break.

The companies that hold Tier 0 hold it because someone runs the Cross-Examination Protocol and the Split-Room Test every quarter. The narrative is the truth the deck cannot tell on its own.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The 5 forensic fault lines and how to monitor each one
**Preview:** Each decays at a different speed. Each opens a different angle of diligence attack.

Hi {{first_name}},

The diagnostic uses five fault lines because a sophisticated diligence team will attack the cheapest one to attack, not the most obvious one.

**Fault line 1: Trailing-data truth.** The fastest signal. Is every load-bearing number in the deck (NRR, growth rate, retention, gross margin, sales efficiency) computed against the trailing four quarters, or is at least one a best-quarter snapshot? The check: pull the deck. For every number, write the window underneath it (Q3 2025 / TTM as of Q4 2025 / FY 2025 / etc.). The windows tell you where the snapshot risk is. A deck with five different windows is a deck the QoE will rebuild for you against one window.

**Fault line 2: Reference-customer readiness.** The second-fastest signal. When was the last time someone on your team called each of the five named reference customers in the deck and verified the story is still current? Champions move every 8-12 months in SaaS. Pilots pause. Contracts get renegotiated below the deck-named ARR. The check: pull the reference list. For each customer, write the date of last contact and the name of the current champion. Any blank cell is a flag. Any contact older than 90 days is a flag.

**Fault line 3: Bear-case honesty.** The third signal. Has someone on your team written the bear case in a document — the most credible short thesis a sophisticated investor would write against you — and has the document been read by every member of the leadership team? The check: ask each leader independently to name the three strongest points of the bear case. If the answers diverge, the bear case is not internalized. If the answers converge on the same three points, the bear case is shared. The internalization is the asset.

**Fault line 4: Split-room consistency.** The fourth signal. If you split the leadership team in half and each half told the company story for thirty minutes, would the two halves match on numbers, arc, and emphasis? The check: actually run it. Half in one room, half in another, thirty minutes each, recorded. Compare. The divergences are the diagnostic. A two-point divergence on emphasis is fine. A divergence on whether the company is "growth-led" or "expansion-led" is structural.

**Fault line 5: Trajectory-shape match.** The slowest signal and the most diagnostic at fundraise time. Plot your trailing four to eight quarters of ARR (or whatever the lead growth metric is) on a chart. Look at the shape. Linear, J-curve, S-curve, step-function. Now look at the shape the deck claims. If the deck claims J-curve and the trailing data is linear, a partner who plots the trailing data — and the partner will plot it — sees a different company than the one the deck describes.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The 90-minute quarterly self-check
**Preview:** Cross-Examination Protocol + Split-Room Test. Two protocols, one calendar reminder.

Hi {{first_name}},

The quarterly self-check is two protocols. Ninety minutes total.

**Protocol 1: Cross-Examination Protocol (50 minutes).**

Pull the current investor deck. List the top fifteen narrative claims — the load-bearing claims, not the throwaway ones. For each claim, code three dimensions:

- Evidence type. Multi-source verifiable / single-source verifiable / anecdotal / unsourced.
- Window. Trailing four quarters / best-quarter snapshot / mixed window / no window stated.
- Owner. Named leader who can defend the claim under partner cross-examination / no named owner.

The variance across the fifteen claims is the diagnostic. Common patterns:

- A claim with anecdotal evidence and a best-quarter window and no named owner is a diligence fatality waiting to happen.
- A claim with multi-source verifiable evidence and a trailing-four-quarter window and a named owner is the kind of claim that survives forensic examination.
- The mix matters. Three claims at 3/3 is not a defense against twelve claims at 1/3.

**Protocol 2: Split-Room Test (40 minutes).**

Three executives, two rooms, 15 minutes per round, recorded. Round 1: CEO + head of sales in one room, CFO + head of customer success in another, both deliver the company story for fifteen minutes independently. Round 2: swap pairings.

Score on three dimensions:

- Numbers convergence. Do the rooms cite the same numbers for the same claims (NRR, growth rate, top-customer concentration, gross margin)?
- Arc convergence. Do the rooms tell the company's history with the same narrative arc (founding insight → wedge → expansion → category)?
- Emphasis convergence. Do the rooms emphasize the same three to five points as the lead claims?

A story where the four executives diverge on numbers is a flag. A story where they diverge on arc is a structural problem. A story where they diverge on emphasis is fine if it represents functional perspective and not strategic disagreement.

Both protocols, same day each quarter, same panel. The discipline is the comparison.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** A Series B SaaS that caught Tier 0 drift ten months before Series C
**Preview:** Two reference customers had had champion changes nobody tracked.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern.

Aperture (anonymized industrial QA SaaS, $42M ARR, Series B + 20 months) ran the quarterly self-check ten months before the planned Series C kickoff. Tier 0 going in. Trailing-data truth was clean (the deck had been rebuilt at Series B close with explicit windows under every number). Bear case had been written and read by the leadership team. Split-room test at Series B had produced a clean result.

What moved was reference-customer readiness. The head of customer success ran the reference-readiness audit and found two of the five named reference customers had had champion changes in the trailing six months. Both champion changes were known internally — one had emailed the head of CS when she left for another role, the other had been mentioned in a customer review call — but neither had been logged against the reference list. The new champions had not been briefed. One of the two contracts had been renegotiated downward by 18% three weeks earlier (rolling up a multi-year discount the original champion had committed to but not documented), and the deck still listed the original ARR.

The team did three things in the six weeks after the self-check:

1. Re-anchored the five reference customers. The head of CS held a 30-minute call with each named reference, briefed them on the Series C window, confirmed the story they would tell if called, and updated the deck ARR for the one renegotiated contract.
2. Added two backup references. Two customers from the trailing four quarters who were not on the original list but had become reference-quality were briefed and added. The deck reference list became seven, with the top five named and the two backups available on request.
3. Re-ran the Cross-Examination Protocol with the updated reference data and the corrected ARR number. Three claims that had been 2/3 moved to 3/3.

Ten months later, at Series C diligence, partners called four of the five named references and both backups. Every call confirmed the deck story. One backup reference volunteered an unprompted expansion-velocity number that became part of the partner memo. The round closed at the target valuation.

The point is not the round. It is the timing. Catching the reference-customer drift ten months before the data room opened was the difference between a clean diligence and a diligence call that surfaced an unflagged renegotiation. The first is a credibility builder. The second is a credibility hit that costs at minimum a valuation turn.

The quarterly self-check is the cadence that catches this. The fundraise-prep sprint is not.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** When to escalate from self-check to a full forensic audit
**Preview:** Two triggers. One is "Series B or C kickoff in the next 90 days."

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email in the welcome arc.

Two triggers move you from quarterly self-check to a full forensic audit.

**Trigger 1: Two or more fault lines slip in the same quarter.**

One fault line moving is noise. Two moving in the same direction inside one quarter is a pattern. When trailing-data truth and reference-customer readiness both move in the same quarter — or split-room consistency and trajectory-shape match both move — the self-check has surfaced what it can. The full forensic audit walks all 9 segments and produces a written Investor Narrative Brief with a Claim-Evidence Matrix anchored against the trailing-data window. That brief is the artifact the leadership team rehearses against and the data room is built against.

**Trigger 2: A fundraise kickoff or major board cycle in the next 90 days.**

The Series B partner meetings. The Series C kickoff. The bridge round timeline. The board review where a major valuation decision (option grants, secondary, employee equity refresh) hinges on internal narrative. Any of these resets the standard the narrative has to clear. The standard a quiet quarter requires is not the standard a partner meeting requires. A 90-day window is long enough to run the full audit and ship the reconstructed brief. A 30-day window is not — at 30 days, the right work is sometimes to push the kickoff and sometimes to walk in with what you have.

If neither fires, you do not need to talk to me. Keep the quarterly self-check on the calendar.
