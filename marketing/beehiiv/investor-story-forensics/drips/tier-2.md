## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The Investor Archetype Calibrator
**Preview:** Platform partner, growth lead, conviction partner. Different questions, different failure modes.

Hi {{first_name}},

At Tier 2 Cracking, the deck has stopped being a single artifact and started being three different artifacts depending on who is reading it. A platform partner reads for portfolio fit, market structure, and exit math. A growth lead reads for unit economics, expansion mechanics, and capital efficiency. A conviction partner reads for founder pattern, category insight, and ten-year arc. The same deck looks defensible to one and indefensible to another — and the audit surfaces which.

The Investor Archetype Calibrator, 60 minutes:

**Setup.**

Pull the current investor deck. Pull the Claim-Evidence Matrix from the kit. Three rounds, twenty minutes each, one per archetype.

**Round 1: the platform partner read (20 minutes).**

The platform partner asks: "What is the market structure that produces a ten-billion-dollar outcome here, and what is your specific lock against the two competitors who will be running at the same outcome in 24 months?"

Score the deck against three platform-partner concerns:

- Market sizing methodology. Bottoms-up with named customer segments and verifiable spend per segment / top-down with TAM citations / no methodology stated. If your TAM number does not have a methodology behind it, the platform partner discards it.
- Exit math. Named comparable transactions with multiples and revenue at exit / vague references to "category leaders" / no exit math. The platform partner will model the exit in the first hour.
- Competitive lock. Specific moat with proof points (data network effects, distribution lock, switching cost numbers) / general claims about being "AI-native" or "first to market" / no lock stated. The platform partner is reading for what makes this defensible at ten times the revenue.

**Round 2: the growth lead read (20 minutes).**

The growth lead asks: "What is the trailing CAC payback, the trailing magic number, and the marginal cost of the next $1 of ARR — and how do those compare to your funded plan?"

Score the deck against three growth-lead concerns:

- Unit economics disclosure. Trailing CAC payback by segment / blended CAC payback / no CAC payback in deck. If the deck does not show segment-level unit economics, the growth lead assumes blended is hiding a problem segment.
- Expansion mechanics. Gross NRR separated from price-increase contribution / blended NRR only / no NRR disclosure at the cohort level. If the deck shows blended NRR without separating price increases, the growth lead's first question is "what's the gross?"
- Sales efficiency trend. Magic number quarter-by-quarter / single-point magic number / no magic number. Trending magic number tells the growth lead whether the unit economics are improving or being held up by one good quarter.

**Round 3: the conviction partner read (20 minutes).**

The conviction partner asks: "What is the insight that made this company possible, what is the ten-year arc, and what is the specific reason you and not someone else?"

Score the deck against three conviction-partner concerns:

- Founder-insight specificity. A named insight with a specific origin moment and verifiable details / general framing of "we saw an opportunity" / no insight stated. The conviction partner is reading for a story they can carry into the partner meeting.
- Ten-year arc. Named milestones with named markets at each milestone / vague "we will be the platform for X" / no arc stated. The conviction partner reads short-arc decks as short-arc companies.
- Founder pattern. Specific evidence of why this founder ships this company / general pedigree references / no founder pattern stated. The conviction partner is investing in the founder more than the business.

Score the three rounds independently. The diagnostic is which archetype the deck fails worst against. A deck that scores well for the platform partner and poorly for the conviction partner is a deck that closes a Tiger or a Coatue and stalls a Sequoia or a Founders Fund. The reverse is also true.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** Aperture's Tier 2 scorecard and the three actions
**Preview:** 9/15 Cracking. +95% claim vs +68% actual. NRR claimed 130% vs 117% gross. Twelve weeks.

Hi {{first_name}},

Aperture at the start of the engagement: $42M ARR industrial QA SaaS, Series B + 20 months, ten months from Series C kickoff. Quiz composite: 9/15 Tier 2 Cracking.

The full forensic scorecard:

| Segment | Score (0–3) | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Trailing-data truth | 3 | Five of top 15 claims used best-quarter snapshots |
| Claim-Evidence Matrix | 2 | 7/15 claims at 1/3 or 0/3, including the headline growth claim |
| Reference-customer readiness | 3 | 2/5 references with unlogged champion changes, 142-day average last contact |
| Bear-case completeness | 1 | Written, owned, recently read — not the problem |
| Split-Room Test | 2 | Numbers convergence clean; arc convergence diverged on growth driver |
| Trajectory shape | 3 | Claimed J-curve, trailing 8 quarters plotted linear |
| Investor Archetype Calibrator | 2 | Strong on platform-partner read, weak on growth-lead read |
| Top-customer concentration | 2 | Top customer 26%, deck claimed "under 15% concentration" |
| Forward-claim survivability | 2 | 4 of 6 forward claims with no proof point on slide |

Total: 20/27. The trailing-data truth and reference-customer readiness segments were the leading layers. Bear case was clean. Split-room arc divergence was a flag worth addressing but not the leading layer.

Three actions over a 12-week engagement:

**Action 1: Investor Narrative Brief rebuild.** Every load-bearing claim re-anchored to the trailing-four-quarter window. The "+95%" headline replaced with "+68% trailing twelve-month, accelerating from +52% the prior twelve-month period." The "J-curve growth" framing replaced with "linear with two acceleration inflections we can name." The "under 15% concentration" claim corrected to "26% top customer, named, with disclosed three-year contract structure and renewal probability." The brief went from 32 pages to 24 pages — fewer claims, every claim defensible.

**Action 2: Reference-customer re-anchoring at higher fidelity than Tier 1.** All five named references re-briefed in 45-minute calls (not 30-minute — the calls included a rehearsal of the diligence-call question set the team expected). Two backup references added from the trailing-four-quarter base. One named reference removed entirely (the renegotiated contract was a credibility liability, not an asset). The reference list became six, with the renegotiated contract surfaced as a footnote disclosure in the deck — disclosing the renegotiation upfront was less expensive than letting the QoE surface it.

**Action 3: NRR methodology document.** The deck NRR claim of 130% was rebuilt as three numbers reported separately: gross NRR (117%), expansion NRR (108%), and price-increase contribution (13 points). The CFO's diligence prep pack was rewritten against the new methodology. A two-page methodology document was added to the data room so the partner's QoE team would not have to rebuild the calculation themselves.

Outcomes at the Series C close ten months later:

- Round closed at the target valuation, oversubscribed 1.4x.
- Lead partner memo specifically cited the NRR methodology document as "the cleanest we've seen at this stage."
- The trailing-shape disclosure (linear, not J-curve) was named as a credibility marker in the partner memo.
- Zero diligence renegotiation on valuation — the budgeted premium held.

The point is not the round close. It is the diagnosis. Aperture had a Tier 2 narrative going into a Series C window. The Tier 2 reconstruction was the structural fix. The trailing-data rebuild and the reference re-anchoring were the cracks the partners would have surfaced themselves. The team chose to surface them first.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** What a Petrichor investor-story-forensics engagement looks like end-to-end
**Preview:** Twelve weeks, three artifacts, one defensible brief.

Hi {{first_name}},

Full engagement shape, no marketing veneer.

**Week 1: data assembly.**

You provide: current investor deck, current data room contents (or planned data room outline), 4 quarters of operating data (deal-level if SaaS, transaction-level if usage-based), 4 quarters of customer revenue (cohort-level for NRR rebuild), reference-customer list with contact dates, current bear-case document if one exists, last QoE if you have run one. We audit data within 72 hours.

**Week 2: full forensic audit.**

We run all 9 segments in two 4-hour working sessions with CEO, CFO, head of customer success. Output: Claim-Evidence Matrix scored across the top 15 claims, fault-line scorecard, written hypothesis brief naming leading layer.

**Weeks 3–5: Investor Narrative Brief draft.**

We rebuild the brief claim-by-claim against the trailing-four-quarter window. Every load-bearing claim gets one of three treatments — kept with new evidence anchor, reframed against trailing data, or killed. Leadership team reviews at end of week 4. Revisions in week 5.

**Weeks 6–7: reference-customer re-anchoring.**

We facilitate 45-minute briefing calls with each named reference (you place the calls; we provide the script and observe). Backup references identified from the trailing-quarter customer base. Reference list rebuilt with primary and backup tiers.

**Week 8: NRR and unit-economics methodology documents.**

NRR rebuilt with gross / expansion / price-increase contribution separated. CAC payback by segment. Magic number trended. Each gets a one- to two-page methodology document for the data room.

**Weeks 9–10: deck rebuild.**

Deck redesigned against the new brief. Every slide tied to a Claim-Evidence Matrix row. Forward-looking claims separated visually from trailing claims. Footnotes used for disclosures the QoE will surface anyway.

**Week 11: Split-Room rehearsal and adversarial diligence call simulation.**

Leadership team runs a full split-room test against the new brief. We facilitate. Three of us play the partner panel — platform partner, growth lead, conviction partner — and run the adversarial question set. Recorded. Reviewed.

**Week 12: ship + measurement plan.**

You ship into the data room. We hand off a 90-day measurement plan against the five quiz fault lines plus three engagement-specific signals (Claim-Evidence Matrix score trajectory, reference-readiness audit cadence, NRR methodology document maintenance).

Three artifacts you keep: Investor Narrative Brief, Claim-Evidence Matrix, methodology documents (NRR, CAC, magic number).

Fixed scope, fixed fee. Tier 2 forensic engagements run roughly $65k. Tier 2 engagements that include a full deck rebuild run roughly $85k. Engagements where the trajectory shape requires a new operating model (rare but possible) run higher and we will tell you that on the call.

Saturday: triage call prep.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** What to bring to the triage call
**Preview:** Claim-Evidence Matrix, three claims you're nervous about, the trajectory plot.

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email. {{calendly-url}}.

Bring three things.

**1. The Claim-Evidence Matrix.**

Filled in. Top 15 claims scored 0/3 to 3/3 with window and owner noted.

**2. Three claims you are nervous about.**

The three claims in the current deck where you suspect — even if you cannot yet prove — that a QoE team will produce a different number than the deck claims. Two-line description each. Why you suspect it. What the alternative number might be if the QoE rebuilds it.

**3. The trajectory plot.**

Trailing eight quarters of ARR (or whatever your lead growth metric is) plotted as a chart, with the deck's claimed shape overlaid. The plot is the most diagnostic single artifact in a Tier 2 conversation because a partner will produce this themselves in the first hour of diligence — and seeing it before they do is the difference between owning the narrative and having it owned for you.

Call ends with one of three answers (internal reconstruction / Petrichor engagement / different team).
