## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** What a Tier 3 forensic reconstruction engagement looks like
**Preview:** 10–12 weeks. Three artifacts. Cost of waiting compounds weekly.

Hi {{first_name}},

Tier 3 engagement is structurally different from Tier 2 forensic audit. The audit alone is not the deliverable. The engagement is audit + Investor Narrative Brief reconstruction + reference-customer rebuild + NRR/unit-economics methodology rebuild + deck rebuild + adversarial diligence rehearsal + measurement plan. Ten to twelve weeks.

**Weeks 1–2: forensic.**

All 9 audit segments plus three Tier 3 additions: full data-room outline audit (every document the QoE will read, scored for internal consistency against the deck), full cohort revenue rebuild (deck NRR thrown out, rebuilt from raw revenue data by cohort), full reference-customer recontact audit (every named reference contacted within the first two weeks of the engagement to surface what the audit cannot infer).

**Weeks 3–4: layer diagnosis.**

Output: written diagnosis picking leading layer — trailing-data / reference / methodology / trajectory. Tier 3 almost always involves two leading layers compounded by the third. The diagnosis names which layer is structural and which is the lagging symptom.

**Weeks 5–7: Investor Narrative Brief rebuild.**

The heaviest single block of work at Tier 3 because it usually involves killing claims, not just reframing them. Some claims cannot be rebuilt and must be replaced. Some claims were never defensible on a trailing basis and need to be retired entirely. Leadership team reviews and revises across two cycles.

**Week 8: methodology documents rebuild.**

NRR rebuilt with gross / expansion / price-increase contribution separated and disclosed. CAC payback by segment. Magic number trended. Gross margin rebuilt with cost-allocation methodology disclosed. Each gets a methodology document for the data room. At Tier 3, these documents often surface trends the team did not know — a CFO who has not seen segment-level CAC payback in 18 months learns things during this week.

**Weeks 9–10: deck rebuild + reference re-anchoring.**

Deck redesigned against the new brief. Reference-customer list rebuilt with re-briefed primaries and added backups. Some Tier 3 engagements remove a reference and add two — the reference list shrinks before it grows because the named customer was a liability the team had not yet acknowledged.

**Week 11: adversarial diligence rehearsal.**

Leadership team runs three full adversarial simulations against the new brief — platform partner, growth lead, conviction partner archetypes, each with their own question set. We facilitate. Recorded. Reviewed. This is the week the rebuild proves itself or proves it needs another cycle.

**Week 12: ship + measurement plan + diligence orchestration plan.**

You ship into the data room. We hand off a 90-day measurement plan plus an orchestration plan for the diligence cycle itself — who answers which questions, who handles the QoE, who runs the reference calls.

Three artifacts you keep: Investor Narrative Brief, Claim-Evidence Matrix (rebuilt), methodology documents.

Fixed scope, fixed fee. Tier 3 forensic engagements run materially higher than Tier 2 because the rebuild involves killing claims and the methodology rebuild is heavy. Fee is named on our first call after the layer diagnosis.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** A Series-B SaaS that took the Tier 3 call ten months before Series C
**Preview:** Deck claimed NRR 130%. Trailing was 91%. Three reference customers had churned. Ten weeks.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern (anonymized, composite).

Company: $38M ARR vertical SaaS, Series B + 18 months, ten months from Series C kickoff. Quiz at engagement start: 13/15 Tier 3 Collapsed.

Picture at engagement start:

- Deck claimed YoY growth of +110%. Trailing four-quarter growth: +44%. Five of the top fifteen deck claims used best-quarter snapshots. Three of the five had been written for the Series B and never updated.
- Deck claimed NRR 130%. Recoded against trailing four quarters with price increases and grandfathered legacy customers separated: gross NRR was 91%, expansion NRR was 84%, price-increase contribution was 7 points. The deck NRR had been calculated against a window that excluded the two largest customer downgrades.
- Five named reference customers. Last contact dates: 178 / 142 / 91 / 211 / 76 days. Two of the five had quietly churned in the trailing two quarters — neither had been removed from the deck. One was on a paused contract the team believed was a renewal pause but was operationally a churn-in-progress.
- Bear case had been started 14 months earlier as part of a board presentation, never completed, owned by a head of strategy who had since left the company. No leader could name the three strongest points independently.
- Split-Room Test (the team ran one as the engagement started): CEO and head of sales told a "growth-led" story with NRR as the headline metric. CFO and head of customer success told an "efficiency-led" story with magic number as the headline metric. The numbers cited diverged by 8 to 14 points on every shared claim.
- Top customer concentration: 31% of ARR in one customer the deck claimed was "under 15% concentration." The 15% number had been true 22 months earlier at the Series B close and had not been updated.

The team had ten weeks. Compressed engagement, three artifacts produced.

Actions in ten weeks:

- Weeks 1–2: forensic + layer diagnosis. Leading layers: trailing-data and NRR methodology. Lagging layer: reference-customer readiness. The reference-customer drift was a symptom — the deeper structural issue was that the team had been operating against a deck the reality had moved away from for 18 months without anyone re-anchoring.
- Weeks 3–5: Investor Narrative Brief rebuild. Eight of the original fifteen claims survived in some form. Four were reframed. Three were killed. The "+110%" headline was replaced with "+44% trailing twelve-month, in transition from a hyper-growth phase to a durable-growth phase." The "growth-led" framing was replaced with "category-defining wedge with disciplined expansion mechanics" — the framing the CFO and head of CS already believed, brought to the front.
- Weeks 4–6 (parallel): reference recontact. Two churned references were removed entirely. The paused-contract reference was surfaced as a footnote disclosure in the deck. Two new references were identified from the trailing-quarter customer base and briefed across three weeks. The reference list went from five to five — same number, different customers.
- Weeks 7–8: NRR methodology rebuild. The 130% number was retired. The deck disclosed three numbers: gross NRR (91%), expansion NRR (84%), price-increase contribution (7 points). A three-page methodology document was added to the data room.
- Weeks 9–10: deck rebuild + adversarial simulation. The deck went from 38 pages to 26 pages. Every load-bearing claim tied to a Claim-Evidence Matrix row. The split-room divergence was closed by adopting the CFO + head of CS narrative as the brief.

Outcomes at Series C ten months later:

- Round closed, but at a 22% discount to the target valuation. Two lead partners passed citing "narrative reconstruction in progress" — code for "the new framing was honest but the prior framing was on the cap table and the partners read both."
- The lead that did close cited the disclosed NRR methodology and the trailing-shape plot as the reasons they came back after the first pass.
- The CEO publicly named the engagement as the reason the round closed at all. Privately, the CEO named it as the reason the round did not close at the original valuation — the rebuild surfaced trends the prior round's investors had not been told about.

The point is not the round outcome. It is the timing. Catching Tier 3 ten months before the data room opened was the difference between a round that closed at a discount and a round that did not close. Six weeks before the data room and the team would have been negotiating from numbers the partners had already modeled themselves. Twelve weeks before and the team has time to rebuild before the partners do.

The cost of waiting compounds because every quarter at Tier 3, the prior round's framing ages further into the trailing data without ever being formally retired. By the next data room, the prior round's framing is on the cap table — the prior investors have heard a version of the story for two years that the new investors are about to see retired. That asymmetry is what costs the valuation turn even when the new framing is defensible.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** Three signals: Tier 3 vs severe Tier 2
**Preview:** Score is one input. Three other signals decide.

Hi {{first_name}},

Quiz score is one input. Three other signals separate a real Tier 3 from a severe Tier 2.

**Signal 1: NRR claimed vs gross NRR divergence.**

Tier 2: deck NRR and gross NRR diverge by 8 to 15 points, usually because price-increase contribution is bundled in. Tier 3: the divergence is 25 points or more, often because the deck NRR window excluded major downgrades or because grandfathered legacy customers were excluded from the calculation. When the deck NRR and the gross NRR tell different stories on direction (one is up, one is flat or down), the deck has stopped being a measurement of the business and started being a description of the business the team wishes existed.

**Signal 2: Reference-customer churn or pause that has not been reflected in the deck.**

Tier 2: occasional reference drift (one champion change, one minor renegotiation). Tier 3: at least one named reference has churned or is operationally churning, and the deck still names the customer. When a named reference would tell a partner a different story than the deck claims, the diligence call ends early. The fastest credibility hit in venture diligence is the one delivered by a customer the founder told the partner to call.

**Signal 3: Split-Room divergence on narrative arc, not just numbers.**

Tier 2: split-room divergence on numbers (within a tolerable range) or emphasis. Tier 3: split-room divergence on the company's narrative arc itself — whether the company is "growth-led" or "efficiency-led," whether the wedge is "category-defining" or "feature-adjacent," whether the next phase is "platform" or "deepening." When two halves of the leadership team disagree on the company's narrative arc, the partners hear the disagreement within thirty minutes of meeting the team — and they cannot un-hear it.

If you have all three signals, the engagement is Tier 3. The cost of waiting compounds because every quarter at Tier 3, the prior round's framing ages further into the trailing data, and the asymmetry between what existing investors have heard and what new investors will see grows. By the time the data room opens, the rebuild itself becomes a signal the new investors price into the term sheet.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** Who else to call if Petrichor is not the right fit
**Preview:** Three referrals. One question each.

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email. Some Tier 3 cases are not the right fit for Petrichor.

**1. The underlying problem is the business, not the narrative.**

When the narrative cannot be reconstructed because the business has stopped working on its own terms — when the growth is durable only because of one customer, when the unit economics do not survive segment-level disclosure, when the trailing trajectory is flat with no acceleration mechanic — the work is operating strategy, not narrative reconstruction. We refer to one operating-strategy partner. Question to ask: "Can you produce a written diagnosis in 4 weeks of which mechanics in the business need to change before the next round is reachable, with a build-or-don't recommendation per mechanic?" The diagnosis is the asset.

**2. The bear case is the prior round's investors.**

When the Tier 3 narrative was constructed under pressure from existing investors (the prior round's lead has been pushing a particular framing, the board has been signing off on metrics the team knew were optimistic, the prior round closed against a forecast the company is now materially behind), the work is sometimes a board-level reset before the narrative reset. We refer to one board-advisor specialist. Question to ask: "Will you facilitate a board conversation in 6 weeks that resets the company's reported framing without producing a write-down on the existing investors' marks?" Most board advisors will not produce that artifact in writing. The ones who will are the right call.

**3. The timing is wrong (fundraise kickoff in less than 30 days).**

If you are less than 30 days from the Series C kickoff or the partner meetings, the reconstruction cannot complete in time. The right work at this point is sometimes to push the kickoff (some leads will respond well to a "we are running a narrative reset and want to talk in 90 days" conversation, framed correctly), sometimes to walk into the partner meetings with a transparent reconstruction-in-progress narrative (defensible if the leadership team has the discipline to hold the line), and sometimes to take the hit on valuation and use the round as the input for the next round's reconstruction. We refer to one fundraise advisor for this conversation. Question to ask: "Will you tell me to walk into the partner meetings with what we have, rather than try to reconstruct on a too-tight clock?" Some will. Most will not.
