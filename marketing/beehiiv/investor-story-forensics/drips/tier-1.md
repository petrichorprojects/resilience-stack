## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Kit + the 9 forensic segments, in order
**Preview:** Open it Monday. The audit works the same week.

Hi {{first_name}},

Tier 1 Vulnerable is the cheapest stage to fix. One or two fault lines have moved. The deck still reads clean to the team that built it. The reference list is mostly current but at least one entry has drifted. The bear case was written and parked. The split-room test would now produce a visible divergence on at least one major claim. None of this is yet visible in partner meetings — and that is the entire point. The window to close the gap cheaply is open right now and closes the day the partner meetings start.

The kit — [investor-story-forensics kit](https://github.com/petrichorprojects/resilience-stack/tree/main/skills/positioning/investor-story-forensics). Block 3 hours. Bring CEO, CFO, and head of customer success. The audit does not work without all three in the room because the vulnerability at Tier 1 usually lives between functional views of the same number.

The 9 forensic segments, in order:

1. Trailing-data truth audit (every load-bearing number coded against trailing-four-quarter, snapshot, or mixed window)
2. Claim-Evidence Matrix (top 15 narrative claims scored 0/3 to 3/3)
3. Reference-customer readiness audit (last contact date, current champion, deck-ARR match per named reference)
4. Bear-case completeness test (named owner, three strongest points named independently by each leader)
5. Split-Room Test (numbers / arc / emphasis convergence across two paired rooms)
6. Trajectory-shape match (trailing data plotted, claimed shape compared)
7. Investor Archetype Calibrator (deck pressure-tested against platform-partner / growth-lead / conviction-partner question set)
8. Top-customer concentration disclosure audit (whether the deck names the actual concentration and discloses the cohort behind it)
9. Forward-claim survivability (every forward-looking claim scored against the proof point that supports it)

Each segment takes 15–20 minutes if inputs are pre-staged. The kit's first page is the inputs checklist. Pre-stage before the session starts.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** How to run the Cross-Examination Protocol in 45 minutes
**Preview:** Fifteen claims. Three columns. The variance is the diagnostic.

Hi {{first_name}},

The Cross-Examination Protocol is the most diagnostic segment in the kit. It is also the segment teams underestimate because it looks like a deck review and it is not.

The protocol, 45 minutes:

**Minutes 0–10: pull the claims.** From the current investor deck, list the top fifteen load-bearing narrative claims. Not the headline tagline — the claims a partner would underline and probe. Examples: "NRR is 130%." "Top customer is under 12% of ARR." "We win head-to-head against [named competitor] 70% of the time." "Sales cycle has compressed from 9 months to 6 months." "Gross margin is 78% and improving." Each claim is one row.

**Minutes 10–35: code each claim.** For each of the fifteen, build a four-column row:

- Evidence type. Multi-source verifiable (3/3) / single-source verifiable (2/3) / anecdotal (1/3) / unsourced or folklore (0/3).
- Window. Trailing four quarters / best-quarter snapshot / mixed window / no window stated.
- Owner. Named leader who will defend the claim under cross-examination / no named owner.
- Adversarial follow-up. Write the single hardest follow-up question a sophisticated partner would ask. Score whether you have a clean 60-second answer.

**Minutes 35–45: count patterns.** Across the 15 claims, look for clusters:

- How many claims at 0/3 or 1/3 evidence?
- How many claims with no window stated or a best-quarter snapshot?
- How many claims with no named owner?
- How many claims where the adversarial follow-up does not have a clean 60-second answer?

Two or three weak claims across 15 is normal — every deck has some claims that are forward-looking or directional. Five or more weak claims is the diagnostic. Seven or more means the deck has become a marketing artifact, not a diligence artifact, and a sophisticated partner will surface that within the first hour of the data room.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The Split-Room Test — facilitation script and what to watch for
**Preview:** Two rooms. Fifteen minutes each. Recorded. The divergence is the diagnostic.

Hi {{first_name}},

The Split-Room Test is the segment teams skip because it feels theatrical. It is the segment partners run themselves, informally, in the second hour of every diligence call. You either run it on your own terms once a quarter or you let the partners run it on theirs once a fundraise.

The protocol, 30 minutes:

**Setup.**

Four leaders, two rooms. Pairings designed for cross-functional mix: CEO + head of sales in room A, CFO + head of customer success in room B. Each pair gets fifteen minutes to deliver the company story to a hypothetical partner audience. Recorded — audio is enough, video is overkill. No deck. No notes. The constraint is the diagnostic.

**Round 1: the open-ended ask (15 minutes).**

"Tell me the company story in fifteen minutes — what we do, who we serve, why we exist, where we are going, why now."

The pairs deliver independently. Note: do not let the pairs prep together. The point is to surface what each pair believes the story is when forced to construct it cold.

**Round 2: the adversarial question set (15 minutes).**

Each pair fields the same five questions independently:

- What is the single biggest risk to the company over the next 24 months?
- What is the trailing twelve-month growth rate, and how does it compare to the claimed forward growth rate?
- What is the top-customer concentration, and what is the contingency if that customer churned?
- What does NRR look like net of price increases versus expansion?
- What is the one thing a competitor could do in the next 12 months that would meaningfully change your position?

The convergence on these five questions is more diagnostic than the open-ended round. The open-ended round shows you how each pair packages the story. The adversarial round shows you whether the pairs share the same underlying view of the company.

**Scoring.**

- Numbers convergence. Do the pairs cite the same numbers (within rounding) for the same claims? Three or more mismatches is a flag.
- Arc convergence. Do the pairs tell the company's history with the same narrative arc? Different arcs is a structural problem — partners will read the divergence as leadership not having converged.
- Emphasis convergence. Do the pairs emphasize the same three to five points as the lead claims? Different emphasis is fine if it represents functional view. Different emphasis on the lead claims is a flag.
- Adversarial answer convergence. On the five adversarial questions, do the pairs give substantively the same answer? Any divergence here is the diagnostic.

If you have one room saying NRR is 130% and the other saying it's 117% net of price increases, you have a Tier 2 problem and you have not noticed yet. The Split-Room Test is the protocol that surfaces it before the partner does.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** How Aperture caught Tier 1 vulnerability ten months before Series C
**Preview:** Trailing growth was +68% on a +95% claim. NRR was 117%, not 130%.

Hi {{first_name}},

Aperture (anonymized industrial QA SaaS, $42M ARR Series B + 20 months) ran the full diagnostic ten months before the planned Series C kickoff. The team came in believing the narrative was Tier 0 Defensible. The audit surfaced something underneath.

The audit findings:

**Trailing-data truth.** Five of the deck's fifteen top claims used best-quarter snapshots framed as steady-state. The big one: the deck claimed "+95% YoY growth." The +95% was Q4 2025 vs Q4 2024, which had been the slowest comparable quarter the prior year. Trailing four-quarter growth was +68%. Same data, different windows. Defensible, but not what the deck claimed.

**Reference-customer readiness.** Two of five named reference customers had had champion changes in the trailing six months, both unlogged. One reference contract had been renegotiated downward by 18% three weeks before the audit — the deck still listed the original ARR. Last documented reference conversation across the five was 142 days earlier.

**NRR coding.** The deck claimed NRR 130%. Recoded against the trailing four quarters with price increases separated from real expansion, gross NRR was 117% and price-increase contribution was 13 points. The 130% number was technically the deck-NRR calculation but masked the trend — real expansion was decelerating quarter-over-quarter.

**Trajectory shape.** The deck claimed J-curve growth. Plotted trailing eight quarters, the actual shape was linear. A partner who plots the trailing data sees a different company than the deck describes.

**Diagnosis:** the leading layer was trailing-data truth (the +95% claim and the J-curve claim). The lagging symptom was reference-customer readiness (the unflagged champion changes and the renegotiated contract). The bear case existed, was honest, and had been read — the bear case was a strength, not a vulnerability.

Three 90-day actions:

**Action 1: Investor Narrative Brief rebuild.** Every load-bearing claim re-anchored to the trailing-four-quarter window. The "+95%" claim was replaced with "+68% trailing-twelve, accelerating from +52% the prior twelve-month period." The "J-curve" framing was replaced with "linear with two acceleration inflections we can name." Less aggressive on the surface, more defensible under examination.

**Action 2: Reference-customer re-anchoring.** All five named references re-briefed in 30-minute calls. The renegotiated contract was updated in the deck. Two backup references were added from the trailing-four-quarter customer base. The reference list became seven (five named, two on request).

**Action 3: NRR recoding and disclosure.** The deck NRR claim was rebuilt as three numbers: gross NRR (117%), expansion NRR (108%), and price-increase contribution (13 points, named separately). The CFO's diligence prep pack was rewritten against the new methodology.

Outcomes at Series C ten months later: the round closed at the target valuation, oversubscribed by 1.4x. Partner diligence calls cited the NRR methodology document as "the cleanest we've seen at this stage." The trailing-shape disclosure (linear, not J-curve) was specifically called out as a credibility marker in the lead partner's memo. The valuation premium the team had budgeted for was held without negotiation.

The point is not the round outcome. It is the diagnosis. If Aperture had walked into Series C with the +95% claim and the unflagged reference renegotiation and the deck-NRR 130%, the diligence would have rebuilt those numbers for them. The +68% trailing number would have come out in the QoE. The reference call would have surfaced the renegotiation. The NRR recoding would have happened in the partner's model, not the team's deck. Each of those is a credibility hit measured in valuation turns.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** How to tell whether your reconstructed Investor Narrative Brief actually improved integrity
**Preview:** The 30-day re-test, with one Tier 1-specific check.

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email.

Re-test protocol, 30 days after you ship the reconstructed Investor Narrative Brief:

**1. Same five questions, same scoring.** Quiz: [tally.so/r/ob5gjM](https://tally.so/r/ob5gjM).

**2. Same data inputs, fresh window.** Trailing-data truth audit against the current trailing-four-quarter window. Reference-customer readiness audit against the current contact dates. Bear-case test (each leader names the three strongest points independently). Split-Room Test re-run with the new brief. Trajectory-shape plot updated.

**3. Score honestly.** Two or more fault lines improved = Tier 1 Vulnerable is closed. Resume the quarterly self-check.

**4. Tier 1-specific check.** Re-run the Cross-Examination Protocol on the same fifteen claims, scored against the new brief. The 0/3 and 1/3 claims should have moved to 2/3 or 3/3, or they should have been replaced by claims that started at 2/3 or 3/3. If the weak claims are still in the brief at the same evidence score, the rebuild was cosmetic — language was changed without changing the underlying evidence.

**5. If less than two improved.** Two possibilities:

- The brief was a language rebuild, not an evidence rebuild. The claims were softened or hedged but the underlying evidence quality is the same. A partner with a QoE team will rebuild against the underlying evidence regardless of the language. Diagnostic: did the Claim-Evidence Matrix scores improve, or only the language?
- The brief addressed deck claims but not the reference list or the trajectory shape. If the deck is now clean but the reference list still has 90+ day gaps or the trajectory shape still does not match the trailing plot, the audit will still surface those in diligence. Diagnostic: did the reference-customer readiness audit and the trajectory-shape match improve?

If less than two improved, escalate. Reply to this email or book — {{calendly-url}}.

Otherwise: quarterly self-check stays on the calendar. Next thing worth your inbox when I have one.
