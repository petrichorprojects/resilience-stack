## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** What a Tier 3 reconstruction engagement looks like
**Preview:** 8-10 weeks. Three artifacts. Cost of waiting compounds weekly.

Hi {{first_name}},

Tier 3 engagement is structurally different from Tier 2 stress test. The stress test alone is not the deliverable. The engagement is stress test + claim-evidence reconstruction + outcome methodology rebuild + competitor verbatim discipline reinstall + measurement plan. Eight to ten weeks.

**Weeks 1–2: forensic.**

All 9 stress-test segments plus three Tier 3 additions: full competitor-set redraw (who you should be tracking now, not who you used to), full buyer-job audit (what job the market is hiring solutions for in your space), and complete outcome-claim trace (every numbered claim back to source data, every claim with folklore evidence flagged for kill or rebuild).

**Weeks 3–4: layer diagnosis.**

Output: written diagnosis picking leading layer — proof layer / discipline layer / positioning layer. Tier 3 almost always involves two leading layers compounded by the third.

**Weeks 5–6: outcome methodology rebuild.**

Every load-bearing outcome claim either gets a published methodology, gets explicitly labeled as estimate, or gets killed. The rebuild is the heaviest single block of work at Tier 3 because it requires going back to customer data, sometimes recontacting customers for permission, and writing methodology documents that survive analyst reconstruction.

**Weeks 7–8: claim-evidence catalog + brief.**

Full claim catalog (40–80 rows). Evidence inventory for every load-bearing claim. Competitive narrative brief with claim-evidence anchors. Drafts and revision cycles with leadership team.

**Week 9: surface rebuild.**

Homepage, deck, demo, one-pager, public evidence pages.

**Week 10: ship + measurement plan + discipline install.**

Ship. Hand off measurement plan. Install the recurring competitor verbatim discipline (monthly cadence, named owner, recurring deliverable).

Three artifacts you keep: claim catalog, evidence inventory, narrative brief. The published methodology documents are reusable for the next round, the next analyst briefing, the next major sales push.

Fixed scope, fixed fee. Tier 3 engagements run materially higher than Tier 2 because the methodology rebuild is heavy. Fee is named on our first call after the layer diagnosis.

Actions in eight weeks:

- Weeks 3–5: outcome methodology rebuild. The "$340M lift" claim was decomposed into three customer-specific case studies. Two had publishable methodology (matched-market design, 90-day window, named source). One did not and was killed.
- Weeks 4–6 (parallel): future-claim proof bridge. "AI-native decisions" was decomposed into three current capabilities and one in-flight engineering plan. Slide 8 of the new deck.
- Weeks 6–7: 60-Second Delivery rehearsal. Eight rehearsals across three executives.

Gartner coverage that quarter: Tessera moved from Challengers to Leaders quadrant. The new dominant competitor (still unknown to the team three months earlier) was named in the report — the team had read its deck by then and the narrative addressed the contrast directly.

Win rate at week 12: 38% (up from 24%, still below the 52% pre-collapse baseline). Trajectory was right.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** Three signals: Tier 3 vs severe Tier 2
**Preview:** Score is one input. Three other signals decide.

Hi {{first_name}},

Quiz score is one input. Three other signals separate a real Tier 3 from a severe Tier 2.

**Signal 1: Lost-deal competitor pattern.**

Tier 2: occasional unknown competitor (one or two per quarter). Tier 3: pattern — three or more, or the same unknown competitor repeatedly. When the same unknown competitor wins three deals in a row, your competitive set has fully drifted.

**Signal 2: Outcome methodology kill list.**

Tier 2: load-bearing outcome claims with folklore evidence number one or two. Tier 3: three or more, including the headline claim. When the headline outcome claim cannot be rebuilt with public methodology, the narrative is functionally collapsed even if the product reality is healthy.

**Signal 3: Executive delivery divergence on adversarial follow-up.**

Tier 2: executives converge on the cold delivery and diverge on the adversarial follow-up. Tier 3: executives diverge on both. Three different 60-second deliveries from three different executives means the team has not internalized the narrative — the team is performing the narrative, which buyers and analysts read instantly.

If you have all three signals, the engagement is Tier 3. The cost of waiting compounds because every quarter at Tier 3, more of the market repositions in the language the analysts are using, not the language you are using. The repositioning is not happening to you — you are letting it happen to you.

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email. Some Tier 3 cases are not the right fit for Petrichor.

**1. The underlying problem is product, not narrative.**

When the narrative cannot be reconstructed because the product no longer addresses the buyer's actual job, the work is product strategy. We refer to one product strategy partner. Question to ask: "Can you produce a written product-market gap diagnosis in 4 weeks with a build-or-don't recommendation per gap?" The diagnosis is the asset.

**2. The category is being defined by a competitor, not by you.**

When share of voice in your category has collapsed and a competitor is defining the category — when buyers describe you in their category language, not yours — the work is category claim, not narrative reconstruction. We refer to one category specialist. Question to ask: "Can you produce a category claim brief in 6 weeks that is defensible against a competitor who has already published their version?" Most category specialists can produce a claim. Few can produce a defensible claim against an active counter-claim. The defensibility is the asset.

**3. The timing is wrong (forced analyst event in less than 30 days).**

If you are less than 30 days from the analyst briefing or the partner meeting, the reconstruction cannot complete. The right work at this point is sometimes to negotiate the briefing window (analysts will often push if the conversation merits it), sometimes to walk into the briefing with a transparent reconstruction-in-progress narrative (which is defensible if framed correctly), and sometimes to take the hit and use the briefing as the input for the next 90-day reconstruction. We refer to one advisor for this conversation. Question to ask: "Will you tell me to walk into the briefing with what we have, rather than try to reconstruct on a too-tight clock?" Some will. Most will not.
