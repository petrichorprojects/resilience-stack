## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Why competitive narratives decay even when win rate looks fine
**Preview:** The narrative was built against a competitor that no longer exists.

Hi {{first_name}},

You came back Tier 0 Holding. Win rate is stable. Someone is reading competitor decks in real time. Future-state claims have roadmaps behind them. Outcome methodology is published. The CEO can deliver the narrative cold.

So why am I writing to you about decay?

Because every Tier 2 Cracking score I have ever read started with a Tier 0 leadership team that thought the competitive narrative was a fixed asset. The narrative landed at Series A and worked. The competitor set drifted. The buyer's mental model of the category shifted. The analysts repositioned. None of these moved in a single quarter. All of them moved between win-rate reviews.

The five attack surfaces in the diagnostic — win rate trend, competitor verbatim discipline, future-claim survivability, outcome methodology, oral delivery under pressure — are the load-bearing surfaces of the competitive story. They decay at different speeds. Competitor verbatim discipline drifts fastest because it requires active maintenance. Future-claim survivability drifts second because product roadmaps change but slide language often does not. Outcome methodology drifts slowly because once published it sticks, until the claim grows and the methodology does not grow with it.

The companies that hold Tier 0 hold it because someone runs the Attack Surface Mapping and the 60-Second Delivery Test every quarter. Without the test cadence, the narrative drifts in the gaps between fundraises and nobody notices until a competitor surfaces it.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The 5 attack-surface layers and how to monitor each one
**Preview:** Each drifts at a different speed. Each opens a different angle of attack.

Hi {{first_name}},

The diagnostic uses five attack surfaces because a competitor will attack the cheapest one to attack, not the most obvious one.

**Surface 1: Win rate trend.** The lagging signal. By the time win rate has dropped 16–30%, the narrative has already been outflanked for two quarters. The leading version of this signal is win rate against one specific competitor in one specific segment. A 10% drop in overall win rate is hard to act on. A 10% drop against one named competitor in your top segment is a positioning emergency.

**Surface 2: Competitor verbatim discipline.** The earliest signal. Someone on your team should read a competitor's full pitch deck — word-for-word, not summarized — every 30 days. Stale verbatim discipline (90+ days) means your narrative is built against an imagined opponent. The right check: ask your head of marketing or head of sales when they last read the top 3 competitors' decks word-for-word. If the answer is hedged, the discipline has lapsed.

**Surface 3: Future-claim survivability.** The buyer's "okay, but how" question is the diagnostic. Strong narrative: specific 12-month roadmap with proof points. Weak narrative: generic answer about innovation or AI. Failure: sales rep does not know. The check: pull the strongest future-state claim in your current deck. Ask the head of sales what the proof point is. The answer should fit on one slide.

**Surface 4: Outcome methodology defensibility.** Every outcome claim ($12M saved, 40% lift) is either anchored to public methodology, partially anchored, internally documented, or folklore. Folklore claims are the cheapest for a competitor to attack — they take one tweet to dismantle. The check: pick your strongest outcome claim. Can a buyer read the methodology without talking to your team? If no, the claim is folklore in practice even if the math is real.

**Surface 5: 60-second delivery under pressure.** The narrative your CEO can deliver cold is the narrative the company actually believes. The narrative the CEO needs to prep for is the narrative the company has not internalized. The check: ask the CEO to deliver the competitive narrative in 60 seconds without preparation. Note the words used. Compare to the deck. The gap is the attack surface.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The 45-minute quarterly self-check
**Preview:** Attack Surface Mapping + 60-Second Delivery Test. Two protocols, one calendar reminder.

Hi {{first_name}},

The quarterly self-check is two protocols. Forty-five minutes total.

**Protocol 1: Attack Surface Mapping (25 minutes).**

Score the five surfaces against the rubric in the quiz, with current evidence. The point is not the score. The point is the evidence column — what specifically changed since the last quarter.

For each surface, document:

- The current score.
- The single piece of evidence that supports the score.
- The single piece of contradictory evidence (there is almost always one).

The contradictory evidence is the diagnostic. A surface scored 0 with strong supporting evidence and one piece of contradictory evidence is healthier than a surface scored 0 with no contradictory evidence and no scrutiny applied.

**Protocol 2: 60-Second Delivery Test (20 minutes).**

Ask three people on the leadership team to deliver the competitive narrative cold, in 60 seconds, without preparation. Record. Compare.

Score on three dimensions:

- Word-for-word convergence. Do the three deliveries use the same words for the same concepts?
- Sequence convergence. Do the three deliveries surface the same claims in roughly the same order?
- Proof-point density. Do the three deliveries cite specific proof points (numbers, customer names, capability differences) versus general claims (innovation, AI, leadership)?

A narrative where three executives diverge on words is fine. A narrative where three executives diverge on sequence is a flag. A narrative where three executives diverge on which proof points to cite means the narrative is not internalized — it is performed.

Both protocols, same day each quarter, same panel. The discipline is the comparison.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** How a Series B SaaS caught Tier 0 drift before the analyst briefing
**Preview:** The surface that moved was not win rate.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern.

Synthetic Co (anonymized retail analytics SaaS, Series B + 8 months) ran the quarterly self-check three weeks before a scheduled Gartner Magic Quadrant briefing. Tier 0 going in. Win rate was 49%, stable. Buyer language match was strong.

What moved was competitor verbatim discipline. The head of product marketing flagged that nobody had read the top 3 competitors' decks word-for-word in 67 days. The check came back: two of the three competitors had repositioned the dimension they competed on in the last 45 days. Both had moved from "data" to "intelligence" framing. Their future-state claims had shifted from "more data" to "decisions delivered." The third competitor had not moved, which became its own signal.

Synthetic Co had a 12-month roadmap that supported "decisions delivered" framing, but the deck still led with "more data." The narrative was lagging the product reality by one quarter.

The team did three things in the two weeks before the briefing:

1. Rewrote the deck opening to lead with the decisions framing the product already supported.
2. Pulled three specific customer outcomes that demonstrated decisions delivered, not just data delivered.
3. Re-recorded the demo opening with the new framing.

The Gartner briefing landed with the new framing. The analyst report later that quarter put Synthetic Co in the Leaders quadrant, with explicit language matching the company's new positioning. The two competitors who had moved earlier got reasonable but less leader-positioned coverage.

The point is not the analyst quadrant. It is the timing. Catching the competitor narrative shift three weeks before the briefing was the difference between the analyst writing about Synthetic Co with current language versus stale language.

The quarterly self-check is the cadence that catches this. The annual deck refresh is not.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** When to escalate from self-check to full stress test
**Preview:** Two triggers. One is "an analyst briefing in 90 days."

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email in the welcome arc.

Two triggers move you from quarterly self-check to full stress test.

**Trigger 1: Two or more surfaces slip in the same quarter.**

One surface moving is noise. Two moving in the same direction inside one quarter is a pattern. When you hit this, the self-check has surfaced what it can. The full stress test runs all 9 audit segments and produces a written competitive narrative brief with claim-evidence anchors. That brief is the artifact for the next analyst briefing, the next board meeting, or the next major sales enablement push.

**Trigger 2: An external pressure event in the next 90 days.**

The Gartner / Forrester / niche analyst briefing. The Series B raise. The category-defining product launch by a competitor. The leadership presentation to the board on competitive position. The acquisition inbound that hinges on narrative defensibility. Any of these resets the standard the narrative has to clear. The standard a quiet quarter requires is not the standard a partner meeting requires.

If neither fires, you do not need to talk to me. Keep running the quarterly self-check.
