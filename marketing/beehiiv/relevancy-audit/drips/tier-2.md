## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** Three layers of disconnect, and how to tell them apart
**Preview:** Most teams misdiagnose the layer. That sets fire to the next six months.

Hi {{first_name}},

Stage 2 Disconnect can sit at one of three layers. Each layer has a different fix and a different cost. The biggest expensive mistake at this stage is misdiagnosing the layer, then aiming a 12-month effort at the wrong problem.

**Layer 1: Positioning layer (cheap to fix, 4–8 week effort).**

The product is fine. The category is fine. The way you describe what you do has decoupled from how buyers describe what they are buying. Symptoms: buyers in discovery calls use category names you do not use; word-for-word match below 30%; analyst language has drifted; you can still win when you get to demo, but you are not getting to demo as often.

The fix is a positioning brief with proof anchors, a homepage rewrite, a deck refresh, an analyst re-briefing, and one round of sales enablement. 4–8 weeks. Petrichor can run this. Internal teams can too if they have a strong head of marketing and a CEO who will hold the brief steady against committee revision.

**Layer 2: Category layer (medium cost to fix, 3–6 month effort).**

The category you have been competing in is no longer the category your buyers are buying in. Either a new category formed underneath you, or the category bifurcated, or your category got absorbed into someone else's bigger one. Symptoms: buyers describe a category that does not match yours; share of voice has dropped sharply against a category name you do not own; new competitors are appearing in your deal flow that compete against a category you have never claimed.

The fix is a category position — you either claim a new category, defend the old one with a sharper definition, or pick which side of a bifurcation to win. Each of these is a 3–6 month effort and a leadership decision. The kit cannot make that decision for you. Petrichor can run the category position; the decision is yours.

**Layer 3: Product layer (expensive to fix, 6–12 month effort).**

The market wants something you do not yet build. The positioning has stopped describing the market because the market moved to a new buying job your product does not address. Symptoms: NPS verbatims show buyers asking for capability you do not have; lost-deal post-mortems cite "missing functionality" as the top reason; sales-cycle stretching is being driven by RFP scoring against capability gaps.

The fix is the product roadmap. Positioning work alone cannot close it. The right sequence here is: name the product gap, decide whether to build, then re-position around the new capability after it ships.

If you are not sure which layer you are in, our call surfaces that. The scorecard from the kit tells me which layer in under 90 seconds.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** Synthetic Co's Stage 2 scorecard and the three 90-day actions
**Preview:** $30M ARR, 38→24% win rate, scorecard pinpointed the fix.

Hi {{first_name}},

Synthetic Co at the start of the engagement: $30M ARR vertical SaaS, win rate dropped from 38% to 24% over two quarters, sales cycle stretched from 47 days to 62 days, three lost deals in a quarter cited a competitor the sales team had never logged. Quiz composite score: 22/30 Stage 2 Disconnect (under the old scoring; equivalent of 10/15 under the current scoring — same tier).

The full audit scorecard, segment-by-segment:

| Segment | Score (0–3) | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Win rate decomposition | 2 | Loss concentrated against one competitor in one segment |
| Sales cycle decomposition | 2 | Time stretching at the "category check" phase, not technical eval |
| Buyer language audit | 3 | Word-for-word match: 18%. Buyers describing a category Synthetic Co does not claim |
| Lost-deal post-mortem coding | 3 | 6 of 10 losses cited "wrong category fit" |
| Competitor narrative pull | 3 | Unknown competitor had reframed the category in their deck |
| Analyst language delta | 2 | Gartner had picked up the new category framing |
| NPS verbatim review | 1 | Customer-facing language was healthy |
| Share of voice trend | 2 | Down 18% in the old category name, share of voice for the new category name was held by someone else |
| Internal language consistency | 1 | CEO and head of sales were aligned internally |

Diagnosis: Layer 2 — category layer. The buyer's category had bifurcated. Synthetic Co was defending the old category name while the buyer's purchase intent had shifted to the new one.

Three 90-day actions:

1. Pick the bifurcation. Synthetic Co chose to claim the new category, not the old. Took two weeks to align leadership.
2. Write the category brief — definition, dimensions, who belongs, who does not. Four weeks.
3. Re-position the marketing surface (homepage, deck, analyst brief, sales enablement) against the new category. Six weeks.

Win rate moved from 24% to 34% within two quarters. The product did not change. The category claim did.

The point of this case study is not the outcome. It is the diagnosis. Misdiagnosing this as a positioning-layer problem would have led to 4–8 weeks of homepage and deck work, and the market would have moved further out of reach during it.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** What a Petrichor audit engagement looks like end-to-end
**Preview:** Six weeks, three artifacts, one decision per layer.

Hi {{first_name}},

I get this question every time someone gets to Stage 2 on the diagnostic. Here is the full engagement shape, no marketing veneer.

**Week 1: data assembly.**

You provide: 4 quarters of win/loss data segmented by competitor and segment, 10 most recent lost-deal post-mortem transcripts (verbatim, not summaries), the last customer survey in raw form, current positioning artifacts (homepage, deck, one-pager, analyst brief), top 5 competitor decks (verbatim), 18 months of share-of-voice data if you have it.

We provide: a data audit response within 72 hours flagging gaps. We do not start segment work until the data is clean. Most engagements that fail fail here.

**Week 2: full diagnostic.**

We run all 9 segments of the kit with you in two 4-hour working sessions. Output: scorecard, layer diagnosis (positioning / category / product), and a written hypothesis brief.

**Weeks 3–4: positioning brief construction.**

We build the brief with claim-evidence anchors. Every claim in the brief has a citation back to a data point in week 1. Drafts go to your CEO, head of sales, head of product, head of marketing. Three rounds of revision before we lock.

**Week 5: surface rebuild.**

We rebuild the homepage hero, the deck opening, the demo opening, and the one-pager against the locked brief. Your team builds the rest (sales enablement, analyst briefing, content backlog) with the brief as the input.

**Week 6: ship + measurement plan.**

You ship. We hand off a 30-day measurement plan against the original five quiz signals. You re-test in 30 days. If the score moved by less than two points across two signals, we owe you a follow-up session.

Three artifacts you keep: scorecard, positioning brief, measurement plan.

Engagement is fixed-scope, fixed-fee. The fee is on the proposal we send after the triage call, calibrated to the layer diagnosis. Layer 1 engagements are roughly $45k. Layer 2 engagements are roughly $75k. Layer 3 engagements are not standalone — they require a product partner and we will tell you that on the call.

**1. The kit scorecard.**

Filled in. All 9 segments scored. Total at the bottom. The scorecard is the diagnostic input. Without it we spend 20 of the 30 minutes reconstructing it.

**2. The three lost deals you remember most clearly.**

Not the post-mortem documents. The story in three sentences each. Why did you lose. Who did you lose to. What was the buyer's stated reason. The pattern in three losses tells me more about the disconnect layer than the scorecard alone.

**3. The most recent positioning artifact your team disagrees about.**

If your team is unanimous on the current positioning, you are not in Stage 2 Disconnect — you are in Stage 0. The disagreement inside the team is the diagnostic. Bring the deck slide, the homepage hero, the one-line description — whichever is most contested. We will read the disagreement together.
