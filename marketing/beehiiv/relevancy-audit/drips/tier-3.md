## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** What a Stage 3 engagement looks like
**Preview:** 8 weeks. Three artifacts. One decision per layer.

Hi {{first_name}},

A Stage 3 Displacement engagement is structurally different from a Stage 2 audit. The audit alone is not the deliverable. The engagement is the audit plus the rebuild plus the measurement plan. Eight weeks, not six.

**Weeks 1–2: forensic.**

We run all 9 segments of the kit plus three additional segments specific to Stage 3: full competitor-set redraw (who you should be benchmarking against now, not who you used to), buyer-job redraw (what job the market is now hiring solutions for in your space), and category-cartography (where you sit on the actual category map your buyers carry, not the one your deck draws).

**Weeks 3–4: layer diagnosis.**

Output: a written diagnosis that picks one of three answers — positioning layer, category layer, or product layer. Stage 3 engagements almost always involve at least two layers. The diagnosis names the leading layer, the lagging layer, and the dependencies between them.

**Weeks 5–6: brief construction.**

Positioning brief with claim-evidence anchors against the leading layer. Category brief if the leading layer is category. Product gap analysis if the leading layer is product. The product gap analysis names the gap and the fix; it does not build the fix.

**Week 7: surface rebuild.**

Same as Stage 2 (homepage, deck, demo, one-pager) but against the new category claim where applicable.

**Week 8: ship + measurement plan.**

You ship. We hand off the measurement plan against the original five quiz signals plus three additional Stage 3 signals (pipeline category mix, competitive-set freshness, buyer-job match rate).

Fixed scope, fixed fee. The fee for a Stage 3 engagement is materially higher than Stage 2 because the work is materially deeper. We name the number on our first call after the layer diagnosis, not before.

Three artifacts you keep: diagnosis, briefs, measurement plan. The artifacts are reusable — they are inputs to the next round, the next category fight, the next analyst briefing.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** A $30M ARR company that took the Tier 3 call
**Preview:** What changed in 12 weeks. What was still broken at week 12.

Hi {{first_name}},

The case study, condensed because Tier 3 readers do not need the full version.

Company: fintech ops layer, $30M ARR, Series C-ready in 9 months. Quiz at engagement start: 14/15 (Stage 3 Displacement). Five-quarter win rate trend: 41% → 36% → 31% → 26% → 22%. Sales cycle: 38 days → 89 days. Three lost deals in the trailing quarter cited a competitor the team had never logged. NPS verbatims showed buyers asking about a job-to-be-done the product partially addressed and the deck did not surface.

Layer diagnosis at week 4: category layer leading, positioning layer lagging. The category had bifurcated 18 months earlier; the company had defended the old side. The new side was where buyers were now placing budget.

The actions, weeks 5–12:

**Weeks 5–6:** Category position. Written claim, definitional brief, who belongs / who does not, ten-page rationale. Three rounds of leadership revision. Final brief locked at end of week 6.

**Week 7:** Surface rebuild. Homepage, deck, demo, one-pager, analyst brief, sales enablement kit.

**Week 8:** Ship. Analyst pre-briefings to Gartner and one niche analyst in the fintech-ops space. Sales team retraining on the new category framing.

**Weeks 9–12:** Pipeline observation. Pipeline mix moved 40 points toward the new category in three weeks. Win rate at end of week 12: 31% (up 9 points from engagement start, still below the 41% pre-decay baseline). Sales cycle: 71 days (down 18 days).

What was still broken at week 12: the product roadmap had not caught up to the new category. The category claim was honest because the product partially addressed the new job — but two specific capabilities the new category implied were not yet built. The company chose to ship the category position on the partial capability and accelerate the missing two onto the next quarter's roadmap. This is the right call at Stage 3. The wrong call is to delay the position until the product is complete.

The point of this case is not the win rate move. It is the layer diagnosis. If we had aimed weeks 1–4 at positioning-layer work, we would have rebuilt the homepage against the old category, and the company would have shipped 8 weeks of work into a market that had stopped wanting it.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** Three signals: Tier 3 versus severe Tier 2
**Preview:** The difference is not the score. It is the time horizon to the breaking point.

Hi {{first_name}},

The quiz score is one input. Three other signals separate a real Tier 3 from a severe Tier 2. If you have all three, the engagement is Tier 3 even if the score was 11. If you have none of three, the engagement is Tier 2 even if the score was 14.

**Signal 1: Competitive surprise concentration.**

Tier 2: occasional surprise (one or two unknown competitors per quarter in lost deals). Tier 3: pattern (three or more, or the same unknown competitor repeatedly). When the same unknown competitor wins three deals in a row, your sales team's competitive set has gone fully stale and the company is now competing in a market it cannot see.

**Signal 2: Share-of-voice direction in the actual category.**

Tier 2: share of voice trending down in your category name but still meaningful. Tier 3: share of voice has collapsed in your category name, and the new dominant share-of-voice holder is in a category name you do not claim. This is the cartography signal — you are not on the map any more, and the new map is being drawn by someone else.

**Signal 3: NPS verbatim coherence.**

Tier 2: NPS verbatims describe your product in language close to your positioning, with one or two of their own words. Tier 3: NPS verbatims describe a product you would struggle to identify. Buyers love what they have bought from you, but the thing they have bought is not the thing you sell, in their words. This is the most diagnostic signal. It is also the one most teams skip because reading raw NPS verbatims at scale is unpleasant.

If you have all three signals, do not wait. The cost of waiting at Tier 3 compounds because positioning decay at this stage is accelerating, not linear. The market is moving away from you faster than you can ship a brief.
