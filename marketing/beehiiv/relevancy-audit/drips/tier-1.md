## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** How to run the buyer language audit in 45 minutes
**Preview:** Three transcripts. Two columns. One revelation.

Hi {{first_name}},

The buyer language audit is the most diagnostic segment in the kit. It is also the segment teams underestimate because it looks like a transcript review and it is not.

The protocol, in 45 minutes:

**Minutes 0–10: gather.** Pull three discovery call transcripts from the last 30 days. Pick one win, one loss, one no-decision. If you don't have transcripts, use Gong / Chorus / Fathom. If you have neither, use the verbatim notes your AEs took in CRM. Do not summarize. Verbatim or nothing.

**Minutes 10–30: code.** Two-column sheet. Left column: your positioning language — the exact words from your homepage hero, your one-liner, your category name, your top three value props. Right column: every word the buyer used to describe what they were buying, why they were buying it, and what alternative they were comparing you against. Copy the buyer's words verbatim. Do not translate.

**Minutes 30–45: read the gap.** Match left to right. Score on three dimensions:

- Word-for-word match — how many of your positioning words did the buyer use unprompted?
- Category match — did the buyer put you in the category name you use, or a different category name?
- Value-prop match — did the buyer surface the same top-three value props you lead with?

If word-for-word match is below 30%, you are not in Stage 1 Drift any more. You are in Stage 2 Disconnect, which means a kit alone is not enough — the gap is wide enough that fixing it requires the website, the sales deck, and the analyst briefing to move together. Reply to this email if your score lands there. I will tell you whether that is what we are looking at.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** Building a competitor intel feed for the competitor you don't know yet
**Preview:** The competitor your sales team will encounter next quarter already exists.

Hi {{first_name}},

The "competitor your sales team did not know existed" signal is the most expensive blind spot in the diagnostic. Once it shows up in your lost-deal post-mortems, you have already lost the round of deals where it mattered. The goal is to find that competitor 30–90 days before sales does.

Three sources, ranked by signal density:

**1. Job-posting language (highest signal).** Set a Google Alert on "PMM" / "head of product marketing" / "VP positioning" for your category and segment. Read every job description posted by a company you have not heard of in the last 30 days. Skim for: who they say they compete against, what category they place themselves in, what proof points they cite. The job description is the company's positioning audited by HR, which usually makes it more honest than the website.

**2. Funded-round announcements (medium signal).** Pipe Crunchbase or Pitchbook into a Slack channel for "Series A/B in <your-vertical>" with announcement date filter "last 30 days." Read the announcement. Read the lead investor's blog post about why they funded. The blog post is the company's positioning translated through investor framing, which usually reveals the bet.

**3. Buyer-mentioned alternatives (lowest leading signal, highest accuracy).** Add a single open-text question to your lost-deal calls: "Who else did you look at?" Code answers. Any name that appears twice in a quarter from companies your sales team has not previously logged is a competitor you need to read about within 48 hours. This is a lagging signal — it tells you who you are already losing to — but it is the most accurate read on actual buying behavior.

Set this up once. Maintain it for 30 minutes per week. The cost of running competitive intelligence well is one of the lowest-ROI things you can do that compounds.

— Phil

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** How to tell whether your refresh actually worked
**Preview:** The 30-day re-test is the only honest measure.

Hi {{first_name}},

Most positioning refreshes fail the same way: the team ships the new language, the team feels good for two weeks, win rate stays where it was, the team decides the framework was the problem (it wasn't), and eight months later someone proposes a re-platforming (also not the problem).

The honest measure is not whether the team feels better. The honest measure is whether the same five signals you scored on have moved.

Re-test protocol, 30 days after you ship the refresh:

**1. Same five questions, same scoring.** Use the original quiz form: [tally.so/r/aQe6A9](https://tally.so/r/aQe6A9). Do not adjust the questions. The point is the comparison to your original score, not the absolute number.

**2. Same data inputs.** Pull win rate, sales cycle, buyer language transcripts, lost-deal post-mortems, and share of voice from the 30-day window after the refresh. Not from the week the team shipped. The week of the ship is too noisy to be useful.

**3. Score honestly.** If two signals improved versus your original score, the refresh worked. The Stage 1 Drift is closed. Resume the quarterly self-check.

**4. If less than two improved.** The refresh moved the language without moving the underlying alignment. Two possibilities:

- The team rebranded but did not re-position. Same value props, new words. Buyers see through this within one demo cycle. Diagnostic: did you change which dimension you compete on, or just which words you use for the same dimension you have always competed on?
- The drift was not at the positioning layer. It was at the category layer or the product layer. The refresh could not have fixed it because it was aimed at the wrong layer. Diagnostic: are buyers describing you in a category name you do not recognize? That is a category-layer problem.

If less than two improved, escalate to a full audit — internal session or Petrichor. The kit's diagnostic tells you which layer.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** When to come back to me
**Preview:** Three triggers. One is "you ran the kit and got more questions, not fewer."

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email in the welcome sequence. You have the kit. You have the protocol. You have the re-test framework. Most Tier 1 leads stop here and run it internally, which is the right move. The kit was built for that.

Three triggers that mean it is time to talk:

**1. The 30-day re-test showed less than two signals improved.**

The refresh did not work. The drift is deeper than the positioning layer. The full audit will surface which layer (category or product) is actually broken. That conversation is not a sales call. It is a triage call.

**2. The audit surfaced a positioning conflict that requires a leadership decision.**

This is the most common reason Tier 1 leads escalate. The kit names the conflict (for example: the founder wants Category X, the head of sales wants Category Y, both are partly right, neither has authority to settle it). A self-serve kit cannot settle a leadership conflict. Someone external in the room can.

**3. A reframe event hit between now and the re-test.**

New round closed, new launch shipped, new category challenger emerged, leadership change. Any of these resets the calibration. The Tier 1 Drift you found may now be Tier 2 territory because the market conditions moved.
