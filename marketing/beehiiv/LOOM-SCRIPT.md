### 0:30 – 1:30 — The case, anchored in numbers

> Anonymized client. Healthcare scheduling SaaS. Twenty-eight million in ARR. Series B plus sixteen months. Walked in convinced they had a discount discipline problem.
>
> Here's what their numbers actually said.
>
> → Discount average climbed eight percent to twenty-one percent across four quarters.
> → Quarter-end approval pattern jumped from eighteen percent of discounts to thirty-eight percent in the same window.
> → Net Revenue Retention was reported as one-oh-two on the deck. Recomputed against the trailing four quarters, cohort-anchored, it was ninety-six.
>
> Three numbers. Three months of board meetings explaining each one in isolation. Nobody plotted them together until we did.

*[On-screen: switch to the scorecard tab. Highlight the three rows: Discount trend, Quarter-end pattern, NRR cohort.]*

> The team thought they had a sales execution problem. The board thought they had a market softness problem. Their pricing page hadn't been touched in fourteen months.
>
> The honest read is that none of those is the story. Discount creep is a symptom. NRR drop is a lagging signal. The disease is somewhere underneath.

---

### 1:30 – 3:00 — The layered diagnostic

> This is the part most consultants skip. They walk in, see discount creep, write "tighten discount approval" in the playbook, charge you forty grand, and disappear.
>
> The kit forces a different question. Where is the decay actually happening?
>
> There are three layers it could be at.
>
> → Discipline layer. Reps are anchoring at the wrong price early in the cycle. Fixable in a quarter with enablement and approval rules.
> → Value-metric layer. The unit you charge by — per seat, per provider, per workflow — has stopped being defensible to buyers. Not fixable with discipline. Requires a structural pricing model change.
> → Rev-rec layer. The way you count price increases versus expansion in your NRR formula has drifted. The deck NRR no longer matches the cohort NRR. Not a sales problem at all. A finance posture problem.
>
> Helix scored highest on layer two. Their reps were anchoring late on most deals — that was real but downstream. The deeper issue was that "per-provider" as a value metric had stopped surviving the variability challenge. Buyers were saying "but our staff turns over thirty percent annually, why are we paying per provider?" — and three different executives gave three different defenses of the metric. Convergence failure.

*[On-screen: scroll the SKILL.md to the layered diagnosis section. Pause on the three-layer model long enough for the viewer to read it.]*

> *Side note — if you've ever sat in a leadership team meeting where the CEO, CFO, and head of sales all defended the same pricing decision with three different reasons, you already know what convergence failure feels like. It's a structural tell. The deeper the disagreement, the deeper the decay.*

---

### 3:00 – 4:30 — The reconstruction

> Once you name the leading layer, the reconstruction has a shape.
>
> For Helix, the layer was value metric. The reconstruction had three options on the table. I'm going to walk all three because the choice is a leadership decision, not a consultant's call.
>
> → Option A. Bring external list price down to closer to what reps actually close at. List becomes honest. Discount creep narrows because the floor moves up. Risk: NRR optics worsen because price-increase math gets smaller.
>
> → Option B. Bring internal effective price up to closer to list. Tighten discount approval. Risk: deal velocity slows for two quarters and some customers churn at renewal because they were implicitly expecting the discount level to continue.
>
> → Option C. Bifurcate. New customers get a new pricing model with a new value metric and tighter discount rules. Existing customers stay on the legacy model until renewal. The two prices diverge cleanly.
>
> Helix chose C. New customers got per-active-user with discount approval at a twelve percent threshold. Legacy customers were grandfathered. Six months later: discount average dropped from twenty-one to thirteen percent on new deals. NRR recovered from ninety-six to one-oh-four on a cohort-anchored basis. The fundraise that was budgeted for closed at the target valuation instead of a haircut.

*[On-screen: pull up the Options A/B/C section from tier-2.md drip if visible, or just talk over the scorecard view.]*

> The point isn't the recovery number. The point is the diagnosis. Discipline discipline alone would have moved discount average by two or three points for one quarter and the same erosion would have reopened. The structural fix was at the value-metric layer. The kit names which layer. The engagement runs the reconstruction.

## Pacing notes

- **Don't apologize for length.** Six minutes is the right length. Anyone bouncing at four minutes wasn't going to book anyway.
- **Pause after the three-number opener (1:30).** Let those numbers land. Don't talk over them.
- **Don't rush the side note (3:00).** Italics in writing = vocal inflection in audio. Slow down 20%, drop register.
- **The "three options" section is the longest single block.** That's intentional. The viewer needs to feel the texture of a real leadership decision, not a sales pitch.
- **End on the manifesto quote.** "Reputation engineering is not the act of being right once..." is the line that does the work. Land it. Then close.

## What NOT to do

- ❌ Don't open with "Hey guys" or "What's up everyone." You're not a YouTuber. You're a strategist showing up at the buyer's desk.
- ❌ Don't reference the Loom inside the Loom ("as you can see in this Loom..."). Reads as defensive.
- ❌ Don't promise outcomes for the viewer's specific situation. You don't know it yet.
- ❌ Don't use the word "leverage," "unlock," "empower," "game-changer," "disrupt," "thought leader," or "deep dive."
- ❌ Don't use em-dashes when talking — they don't exist in speech. Use hyphens and pauses.
- ❌ Don't apologize for the price. Don't mention the price unless asked on the call.
- ❌ Don't re-record. The viewer can tell.
