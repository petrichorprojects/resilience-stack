## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The two-version reconciliation: Internal-Honest vs Externally-Defensible
**Preview:** One version for the team. One version for the data room. Both true.

Hi {{first_name}},

The biggest mistake teams make at Tier 2 is treating the reconciliation as one narrative. It is two narratives.

**Version 1: Internal-Honest.**

The version the leadership team uses for planning, comp setting, hiring decisions, and product investment. This version uses the messiest, most honest numbers. Phantom expansion is removed. Best-quarter snapshots are replaced with trailing-four-quarter. NRR is cohort-anchored. Customer concentration is named with mitigation plans. The forecast variance is decomposed into specific assumption changes. The Internal-Honest version is sometimes uncomfortable. It is always the planning input.

**Version 2: Externally-Defensible.**

The version that goes in the deck, the data room, the board reporting, and the partner conversations. This version is the Internal-Honest version, translated into the framing a sophisticated external reader will not flag. It uses labeled best-quarter highlights where they exist. It explicitly names the forecast assumption changes. It walks the cohort definitions before showing the NRR number. The Externally-Defensible version survives a QoE reconstruction because it was built to.

The reconciliation work is producing both versions and making sure they are derivable from each other.

The artifact has two parts.

**Part 1: The narrative brief.** Eight pages. Internal-Honest version on the left page, Externally-Defensible version on the right page. Every claim on the right page has a citation to the corresponding claim on the left page. Every variance between left and right is named explicitly with the reason for the difference (window, methodology, labeling, scope).

**Part 2: The reconciliation appendix.** Twenty pages. Working calculations, cohort definitions, rev-rec rules, channel attribution methodology, SPOF inventory with owners. This is what gets handed to a QoE provider in week one of diligence. It cuts diligence time by 30–50%.

Internal teams can build the two-version reconciliation. Most do not because the conversation it requires — the moment where the CFO says "the trailing actual is 47%, not 120%" out loud in a leadership meeting — is hard to run without someone external in the room.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** NorthStack's Tier 2 scorecard and the three 30-day reconciliation actions
**Preview:** 22/30 Inflated. Top-1 at 22%. Forecast off by 73 points.

Hi {{first_name}},

NorthStack at the start of the engagement: $20M ARR, Series B + 14 months, six months out from Series C kickoff (not nine months, not three). Quiz composite: 11/15 Tier 2 Inflated.

The full audit scorecard:

| Segment | Score (0–3) | Read |
|---|---|---|
| Forecast reconciliation | 3 | Deck +120% / trailing +47%. 73-point gap. |
| Customer concentration | 3 | Top-1 at 22%, no named mitigation. |
| Channel attribution | 2 | Sales leader could not defend channel breakdown with source data. |
| NRR cohort breakdown | 3 | Deck 115% / cohort-anchored 96%. 19-point gap. |
| SPOF inventory | 2 | Team listed three, audit found five. |
| Revenue mix decomposition | 2 | Trace gap 9%. Phantom expansion concentrated in two customers. |
| Expansion-vs-renewal classification | 3 | Rev-rec rules 18 months old. Pure price increases coded as expansion. |
| Forecast variance decomposition | 3 | Variance traced to three assumption changes, none documented. |
| Board deck claim-evidence audit | 1 | Method documentation existed for most claims. |

Total: 22/27. The narrative-layer scores (2, 5, 9) were healthy. The data-layer scores (1, 7, 8) and attribution-layer scores (3, 4) were the problem.

Three 30-day reconciliation actions:

**Action 1: Rewrite the rev-rec rules.** Two senior team members, three working days. The rewrite covered the expansion-vs-renewal-vs-new boundary, the customer-vs-account boundary, and the labeling of pricing changes. Document was eight pages. Every revenue claim in the next deck was derivable from the rewritten rules.

**Action 2: Rebuild the NRR cohort math.** CFO + analyst, four working days. The rebuild applied the new cohort definitions to the trailing 12 months. The new NRR number was 96%. The deck was updated. The board saw the reset two months before kickoff.

**Action 3: Name the top-1 mitigation plan.** Founder + head of sales, two working weeks. The plan named three actions — accelerate two named expansion targets in the top-10, sign one named strategic prospect in the next quarter, and shift one specific product investment toward de-risking the top-1's renewal. The plan went into the SPOF inventory with a named owner and a 90-day review cadence.

Series C diligence opened six months later. The Externally-Defensible narrative survived the QoE reconstruction. Round closed clean.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** What a Petrichor engagement looks like end-to-end
**Preview:** Six weeks, two artifacts, one calibrated narrative.

Hi {{first_name}},

The full engagement shape, no marketing veneer.

**Week 1: data assembly.**

You provide: 4 quarters of customer-level revenue (CRM export plus rev-rec export), the current deck, the current data room outline, the existing rev-rec rules document (if any), the current SPOF inventory (if any), the last QoE report (if applicable). Most engagements that fail fail here. We do not start segment work until the data is clean.

**Week 2: full diagnostic.**

We run all 9 segments of the kit in two 4-hour working sessions with CEO, CFO, head of sales. Output: scorecard, layer diagnosis (data-layer / narrative-layer / attribution-layer), written hypothesis brief naming the leading layer.

**Weeks 3–4: two-version reconciliation construction.**

We build the narrative brief — Internal-Honest version on the left page, Externally-Defensible version on the right. Every claim cross-referenced. Every variance named. Three rounds of revision with the leadership team. Drafts go to CEO, CFO, head of sales, head of marketing, board chair.

**Week 5: reconciliation appendix.**

We build the 20-page appendix — working calculations, cohort definitions, rev-rec rules, channel attribution methodology, SPOF inventory. This is the document that gets handed to a QoE provider.

**Week 6: ship + measurement plan.**

You ship the new deck. We hand off a 30-day measurement plan against the five quiz mechanics. You re-test in 30 days. If less than two scores moved, we owe you a follow-up session.

Two artifacts you keep: the narrative brief and the reconciliation appendix. Both are reusable for the next round, the next board cycle, and the next CEO/CFO transition.

Fixed scope, fixed fee. The fee is calibrated on our first call after the layer diagnosis. Tier 2 narrative-layer engagements run roughly $45k. Tier 2 data-layer engagements run roughly $65k because they include the rev-rec rules rewrite. Attribution-layer engagements are not standalone — they require a data infrastructure partner and we will tell you that on the call.

Bring three things.

**1. The kit scorecard.**

Filled in. All 9 segments scored 0–3. Total at the bottom. Without it we spend 20 of the 30 minutes reconstructing it.

**2. Three forecast variances you cannot defend with assumption changes.**

Not the full variance bridge. Three specific lines where the number changed and the team did not write down why. The pattern across three undocumented variances tells me which layer is leading. Data layer if the variances cluster on rev-rec definitions. Narrative layer if the variances cluster on framing or labeling. Attribution layer if the variances cluster on channel or motion.

**3. The slide your leadership team disagrees about.**

If your team is unanimous on the current narrative, you are not at Tier 2 — you are at Tier 0 or 1. The disagreement inside the team is the diagnostic. Bring the most contested slide. We will read the disagreement together.
