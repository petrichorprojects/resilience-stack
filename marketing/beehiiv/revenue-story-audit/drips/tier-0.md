## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Why revenue narratives drift even when the business is healthy
**Preview:** Story and mechanics decouple inside a single board cycle if nobody is watching.

Hi {{first_name}},

You came back Tier 0 Reconciled. Forecast tracks trailing. Concentration is inside the band. Channel attribution is data, not narrative. NRR is cohort-anchored. You can name the failure points.

So why am I writing to you about drift?

Because every Tier 2 Inflated scorecard I have ever read started with a Tier 0 leadership team that thought reconciliation was a one-time event. The deck got rebuilt for the last round. The trailing data matched. Everyone moved on. Eighteen months later, a forecast assumption nobody re-checked started carrying more weight than it should. Customer concentration crept past 10%. The sales leader's channel breakdown turned into a sentence. NRR became a few champion logos instead of cohort math. None of these moved in a single quarter. All of them moved between board reviews.

The five mechanics in the diagnostic — forecast truth, concentration, channel attribution, NRR truth, single-points-of-failure awareness — are the load-bearing surfaces of the revenue story. They decay at different speeds. Forecast and NRR drift fastest because they are aggregated. Channel attribution and SPOF awareness drift slowest because they are felt by individuals before they are seen in dashboards.

The companies that hold Tier 0 hold it because someone runs the $1 Trace and the Board Deck Lie Detector every quarter. The trace is the discipline. Without it, the story drifts in the gaps between board reviews and nobody notices until the next round opens a data room.

Tomorrow: walking the five mechanics one by one, what each looks like when it slips.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The 5 leading indicators of revenue-narrative drift
**Preview:** Each drifts at a different speed. Each surfaces a different layer.

Hi {{first_name}},

The diagnostic uses five mechanics because each one decays at a different speed and points at a different problem.

**1. Forecast vs trailing.** Drifts fastest. The forecast inherits assumptions from the last board cycle. The trailing data updates every quarter. Within four quarters, the two can be 30 points apart without anyone noticing because the deck still says what the deck said. The check: pull the forecast you presented six months ago and the trailing four-quarter actuals. Plot side by side. If the gap is more than 20 points and nobody on the team can defend the variance with specific assumption changes, the forecast has decoupled from the trailing data.

**2. Customer concentration.** Drifts second-fastest. One customer that grew 3× over two years became your single largest by accident, not by strategy. The check: top-1 customer as % of revenue. Top-3 as % of revenue. Trend the last 4 quarters. Anything north of 10% top-1 without a named mitigation plan is starting to look like a risk factor on a term sheet.

**3. Channel attribution.** Drifts third. The sales leader stops being able to defend channel breakdown with data because the data infrastructure was built two attribution models ago. Outbound vs inbound vs referral becomes "it's a mix." Without channel truth, every growth claim in the next deck is unprovable.

**4. NRR truth.** Drifts fourth — and is the most diagnostic at fundraise time. The deck claims a trailing-quarter NRR. The data room has a slightly different number because the cohort definitions never got reconciled. A QoE provider will surface this in week one of diligence.

**5. Single-points-of-failure awareness.** Drifts slowest because awareness fades slowly. The team that knew the three SPOFs at the last round forgets one of them. The one they forget is usually the one a partner will find in week two.

Tomorrow: the 45-minute quarterly self-check.

— Phil

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The 45-minute quarterly self-check
**Preview:** $1 Trace + Board Deck Lie Detector. Two protocols, one calendar reminder.

Hi {{first_name}},

The quarterly self-check is two protocols. Forty-five minutes total. The point is the comparison to last quarter, not the absolute scores.

**Protocol 1: $1 Trace (20 minutes).**

Pick the most recent quarter. For every dollar of revenue, walk the path: which customer, which channel, which product, which expansion vs new vs renewal. Sum to the quarter's reported revenue. The trace fails most often in three places — phantom expansion (revenue counted as expansion that was actually new logo from the same parent account), attribution collapse (revenue attributed to a channel the customer did not come through), and renewal-vs-expansion conflation (a price increase counted as expansion when the customer did not add seats or modules).

Output: one number. The trace gap — dollars that cannot be cleanly attributed. Anything above 5% of revenue is a flag. Above 10% means the data infrastructure is broken at the rev-rec layer.

**Protocol 2: Board Deck Lie Detector (25 minutes).**

Pull the last deck you presented to the board or investors. For every claim that contains a number, code it on three dimensions:

- Source — is the number from trailing four-quarter data, best-quarter snapshot, projection, or estimate?
- Method — is the calculation method written down or could a different team member compute it the same way? Yes / no.
- Survivability — would the number survive a QoE provider's reconstruction? Yes / no / maybe.

Any claim coded "best-quarter snapshot" without explicit labeling, "method not written down," or "survivability: no" is a lie detector flag. Three or more flags in a 15-slide deck is Tier 1 Drifting. Five or more is Tier 2 Inflated.

Both protocols, the same day each quarter, same panel scoring. The discipline is the comparison.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** A Series B fintech that caught drift one quarter before kickoff
**Preview:** The trace gap was 7%. The forecast variance was 28 points.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern.

NorthStack (anonymized fintech ops layer, Series B closed 14 months earlier, $20M ARR) ran the quarterly self-check. Tier 0 going in. Came out with two flags.

$1 Trace gap: 7%. Trace failed at the expansion/renewal boundary. A pricing increase pushed through on three of the top ten customers had been counted as expansion in the board reporting, which made expansion-as-percent-of-growth look 9 points higher than it actually was. Not deceptive — the rev-rec rules had been set 18 months earlier and nobody had revisited them. The trace surfaced the conflation in 20 minutes.

Board Deck Lie Detector flags: four. The forecast slide claimed +120% trailing growth. The trailing four-quarter actual was +47%. Not because growth had slowed dramatically — because the +120% number used a 12-month rolling window that included the post-launch ramp of the previous year. Two of the four flags were "best-quarter snapshot framed as steady-state." The other two were "calculation method not written down."

NorthStack was 90 days from kickoff on a Series C planning cycle. The team had nine months to a serious investor conversation. Catching the drift one quarter before kickoff meant the team had time to:

1. Rewrite the rev-rec rules and rerun the trailing twelve months under the new definitions.
2. Re-label every "best-quarter" claim in the deck explicitly, and add the trailing-quarter equivalent.
3. Document the calculation method for the four flagged claims as a one-page appendix.

Zero of these actions required new tooling, an agency, or a CFO change. They required four hours of work and one leadership conversation. The Series C diligence was clean.

The point is not the outcome. It is the timing. Catching the drift one quarter before kickoff is the difference between fixing it cheaply and fixing it under pressure. Catching it during diligence is not "fixing" — it is "explaining," and explaining costs valuation.

Tuesday: when to escalate from self-check to full audit.

— Phil

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** When to escalate from self-check to full audit
**Preview:** Two triggers. One is "you have a serious investor conversation in 9 months."

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email in the welcome arc.

The 45-minute quarterly self-check covers 90% of Tier 0 maintenance. The remaining 10% has two triggers.

**Trigger 1: Two or more mechanics slip in the same quarter.**

One mechanic moving is noise. Two moving inside one quarter is a pattern. The self-check has surfaced what it can. The full audit walks all nine segments and produces a two-version revenue narrative — Internal-Honest and Externally-Defensible — with claim-evidence anchors. That two-version artifact is the asset a CFO brings to a fundraise, a board reset, or a CEO transition.

**Trigger 2: A serious investor conversation in 6–9 months.**

The board reset, the secondary, the Series B or C, the strategic round, the partial-exit conversation, the acquisition inbound that became real. Any of these resets the standard the narrative has to clear. The standard a quiet quarter requires is not the standard a partner meeting requires. The full audit calibrates the narrative against the harder standard before someone external calibrates it for you.
