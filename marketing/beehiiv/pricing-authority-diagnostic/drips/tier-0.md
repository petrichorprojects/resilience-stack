## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Why pricing authority decays even when the business looks healthy
**Preview:** Discounts tell the truth that the deck does not.

Hi {{first_name}},

You came back Tier 0 Holding. Discount curve flat or down. Quarter-end is not buying your quarters. NRR is healthy. Value metric is defensible. The team would not flinch at a 10% list-price lift.

So why am I writing to you about decay?

Because every Tier 2 Compromised scorecard I have ever read started with a Tier 0 leadership team that thought pricing authority was a fixed asset. The price held at Series A. It held at Series B. Discounts crept up 2% per quarter for six quarters without anyone calling it. NRR flattened, then drifted down. One quarter the head of sales said "buyers are tighter this year" in a board meeting and nobody asked the next question. By the time NRR fell below 100% the pricing decay had been underway for 18 months.

The five mechanics in the diagnostic — discount trend, quarter-end pattern, NRR direction, value-metric defensibility, price-increase readiness — are the load-bearing surfaces of pricing authority. They decay at different speeds. Discount trend drifts fastest because every individual deal is a small concession that compounds. NRR drifts second because expansion mechanics slow before new logo wins do. Price-increase readiness drifts slowest because it is a leadership posture, not a metric — and the posture is the last thing to break.

The companies that hold Tier 0 hold it because someone runs the Discount Autopsy and the Price Increase Readiness Test every quarter. The discount file is the truth the deck cannot tell.

Tomorrow: the five mechanics, one by one.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** The 5 leading indicators of pricing-authority erosion
**Preview:** Watch these before NRR catches up.

Hi {{first_name}},

The diagnostic uses five mechanics because each one decays at a different speed and points at a different layer of the problem.

**1. Discount trend.** The fastest signal. Average discount % across closed-won deals, last 4 quarters. A 2% creep per quarter for two quarters is statistical noise. A 2% creep per quarter for four quarters is a 8-point shift — and a shift that large means reps have started anchoring the deal at the discount, not at the list price. The check: pull average discount by quarter, segment, and rep. The variance by rep tells you whether the erosion is operational (one or two reps) or structural (all reps).

**2. Quarter-end pattern.** The second-fastest signal. Percentage of last quarter's discounts approved in the final 5 business days. Under 10% is healthy. 41% or more means the discount has stopped buying the deal and started buying the quarter. The check: pull approval timestamps. Plot histogram by week. The shape is the diagnostic.

**3. NRR direction.** The third signal. Where trailing-quarter NRR sits versus 4 quarters ago. Flat NRR is a flag (not a healthy holding pattern — expansion mechanics have stalled). Falling NRR is an alarm. The check: cohort-anchored NRR, not deck NRR. If the two numbers diverge by more than 5 points, the deck NRR is being calculated against a window that flatters the trend.

**4. Value-metric defensibility.** The fourth signal. The "why per X" test. Can a buyer ask "why per seat?" or "why per request?" or "why per workflow?" and get a 60-second defense without your team saying "because that's what we built." The check: ask the head of sales to defend the value metric to a hostile buyer. Time the answer. If it stretches past 90 seconds or relies on internal language, the metric is breaking under scrutiny.

**5. Price-increase readiness.** The slowest signal and the most diagnostic at fundraise time. Could you raise prices 10% next quarter without customer revolt? Confidently across the board is healthy. "Maybe on Enterprise only" is partial authority. "Probably not, the team would talk us out of it" is eroded authority. "No, we haven't raised in 24+ months and we are scared to test" is structural failure.

Tomorrow: the 45-minute quarterly self-check.

— Phil

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The 45-minute quarterly self-check
**Preview:** Discount Autopsy + Price Increase Readiness. Two protocols, one calendar reminder.

Hi {{first_name}},

The quarterly self-check is two protocols. Forty-five minutes total.

**Protocol 1: Discount Autopsy (25 minutes).**

Pull the 10 largest discounts approved last quarter. For each, code three dimensions:

- Trigger: who requested the discount (rep, buyer, manager)?
- Reason claimed: what reason did the rep submit when requesting approval?
- Reason actual: what was the underlying business reason? (Often different from the claimed reason.)

The variance between claimed reason and actual reason is the diagnostic. Common reason-collapse patterns:

- Claimed: "competitive pressure." Actual: rep had not prepared the value-anchored close.
- Claimed: "budget constraint." Actual: rep had not surfaced the budget conversation early enough.
- Claimed: "multi-year commitment." Actual: customer would have signed annual at list with the right close.

Two or three reason-collapses across 10 deals is normal. Five or more is a flag. Seven or more means the discount approval process has become a rubber stamp and the reps have learned that the claim does not need to match reality.

**Protocol 2: Price Increase Readiness Test (20 minutes).**

Ask the head of sales, the head of customer success, and the CEO a single question independently: "Could you raise prices 10% on Enterprise customers next quarter without losing more than 5% of them?"

Score three things:

- Confidence: confidently yes / probably yes / unsure / probably no / definitely no.
- Reasoning: did each leader cite specific customer evidence, or general posture?
- Convergence: do the three answers match within one confidence tier?

Three confidently-yes answers with specific customer evidence is Tier 0. Two confidently-yes with one unsure is Tier 0 going into Tier 1. Three different answers is a flag — the leadership team has not internalized pricing authority as a shared posture.

Both protocols, same day each quarter, same panel.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** A healthcare SaaS that caught quarter-end bias before the NRR cliff
**Preview:** Quarter-end approval climbed from 18% to 31% in two cycles. NRR was still 108%.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern.

Helix (anonymized healthcare scheduling SaaS, $28M ARR, Series B + 16 months) ran the quarterly self-check three quarters before NRR cracked below 100%. Going in: NRR 108%, discount average 11%, quarter-end approvals 18% in the trailing quarter. Tier 0 on the surface.

The self-check surfaced two patterns the team had not seen because no one was looking at the data this way.

**Pattern 1: Quarter-end approval climbing.**

18% in Q-2. 24% in Q-1. 31% in the current quarter. Trajectory was clear. The trend had been visible in CRM for three quarters and no one had plotted it. Quarter-end discount approval is a leading signal — it moves before discount magnitude does because reps first learn that quarter-end gets approval, then start asking for larger discounts at quarter-end. Helix was at the first stage. Six months from the second.

**Pattern 2: Reason-collapse in the discount autopsy.**

Top 10 discounts last quarter, recoded. Six of ten cited "competitive pressure." The actual reason on four of those six was "rep submitted the deal to manager review on the second-to-last day of the quarter without a value-anchored close prepared." Competitive pressure was the cover story. The pattern was rep behavior, not market pressure.

Two actions in the two-week window after the self-check:

1. Quarter-end approval cutoff. New rule: any discount above 15% requested in the final 5 business days requires VP Sales review and a written justification. Not to refuse the discount — to surface the rep-prep gap.
2. Rep enablement on value-anchored close. Three of the reps with the highest discount averages went through a one-day refresher on closing without leading with discount.

90 days later: quarter-end approvals dropped to 21% in the next cycle. Discount average held. NRR moved up two points to 110%. The cliff was averted because the leading signal got attention before NRR moved.

The point is not the NRR number. It is the cadence. Helix caught the quarter-end bias one cycle before it would have shown up in NRR. The self-check is the cadence. Without it, the same leadership team would have been looking at a sub-100% NRR number in six months and asking the wrong question.

Tuesday: when to escalate from self-check to a full diagnostic.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** When to escalate from self-check to a full diagnostic
**Preview:** Two triggers. One is "discount approvals are now 30%+ in the final 5 days."

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email in the welcome arc.

Two triggers move you from quarterly self-check to a full pricing-authority diagnostic.

**Trigger 1: Two or more mechanics slip in the same quarter.**

One mechanic moving is noise. Two moving in the same direction inside one quarter is a pattern. When discount trend and quarter-end pattern both move up in the same quarter — or NRR and value-metric defensibility both move down — the self-check has surfaced what it can. The full diagnostic walks all 9 segments and produces a written discount-discipline document, a rebuilt rev-rec posture if needed, and a Price-Value Integrity scorecard the CEO and CFO can use as a leadership artifact.

**Trigger 2: A renewal cycle, fundraise, or pricing reset in the next 90 days.**

The major renewal wave in a quarter. The Series C raise. The list-price reset the team has been postponing for two years. The buyer-side procurement event (CFO mandate to renegotiate vendor contracts in your category). Any of these resets the standard pricing authority has to clear. The standard a quiet quarter requires is not the standard a renewal wave requires.

If neither fires, you do not need to talk to me. Keep the quarterly self-check on the calendar.
