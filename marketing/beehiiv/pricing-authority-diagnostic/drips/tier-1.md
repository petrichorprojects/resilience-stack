## Email 1 — Day 2

**Subject:** Kit + the 9 diagnostic segments, in order
**Preview:** Open it Monday. The diagnostic works the same week.

Hi {{first_name}},

Tier 1 Eroding is the cheapest stage to fix. Discounts have crept up. Quarter-end is doing more work than your value proposition is. NRR has flattened. The pattern is not yet visible in the deck.

The kit — [pricing-authority-diagnostic kit](https://github.com/petrichorprojects/resilience-stack/tree/main/skills/growth/pricing-authority-diagnostic). Block 2.5 hours. Bring CEO, CFO, head of sales. The diagnostic does not work without all three in the room because the erosion at Tier 1 usually lives between functional views of the same discount file.

The 9 segments, in order:

1. Discount trend decomposition (by segment, by rep, by quarter)
2. Quarter-end approval pattern (timestamp histogram)
3. NRR cohort breakdown (deck vs cohort-anchored)
4. Value-metric defensibility audit (the "why per X" test against 5 real buyer objections)
5. Price-increase readiness (CEO + CFO + head of sales independent answer)
6. Discount reason audit (claimed vs actual, top 10 deals)
7. Rev-rec rules audit (expansion-vs-renewal classification, price increase coding)
8. List-price-vs-floor-price decomposition (where reps are anchoring)
9. Competitive concession audit (what discounts went to "competitive pressure" deals vs other reasons)

Each segment takes 15–20 minutes if inputs are pre-staged. The kit's first page is the inputs checklist. Pre-stage before the session starts.

## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** How to run the Discount Autopsy in 45 minutes
**Preview:** Ten deals. Three columns. The variance is the diagnostic.

Hi {{first_name}},

The Discount Autopsy is the most diagnostic segment in the kit. It is also the segment teams underestimate because it looks like a deal review and it is not.

The protocol, 45 minutes:

**Minutes 0–10: pull the data.** The 10 largest discounts approved in the trailing quarter, by absolute discount dollars. Not the 10 largest deals — the 10 largest discount dollars. Include: deal size, list price, sold price, discount %, discount approver, approval date, rep, segment, claimed reason on the approval submission.

**Minutes 10–35: recode each deal.** For each of the 10, build a three-column row:

- Reason claimed (verbatim from the approval submission).
- Reason actual (your honest read of what happened).
- Behavior pattern (what specifically did the rep do or not do that led to the discount).

The reason actual usually falls in one of five patterns:

1. Rep submitted the deal to manager review late, no value-anchored close prepared.
2. Rep anchored the buyer at the discount price early in the cycle ("our enterprise pricing starts at...") and could not move them back.
3. Buyer presented a competitive bid the rep had not seen and the rep matched without questioning the bid's structure.
4. Buyer asked for a multi-year commitment in exchange for a discount the company would have given anyway for an annual.
5. Rep used quarter-end timing as the closing mechanism instead of value, and the buyer cashed in.

**Minutes 35–45: count patterns.** Across the 10 deals, count which pattern dominates. The pattern is the diagnostic. Pattern 1 is rep enablement. Pattern 2 is discovery process. Pattern 3 is competitive intelligence. Pattern 4 is rev-rec rules. Pattern 5 is calendar discipline.

The pattern across 10 deals is more diagnostic than any single metric. Five or more deals in the same pattern is the fix priority.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** The Value Metric Stress Test — recoding "per X" against buyer scrutiny
**Preview:** Three buyer objections. One 60-second defense. No internal language.

Hi {{first_name}},

The value metric is the unit your customers buy by — per seat, per request, per workflow, per transaction, per gigabyte, per outcome. It is the load-bearing decision in your pricing model. When it stops being defensible, the discount creep is downstream, not the disease.

The Value Metric Stress Test, 30 minutes:

**Setup.**

Pick the head of sales or the head of customer success. The CEO can play the buyer if needed. The CFO observes and scores.

**Round 1: the buyer's "why per X?" (10 minutes).**

The buyer asks the why-per-X question three different ways:

- "Why per seat when our user base fluctuates?" (variability challenge)
- "Why per seat when the value comes from outcomes, not access?" (alignment challenge)
- "Why per seat when your competitor charges per workflow?" (comparison challenge)

The defender has 60 seconds per response. Score:

- Did the defender use a buyer-facing proof point? (specific customer, specific number, specific verifiable claim)
- Did the defender avoid internal language? ("because that's our pricing model," "because that's what we built," "because of how we account for usage")
- Did the defender's answer survive a 60-second follow-up?

Three clean defenses = Tier 0 on this surface. One folklore defense = Tier 1. Two or three folklore defenses = Tier 2.

**Round 2: the competitor's value metric (10 minutes).**

Pull the value metric of your top 3 competitors. For each, ask:

- Is their value metric simpler than yours?
- Does their value metric align more directly to a buyer outcome than yours?
- Is their value metric easier to forecast for a buyer?

If two of three competitors score "yes" on any of these dimensions, your value metric is structurally exposed. The discount creep you are seeing is the buyer paying for the friction your value metric creates.

**Round 3: the rebuild question (10 minutes).**

If you were starting today, would you choose this value metric? Honestly. CEO, CFO, head of sales independently. If two of three say "no" or "I would change at least one dimension," the value metric needs work at the audit level, not the discount-discipline level.

A value metric problem is not solvable by discount discipline. The discount discipline is the symptom. The value metric is the disease.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** How Helix caught Tier 1 erosion 90 days before the NRR cliff
**Preview:** Discount climbed 8→14% in three quarters. NRR was still 108%.

Hi {{first_name}},

Helix (anonymized healthcare scheduling SaaS, $28M ARR) ran the full diagnostic four quarters before NRR dropped below 100%. The team came in believing the erosion was a discount problem. The diagnostic surfaced something underneath.

The diagnostic findings:

**Discount trend:** average discount climbed from 8% to 14% across three quarters. Quarter-end approvals climbed from 18% to 31%.

**Discount Autopsy across 10 deals:** six of ten cited "competitive pressure." Recoded, the actual reason on five of those six was Pattern 2 — reps were anchoring the buyer at discount pricing early in the cycle. The phrase "our enterprise pricing starts at..." had crept into discovery decks across three reps. The starting point in buyer conversations was the discount floor, not the list price.

**Value Metric Stress Test:** Helix charged per-provider per-month. The buyer's "why per provider when our staff turns over 30% annually?" got a 90-second response from the head of sales that drifted into internal language. The CEO's response was clean but acknowledged the variability concern was real. The CFO's response cited the rev-rec rules. Three different defenses across three executives — convergence failure.

**Diagnosis:** the value metric was structurally exposed to the variability concern. The discount creep was reps absorbing buyer friction the value metric was creating. Discount discipline would have flattened the discount trend for one or two quarters — and then the same erosion would have reopened because the value metric was still creating the friction.

Three 90-day actions:

**Action 1: Value metric augmentation.** Helix added a second metric option (per active user per month) alongside the per-provider option. Existing customers stayed on per-provider unless they requested the switch. New deals offered both, with a recommendation engine that picked the cheaper option for the buyer based on usage projections. The change took six weeks of product work and three weeks of go-to-market enablement.

**Action 2: Discovery enablement.** All reps went through a one-day refresher on anchoring at list price, not at floor. The phrase "our enterprise pricing starts at..." was killed from all sales materials. New language: "list price for your team size is X; we have a path to optimization based on usage."

**Action 3: Discount approval rebuild.** New rule: discounts above 15% require VP Sales review and a written justification matching one of five approved patterns. Reason-collapse pattern was monitored quarterly.

Outcomes at four quarters out: discount average dropped from 14% to 9%. Quarter-end approval dropped from 31% to 22%. NRR moved from 108% to 113% (instead of cliff to 96% which was the trajectory). The cliff was averted.

The point is not the NRR number. It is the diagnosis. If Helix had treated this as a discount discipline problem, they would have caught the discount creep for one quarter and re-opened it. The fix was at the value-metric layer, not the discipline layer.

## Email 5 — Day 14

**Subject:** How to tell whether your discount discipline actually closed the gap
**Preview:** The 30-day re-test, with one Tier 1-specific check.

Hi {{first_name}},

Last email.

Re-test protocol, 30 days after the discipline reset:

**1. Same five questions, same scoring.** Quiz: [tally.so/r/1ANOQL](https://tally.so/r/1ANOQL).

**2. Same data inputs, fresh window.** Discount trend from the 30 days after the reset. Quarter-end approval timestamps from the next quarter-end cycle (the timing matters — the discipline is tested at quarter-end, not mid-quarter). NRR from the cohort-anchored calculation. Value-metric defense from a fresh stress test.

**3. Score honestly.** Two or more mechanics improved = Tier 1 Eroding is closed. Resume the quarterly self-check.

**4. Tier 1-specific check.** Re-run the Discount Autopsy on the top 10 deals from the 30-day window. Same recoding protocol. If the reason-collapse pattern improved (fewer "competitive pressure" claims, more accurate reason matching), the discipline reset is internalized. If the pattern is the same, the reps have learned to recode the claims without changing the behavior — a sanitization fix, not a discipline fix.

**5. If less than two improved.** Two possibilities:

- The discipline reset fixed the symptom without fixing the cause. The discount creep was downstream of the value metric or the discovery process. The discipline reset will hold for one or two quarters and then reopen. Diagnostic: do the Discount Autopsy patterns cluster on Pattern 2 (anchoring) or Pattern 3 (competitive)? If yes, the cause is the value metric or competitive intelligence, not discount discipline.
- The reset addressed reps but not the rev-rec rules. If a pure price increase is being coded as expansion in your rev-rec system, NRR will hold artificially while real expansion mechanics are stalling. The discipline cannot surface this. Diagnostic: does your rev-rec rules document distinguish price-increase from expansion clearly? Yes/no.

If less than two improved, escalate. Reply to this email or book — {{calendly-url}}.

Otherwise: quarterly self-check stays on the calendar. Next thing worth your inbox when I have one.
