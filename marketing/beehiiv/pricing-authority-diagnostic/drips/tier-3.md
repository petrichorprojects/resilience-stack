## Email 2 — Day 4

**Subject:** What a Tier 3 reconstruction engagement looks like
**Preview:** 8–10 weeks. Three artifacts. Cost of waiting compounds weekly.

Hi {{first_name}},

Tier 3 engagement is structurally different from Tier 2. The diagnostic alone is not the deliverable. The engagement is diagnostic + Internal-Effective vs External-List reconciliation + value-metric rebuild + rev-rec rules rebuild + discount discipline reinstall + measurement plan. Eight to ten weeks.

**Weeks 1–2: forensic.**

All 9 diagnostic segments plus three Tier 3 additions: full rev-rec audit (every price increase classified, every expansion classified, every renewal classified, every dollar in the deck NRR traced to its mechanic), full cohort NRR rebuild (deck NRR thrown out, rebuilt from raw revenue data by cohort), full discount file audit (4 quarters of deals, every line item recoded against the five-pattern framework).

**Weeks 3–4: layer diagnosis.**

Output: written diagnosis picking leading layer — discipline / value-metric / rev-rec / list-price. Tier 3 always involves two leading layers compounded by the third. The diagnosis names which layer is structural and which is the lagging symptom.

**Weeks 5–7: pricing model rebuild.**

The heaviest single block of work at Tier 3 because it usually involves value-metric work, list-price reset, and new-customer pricing model design simultaneously. Some companies bifurcate (Option C) — new pricing for new customers, legacy customers grandfathered until renewal. Some rebuild end-to-end. Leadership decides after the diagnosis.

**Week 8: rev-rec rules rebuild.**

Price-increase coding rewritten. Expansion-vs-renewal classification documented. Cohort NRR calculation methodology written and embedded in the finance close process. The CFO leaves with a methodology document that survives an auditor reading.

**Week 9: discount discipline reset.**

Five-pattern reason coding. Threshold-and-justification rules. Recurring autopsy deliverable. Sales enablement on anchoring, discovery, and value-anchored close.

**Week 10: ship + measurement plan.**

Ship the new model. Hand off a 90-day measurement plan against all five quiz mechanics plus three Tier 3-specific signals (Internal-Effective price gap closure by segment, cohort NRR trajectory by quarter, value-metric switch rate if augmented).

Three artifacts you keep: Internal-Effective price map, pricing model document (list, floor, value metric, rev-rec rules, discount approval rules in one document), discount discipline document.

Fixed scope, fixed fee. Tier 3 engagements run materially higher than Tier 2 because the rebuild is heavier and involves more cross-functional work. Fee is named on our first call after the layer diagnosis.

## Email 3 — Day 7

**Subject:** A healthcare SaaS that took the Tier 3 call
**Preview:** Discount 8→24%. NRR 108→94%. Two leading layers, one lagging. Ten weeks.

Hi {{first_name}},

The synthetic case, drawn from a real engagement pattern (anonymized, composite).

Company: healthcare scheduling SaaS, $34M ARR, Series B + 22 months, six weeks from a fundraise the board was relying on. Quiz at engagement start: 13/15 Tier 3 Collapsed.

Picture at engagement start:

- Discount average: 8% → 24% across five quarters. Quarter-end approvals: 18% → 44%.
- NRR: deck 102%, cohort-anchored 94%. The deck NRR was being calculated against a window that included two price increases coded as expansion. When recoded, expansion NRR was 71% and gross NRR (real expansion mechanics) was breaking.
- Value-metric defensibility: per-provider pricing was failing the variability challenge across all three executives. Three different defenses. CEO's defense was clean but acknowledged the concern was real. Head of sales drifted to internal language at 50 seconds. CFO cited rev-rec rules and did not address the value question.
- List-vs-floor: reps had been anchoring at 65% of list as "starting point" for six months. The 35% gap between list and starting-point was the de facto list price.
- Discount Autopsy: 8 of 10 top discounts last quarter cited "competitive pressure." Recoded: 5 of 8 were Pattern 2 (rep anchoring at floor early), 2 of 8 were Pattern 4 (multi-year commitment giveaway), 1 of 8 was actual competitive.

The team had ten weeks. Compressed engagement, three artifacts produced (Internal-Effective price map, pricing model document, discount discipline document).

Actions in ten weeks:

- Weeks 1–2: forensic + layer diagnosis. Leading layers: value-metric and rev-rec. Lagging layer: discount discipline. Discount discipline reset alone would have moved discount average by 3 points for a quarter and reopened — the value-metric and rev-rec layers were the structural fix.
- Weeks 3–5: pricing model rebuild. Bifurcation (Option C). New customers got per-active-user with discount approval at 12% threshold, list price reset to 110% of the old effective price, two-tier pricing eliminated. Legacy customers grandfathered until renewal; renewal conversion plan tracked per customer.
- Weeks 6–7 (parallel): rev-rec rules rebuild. Price-increase line coded separately from expansion. Cohort NRR calculation methodology written. The deck NRR was killed and replaced with three numbers (gross NRR, expansion NRR, price-increase contribution) reported separately.
- Weeks 8–9: discount discipline reset. Five-pattern coding. 12% threshold for VP Sales review. Quarterly autopsy as recurring deliverable.
- Week 10: ship + measurement plan + board prep.

Outcomes at 6 months post-engagement:

- Discount average: 24% → 14% on new deals (legacy customers tracked separately).
- Quarter-end approval: 44% → 26%.
- Cohort NRR: 94% → 101% (real recovery, not coding-driven).
- Fundraise closed at the target valuation. The board diligence included the rebuilt rev-rec methodology document and the Internal-Effective price map. Investors flagged the rebuild as a positive signal in the term sheet conversation.

The point: catching Tier 3 six weeks before a fundraise is the difference between negotiating from defensible numbers and negotiating from numbers the diligence team will rebuild for you in a less flattering direction.

## Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** Three signals: Tier 3 vs severe Tier 2
**Preview:** Score is one input. Three other signals decide.

Hi {{first_name}},

Quiz score is one input. Three other signals separate a real Tier 3 from a severe Tier 2.

**Signal 1: Cohort NRR vs deck NRR divergence.**

Tier 2: deck NRR and cohort NRR are within 5 points. The deck is optimistic but not deceptive. Tier 3: the divergence is 8 points or more. The deck is masking the trend, usually because price increases are being coded as expansion. When the deck NRR is structurally disconnected from the cohort NRR, the rev-rec rules are part of the disease, not just a measurement artifact.

**Signal 2: Value-metric failure across three executives.**

Tier 2: the value metric fails the "why per X" test on one or two of three executives. The team has not converged but the metric is defensible from at least one angle. Tier 3: the metric fails on all three executives, and the three defenses are non-overlapping. When three executives cannot converge on a single defense of the value metric, the metric has stopped being a metric and started being a legacy decision that nobody has the authority to unwind.

**Signal 3: List-vs-floor gap exceeds 30%.**

Tier 2: reps are anchoring at 80–85% of list. The list price is aspirational but not theatrical. Tier 3: reps are anchoring at 65–70% of list. The list price has become marketing — the price the deck shows for credibility, not the price anyone expects to pay. When the gap is structural, the discount discipline reset cannot close it because the reps are not the problem; the model is.

If you have all three signals, the engagement is Tier 3. The cost of waiting compounds because every quarter at Tier 3, more of the customer base anchors to the discounted reality, and the price-increase readiness erodes further. By the next fundraise or major renewal cycle, the diligence team rebuilds the numbers for you in the direction you would not have chosen.
