# Investor Story Forensics Scoring Rubric

A 10-dimension diagnostic measuring narrative survivability under forensic diligence. Score each 0-3. Total 0-30. Composite maps to Narrative Integrity tier (Defensible / Vulnerable / Collapsed).

This rubric is the spine of every investor-story-forensics run. Use it after Phase 2 Segments 1-7 (Forensic Evidence Wall through Reference Arc Audit), once trailing-data verification, the bear-case pre-mortem, and the split-room test are on the table. Scoring earlier means scoring deck narrative; scoring after remediation is locked turns the rubric into a ratification exercise rather than a diagnostic.

## How to Use

**When to score**: After Phase 2 Segment 7 (Investor Memory Anchor Design + Reference Arc Audit) and before Segment 8 (Narrative Integrity Score + Remediation Architecture). The right moment is when you have all of: the current deck claim-by-claim, trailing-quarter data verified against deck-stated metrics, the bear-case pre-mortem written by the team and re-written by the facilitator, the split-room transcript from both halves of the leadership team, named reference customers with current relationship status, and the CEO plus CFO plus Head of Sales in the room.

**Evidence rule**: Every score requires a specific trailing-data anchor, a verifiable artifact, or a reference-customer status record. "We're confident references will confirm" is not evidence. "Reference customer #3 had its VP Ops champion replaced 6 weeks ago; the replacement has not spoken with us; deck still names that account as a flagship" is evidence. If you cannot produce evidence at the level the dimension demands, score it `3` and treat the missing data itself as a Vulnerable or Collapsed signal — a narrative that cannot survive its own evidentiary self-audit will not survive a partner's diligence team.

**Scoring discipline**: Facilitator scores independently first, playing the diligence partner. Then converges with CEO, CFO, and Head of Sales. If facilitator and team disagree by more than 1 point on any dimension, the team must produce the trailing-data or reference-status artifact that lowers the score. Default direction is conservative: when in doubt, score the higher (more collapsed) value. Founders systematically over-rate Why-We-Win and Why-Us; CFOs systematically under-rate Why-This-Price math because the multiple still works at deck numbers. Both biases push the same direction at diligence — the rubric corrects for both.

## Dimensions

### 1. Why-We-Win Claim Anchor
**What it measures**: Whether the win-story is anchored to verifiable operator, research, or category-event history versus reconstructed pitch narrative.
**Evidence required**: Why-We-Win claim verbatim from the current deck plus the underlying artifact — founder operator history with public traction, named research with citation, dated category event, or proprietary dataset with provenance — that survives a five-minute partner challenge.

- **0** — Win-story anchor is verifiable third-party, survives unaided partner challenge (named operator roles with public outcomes, named research, dated proprietary asset). Majority of founders have lived the customer's problem.
- **1** — Win-story anchor is real but one element is paraphrased or stretched; survives a 30-second challenge but a diligence team would chase the discrepancy.
- **2** — Win-story anchor leans on a real artifact but stretches it (advisor role described as operator role; customer-interview round described as primary research); fails the partner's second question.
- **3** — Win-story has no underlying artifact, or the artifact contradicts the claim, or the claim is category-creation assertion ("nobody else thinks about this") with no supporting evidence.

### 2. Why-Now Claim Survivability
**What it measures**: Whether the macro / market-timing claim survives 18-month forward scrutiny — does the wave you're claiming still exist, is it accelerating, and can you cite the dated catalysts that prove it?
**Evidence required**: Why-Now claim verbatim plus three or more dated catalysts (regulation, technology shift, buyer-behavior data, named customer-side budget reallocation) inside the last 18 months — and an answer to "what kills this thesis in 18 months." If the team cannot name what kills the thesis, score 3.

- **0** — Why-Now is anchored to 3+ dated catalysts inside 18 months, the team can name what kills the thesis, and the kill-case has been ruled out with named-source evidence. Wave is accelerating, not crested.
- **1** — Why-Now has 2 dated catalysts and a named kill-case but the kill-case is partially active; thesis still holds but a diligence team would push on the timing.
- **2** — Why-Now leans on one major catalyst, the rest of the thesis is directional, and the kill-case is acknowledged but not ruled out; partner would discount the timing claim by 12-18 months.
- **3** — Why-Now is pure category-prediction ("AI is changing everything") with no dated catalysts inside 18 months, OR a competitor has made the same Why-Now claim with stronger catalysts, OR the team cannot name what kills the thesis.

### 3. Why-Us Numerical Truth
**What it measures**: Whether claimed metrics (NRR, customer count, growth rate, gross margin, win rate) match trailing-data verified metrics computed from the actual cohort. This is the load-bearing forensic dimension — the one diligence teams check first.
**Evidence required**: Side-by-side table of every deck-stated metric vs. trailing-quarter verified metric from CFO source-of-truth data. If the gap exceeds 5 percentage points on any single metric, the dimension cannot score below 2; if it exceeds 10 points on any metric or if multiple metrics are materially misstated, the dimension scores 3.

- **0** — Deck-stated metrics match trailing-data verified metrics within 2 percentage points on every claim; methodology for each metric is published and reproducible.
- **1** — One metric is off by 3-5 points and is explainable by a defined methodology delta (e.g., logo NRR vs. gross-revenue NRR); no metric is off by more than 5 points.
- **2** — One metric is off by 5-10 points, OR methodology gaps mean a diligence team will recompute the metric and arrive at a lower number. Gap is defensible only if surfaced upfront.
- **3** — Any single metric is off by more than 10 points, OR multiple metrics are materially misstated (e.g., NRR claimed at 130% with trailing of 117% AND customer count claimed at 14/25 OEMs with active count of 11/25). Numerical truth has failed; the deck cannot be presented as-is.

### 4. Why-This-Trajectory Shape Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether the claimed growth shape (J-curve, step-function, compounding, hockey-stick) matches the actual trailing shape — and whether the underlying mechanic (e.g., expansion ARR ramp, new-segment unlock, product-led acceleration) is visible in the data.
**Evidence required**: Trailing 8-quarter revenue waterfall by new vs. expansion vs. churned ARR, with the claimed shape overlaid. If actual shape is materially different from claimed shape, dimension scores 2 minimum; if the claimed shape requires a mechanic the trailing data does not show, dimension scores 3.

- **0** — Claimed shape (J-curve, step-function, compounding) is visible in trailing 8-quarter data; the mechanic driving the shape is named and quantified; next 4 quarters are bridged with specific commitments.
- **1** — Claimed shape directionally matches trailing data; one of two driving mechanics is visible in the data, the other is forward commitment with a named anchor.
- **2** — Claimed shape diverges from trailing shape on at least one cycle (e.g., claim "J-curve about to inflect" but trailing shows linear); team has rationale but partner will discount the forward number.
- **3** — Claimed shape is incompatible with trailing data (claimed J-curve but actual is linear 4 quarters running, or claimed step-function but trailing shows compression), OR the claimed mechanic is not visible at all in the cohort math.

### 5. Why-This-Price Math
**What it measures**: Whether the asked multiple survives at trailing-data inputs — i.e., recompute the valuation case using verified trailing metrics from dimension 3 and trailing shape from dimension 4, and check whether the multiple still maps to comparable-set medians.
**Evidence required**: Comparable-set valuation table with the company's deck-input multiple and the trailing-input multiple side by side. If the trailing-input multiple lands outside the comp-set top quartile while the deck claims top-quartile positioning, dimension scores 2 minimum; if it lands below the comp-set median, dimension scores 3.

- **0** — Trailing-input multiple lands at or above the deck-claimed comp-set position; the price story is durable at verified inputs, not just at deck inputs.
- **1** — Trailing-input multiple lands one notch below deck-input (e.g., 75th percentile vs. claimed 90th); the price is still defensible but the team must surface the gap proactively.
- **2** — Trailing-input multiple lands at comp-set median or just above; the deck price requires deck-inflated inputs to clear. Partner will model trailing and counter at a lower number.
- **3** — Trailing-input multiple lands at or below comp-set median while deck claims a premium; the multiple does not survive recomputation at honest inputs. The price is fictional and the round is at risk on price alone.

### 6. Reference Customer Arc Readiness
**What it measures**: Current relationship status of every reference customer named in the deck — champion in seat, champion changed, paused, renewing, churned. References are the diligence audit; if half of named refs have champion changes or churn risk, the deck claim cannot be presented as live.
**Evidence required**: Named-reference status table with: champion name, champion seat status (in seat / changed in last 90 days / changed in last 12 months / paused / churned), last contact date, willingness-to-take-call confirmation (yes / no / unverified).

- **0** — All 5 named references have champion-in-seat, last contact inside 60 days, and explicit willingness to take a diligence call. Reference list is live and rehearsed.
- **1** — 4 of 5 references are live; one has had a champion change inside 90 days but the relationship has been rebuilt with the replacement and the replacement will take a call.
- **2** — 2 of 5 references have champion changes inside 90 days, OR 1 reference is paused / on contract risk, OR 1 reference is named in the deck but the team has not spoken to the new champion. Reference deck cannot be presented as-is without remediation.
- **3** — 3+ references have champion changes, paused contracts, or renewal-at-risk status; OR the deck lists references the team has not contacted in 6+ months; OR named flagship has actively expressed displeasure. Reference arc has collapsed.

### 7. Bear Case Honesty
**What it measures**: Completeness and intellectual honesty of the internally-written short thesis — does it name the real risks (the ones a sophisticated short would write) or is it a sanitized version that lists "execution risk" and "competition" and stops?
**Evidence required**: Bear-case document written by the team plus the facilitator-rewritten version. Compare. If the team's bear case omits any of the top 3 risks the facilitator surfaces, dimension scores 2 minimum; if the team's bear case is a list of generic risks with no quantified mechanic, dimension scores 3.

- **0** — Team-written bear case names the same top 3 risks the facilitator would write, with quantified mechanics (e.g., "if non-automotive pilot conversion stays at 38% churn, segment ARR collapses inside 2 quarters") and named mitigations. Honesty is operational, not theatrical.
- **1** — Team-written bear case names 2 of 3 top risks; one is missing or sanitized. The missing risk is the one the partner will surface, but the team is at least 80% honest with itself.
- **2** — Team-written bear case names 1 of 3 top risks; the other two are paraphrased into less concerning language ("execution risk" instead of "we have no replacement for the VP Sales who left in Q-2"). Partner will write the bear case from scratch.
- **3** — Team-written bear case is a generic list (competition, execution, market timing) with no quantified mechanic and no named risk that would surprise a board observer. The team has not internalized the short thesis.

### 8. Split-Room Coherence
**What it measures**: Whether the leadership team — split into two halves and asked to tell the company story independently and unprompted — produces a coherent single narrative, or whether the two halves tell materially different stories.
**Evidence required**: Split-room transcripts from both halves, captured in Phase 2 Segment 6. Score by counting material divergences on the 5 claim classes (Why-We-Win, Why-Now, Why-Us, Why-This-Trajectory, Why-This-Price). One divergence is tolerable; two or more is a Vulnerable signal; three or more is Collapsed.

- **0** — Both halves of the leadership team tell the same story on all 5 claim classes; word-level divergences only; the narrative is fully internalized.
- **1** — Both halves agree on 4 of 5 claim classes; one class (typically Why-This-Trajectory or Why-This-Price) shows meaningful divergence in shape or magnitude but not direction.
- **2** — Two claim classes diverge meaningfully between the halves (e.g., one half describes a J-curve trajectory, the other describes a step-function; or one half cites 130% NRR, the other cites 117%). The team has not aligned and a partner will surface the divergence in references and follow-up calls.
- **3** — Three or more claim classes diverge between halves, OR one half contradicts the deck on a load-bearing claim. The company has not aligned on its own story; the round will not survive partner cross-checks.

### 9. Investor Memory Anchor Clarity
**What it measures**: Whether the team can name, in one sentence, what the investor should remember 48 hours after the partner meeting — and whether that sentence matches the company's actual differentiated position.
**Evidence required**: Single-sentence anchor written independently by CEO, CFO, and Head of Sales. Compare. If the three sentences converge on the same anchor and the anchor maps to the company's verifiable differentiation, dimension scores 0-1. If they diverge or the anchor is generic, score climbs accordingly.

- **0** — CEO, CFO, and Head of Sales independently write the same single-sentence anchor; the anchor names a specific, defensible position ("the manufacturing data layer," "the only PI legal stack with both software and paralegal teams"). 48-hour recall is engineered.
- **1** — Anchors converge directionally but diverge on phrasing; one of the three uses a different category descriptor. Anchor is recognizable but not yet sharp enough to repeat.
- **2** — Two of three converge; the third writes a generic anchor ("enterprise SaaS for X market"). The anchor is fragile — the investor will remember a competitor's anchor instead.
- **3** — All three sentences diverge, OR the anchor is a category claim with no specific differentiator, OR the team cannot produce a one-sentence anchor in 60 seconds. The investor will forget you inside 48 hours.

### 10. QoE Pre-Mortem Coverage
**What it measures**: Whether the team has run a Quality of Earnings pre-mortem — anticipating what a QoE auditor will find when they recompute revenue recognition, customer concentration, expansion-vs-new ARR split, churn definition, and adjusted EBITDA — and addressed those findings proactively.
**Evidence required**: QoE pre-mortem checklist with: revenue recognition methodology, customer concentration table (top 5, top 10, top 20 as % of ARR), expansion-vs-new ARR split with methodology, churn definition (gross / net / logo / dollar) with cohort math, and adjusted EBITDA bridge. If any line is unaddressed, dimension scores 2 minimum.

- **0** — Full QoE pre-mortem complete; all five areas have documented methodology, the team can defend each definition, and any aggressive accounting choice has been surfaced with rationale and conservative-case impact.
- **1** — QoE pre-mortem covers 4 of 5 areas; one area (typically adjusted EBITDA bridge) is documented but not stress-tested against an auditor's recompute.
- **2** — QoE pre-mortem covers 2-3 areas; revenue recognition and customer concentration are addressed but expansion-vs-new ARR or churn methodology has not been pre-mortem'd. A QoE finding is likely.
- **3** — No QoE pre-mortem exists, OR a known accounting aggression has not been surfaced (e.g., capitalized commissions stretched, multi-year prepayments booked as ARR, expansion from price increases counted as net new). The QoE will find what the team did not find first.

## Worked Example: Aperture
**Industrial QA SaaS, $42M ARR, Series B, 10-12 months to Series C. The team has been working from a fundraise narrative drafted at the Series B close 22 months ago. Triggered by a friendly partner at a target firm flagging that "the numbers in your last update don't reconcile with what we'd model from your category." Five named reference customers in the deck. Pre-engagement 5-Layer Narrative Integrity Score was 14/25 — borderline Fragile / Collapsed on the parent skill's coarser scale.**

The audit was triggered by three signals arriving in the same week: (1) the friendly partner's reconciliation flag, (2) two of five named reference customers had their champions replaced inside 90 days without the team noticing, and (3) the CFO's trailing-quarter recompute showed NRR at 117% against a deck claim of 130%. The CEO commissioned the forensics audit 11 months from planned Series C kickoff.

| # | Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Why-We-Win Claim Anchor | 0 | 4 of 5 founders are former manufacturing operators at named OEMs (two at Tier-1 automotive suppliers, two at industrial controls vendors). Win-story anchor is verifiable, third-party-checkable, and survives the partner's unaided five-minute challenge. The category-event date (post-pandemic capex unlock for QA automation) is also defensible. |
| 2 | Why-Now Claim Survivability | 1 | Why-Now cites three dated catalysts (post-pandemic QA-automation capex unlock, regulatory shift in industrial reporting, generative-AI-driven anomaly detection cost collapse). All three real. Kill-case named: "AI-enabled visual QA from hyperscaler vendors commoditizes the category in 18 months." Kill-case is partially active — one hyperscaler announced a competing offering 6 weeks ago. Lands at 1, not 0. |
| 3 | Why-Us Numerical Truth | 3 | Deck claims NRR 130%; trailing-quarter verified NRR is 117% — gap of 13 points. Deck claims 14/25 named OEMs active; trailing reality is 11/25 active (3 paused). Deck claims +95% growth YoY; trailing-quarter computed YoY is +68%. Two claims off by >10 points; one off by 27 points. Numerical truth has failed materially on the load-bearing diligence dimension. |
| 4 | Why-This-Trajectory Shape Coherence | 3 | Deck claims J-curve inflection underway with step-function pending. Trailing 8-quarter revenue waterfall shows linear growth, not J-curve. Expansion ARR has flatlined the last 3 quarters. The claimed mechanic — non-automotive segment unlock — is showing 38% pilot churn, the opposite of step-function unlock. Trajectory shape is incompatible with trailing data. |
| 5 | Why-This-Price Math | 3 | Deck targets top-quartile vertical-SaaS multiple (~12-14x forward ARR) based on deck-input metrics. Recomputing with trailing-input metrics from dimension 3 (NRR 117%, +68% growth, 11/25 logo concentration) drops the multiple to comp-set median (~7-9x). The price story collapses at honest inputs — the round is at risk on price alone. |
| 6 | Reference Customer Arc Readiness | 2 | 5 named references in deck. 2 have champion changes inside 90 days (the team had not contacted either replacement). 1 renewal pending on a 6-month notice with a contract risk flag. 1 paused contract (treated as "active pilot" in deck commentary). 1 healthy reference with engaged champion. Reference arc cannot be presented as-is; requires 60-90 days of remediation. Lands at 2 (the threshold is met) but on the cusp of 3. |
| 7 | Bear Case Honesty | 1 | Team-written bear case names 2 of 3 facilitator-surfaced risks (non-automotive pilot conversion, NRR slippage). Misses the third (hyperscaler commoditization is sanitized into "platform competition"). Bear case is 80% honest with itself but the missing risk is the one the partner will surface in 30 seconds. |
| 8 | Split-Room Coherence | 1 | Both halves of leadership team agreed on Why-We-Win, Why-Now, Why-Us numerical baseline, and Why-This-Price framing. Diverged on Why-This-Trajectory: one half described "J-curve about to inflect," the other described "we're rebuilding the non-automotive motion and the next 2 quarters will be flat-to-up." Direction was the same; magnitude and shape diverged. One claim class diverged — lands at 1, not 0. |
| 9 | Investor Memory Anchor Clarity | 0 | CEO, CFO, and Head of Sales independently wrote "the manufacturing data layer" as the 48-hour anchor. All three sentences converged on phrasing within paraphrase tolerance. The anchor maps to a defensible position (proprietary integrations across 11 OEMs and 7 industrial-controls vendors) and is sharp enough to repeat. Engineered memory anchor is in place. |
| 10 | QoE Pre-Mortem Coverage | 0 | Full QoE pre-mortem complete. Revenue recognition methodology published and matches GAAP. Customer concentration table addressed (top 5 = 31% of ARR, surfaced with rationale). Expansion-vs-new ARR split documented with methodology. Churn defined as logo-and-dollar net with cohort math. Adjusted EBITDA bridge stress-tested against an auditor's recompute. No accounting aggression. Engineered the QoE finding before the QoE engagement begins. |

**Math check**: 0 + 1 + 3 + 3 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 1 + 0 + 0 = 14.

**Total: 14 / 30**
**Tier: Vulnerable — cracks present; rebuild before partner meeting.**

The composite hides a more aggressive truth: dimensions 3, 4, and 5 — Why-Us numerical truth, Why-This-Trajectory shape, and Why-This-Price math — are all at 3 simultaneously. That triplet is the signature of a Vulnerable narrative one diligence team away from Collapsed. Aperture's win-story (dimension 1), memory anchor (dimension 9), and QoE pre-mortem (dimension 10) are in good shape. The forensic failure is concentrated in the load-bearing numerical and trajectory triple: the metrics are misstated, the shape doesn't match the math, and the price doesn't survive recomputation. Until those three are rebuilt, the team cannot enter a partner meeting.

**Facilitator convention on dimensions 9 and 10**: Aperture entered the engagement having already done the memory-anchor exercise and the QoE pre-mortem as part of pre-work. Scoring those at 0 here is honest — the work was done. In a typical engagement where the company has not done that work, both dimensions score 2-3 at intake and are re-scored after remediation. The composite movement is the deliverable.

## Composite Score → Tier Mapping

| Composite | Tier | Interpretation | Diligence Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-9 | **Defensible** | Story survives partner reference calls and a QoE pre-mortem. Numerical truth, trajectory shape, and price math all hold at trailing inputs. | Proceed to partner meetings; keep 60-day re-score and reference-arc refresh. |
| 10-20 | **Vulnerable** | Cracks present; specific claim classes will fail forensic check. Rebuild before partner meeting or accept a valuation discount. | Rebuild deck, reference arc, and bear-case prep over 60-90 days before partner meetings begin. |
| 21-30 | **Collapsed** | Multiple claim classes fail at trailing inputs. Round is at risk on numerical truth or price math alone; references will not confirm; bear case is sanitized. | Full narrative reset required; delay partner meetings if possible. Going to market with a Collapsed score is round-death risk. |

**Borderline rule**: A composite within 2 points of the next tier band, with any one of dimensions 3, 4, 5, or 6 at score 3, classifies at the higher (more collapsed) tier. Why-Us numerical truth, Why-This-Trajectory shape, Why-This-Price math, and Reference Customer Arc are the load-bearing forensic dimensions — a single 3 on any of those is more diagnostic than a clean composite.

**Collapse override**: If any three of dimensions 3, 4, 5, 6, and 8 are simultaneously at 3, classify Collapsed minimum regardless of composite. Those five dimensions measure whether the numbers are true, whether the shape matches the math, whether the price survives recomputation, whether references will confirm, and whether the team tells the same story unprompted. Three failing at once is the signature of a collapsed investor narrative, not a vulnerable one. Aperture is one dimension away from this override firing — dimensions 3, 4, and 5 are at 3; dimensions 6 and 8 are at 2 and 1 respectively. If reference arc deteriorates one more notch before the round opens, Aperture moves from Vulnerable to Collapsed without a composite change large enough to flag it.

## Notes for Facilitators

- **Anchor every score to a trailing-data artifact, a verifiable bio, or a reference-status record.** A rubric without trailing-quarter recomputation and named-reference status is theater. The CFO is the only one who can confirm what trailing data says; the Head of Sales is the only one who knows reference-customer relationship status. Score in the room with both present.
- **Score conservatively when data is missing.** Absent data is itself a Vulnerable signal: a company that cannot recompute its own NRR, cannot name what kills its Why-Now thesis, or cannot produce a current reference-status table is, by construction, not narrative-ready for diligence.
- **Refuse to round down on deck conviction.** When the CEO pushes back on dimensions 3, 4, or 5 ("we know the numbers"), ask for the CFO-source-of-truth recompute that lowers the score. If the recompute matches deck-stated metrics within 2 points, the score drops. Deck conviction is not evidence; CFO recompute is evidence.
- **Score in the room with CFO and Head of Sales present.** A score without the CFO is a CEO narrative; a score without the Head of Sales is a finance narrative. The forensic audit needs both — the numerical truth dimension belongs to the CFO and the reference arc dimension belongs to the Head of Sales.
- **Tier classification overrides composite when load-bearing dimensions fail.** The collapse override is not a tiebreaker — it is the primary instruction when three of dimensions 3, 4, 5, 6, and 8 are at 3.
- **Re-score 60 days after engagement and 30 days before partner meetings.** A one-time rubric is a diagnostic. A re-scored rubric is a narrative-due-diligence governance capability. Companies that hold tier into partner meetings repeat the score on a documented cadence.
- **The rubric is not the deliverable.** The deliverable is the rebuilt narrative, the rehearsed reference arc, and the bear-case-ready FAQ the rubric justifies. If you finish scoring and the CEO cannot deliver the rebuilt narrative cold to a partner-simulated cross-examination, you stopped too early.
