# Competitive Narrative Stress Test Scoring Rubric

A 10-dimension diagnostic measuring narrative attack-surface durability. Score each 0-3. Total 0-30. Composite maps to Attack-Surface Durability tier (Holding / Fragile / Collapsed).

This rubric is the spine of every competitive-narrative-stress-test run. Use it after Phase 2 Segments 3-5 (Attack Surface Mapping → Blank Check → Competitor's Best Day) so each dimension is anchored to a competitor verbatim, a lost-deal artifact, or a piece of proof — not to facilitator instinct.

## How to Use

**When to score**: After Phase 2 Segment 5 (Competitor's Best Day Simulation) and before Segment 6 (Narrative Reconstruction). Scoring earlier means scoring vibes — the team hasn't been hit with the competitor's best pitch yet, so cracks read as fine cracks instead of fault lines. Scoring later means the rebuild is already underway and the rubric becomes a ratification exercise rather than a diagnostic. The right moment is when you have all of: verbatim competitor language for top 3 competitors, 5 lost-deal post-mortems with competitor reasons quoted, your current narrative captured layer-by-layer, and the participant team having just absorbed the competitor's strongest 60-second attack live in the room.

**Evidence rule**: Every score requires a specific artifact — a competitor verbatim, a lost-deal quote, a piece of proof material, an analyst note, or a recorded oral delivery. "We feel good about our origin story" is not evidence. "Competitor's homepage hero claim verbatim, two analyst quotes from the last cycle, and three lost-deal post-mortems naming the same gap" is evidence. If you cannot produce an artifact at the level the dimension demands, score it `3` and treat the missing artifact as a Fragile or Collapsed signal — a competitive narrative without supporting evidence is, by definition, not surviving scrutiny.

**Scoring discipline**: Facilitator scores independently first (playing competitor), then converges with CEO, Head of Marketing, and Head of Sales. If facilitator and participant disagree by more than 1 point, the participant must produce the artifact that pulls the score down. Default direction is conservative: when in doubt, score the higher (more collapsed) value. This counteracts founder attachment to the origin story and marketing attachment to the differentiation claim.

## Dimensions

### 1. Origin Claim Defensibility
**What it measures**: Whether the founder/origin claim is anchored to verifiable history (named operator experience, named research, named market event) versus reconstructed marketing.
**Evidence required**: Origin claim verbatim plus the underlying artifact (founder bio, research citation, prior-company outcome, public market data) that survives a five-minute analyst challenge.

- **0** — Origin claim is anchored to a verifiable, third-party-checkable artifact (named operator role with public traction, named research, dated market event); claim and artifact align without spin.
- **1** — Origin claim is largely defensible; one element is paraphrased or stretched but the underlying artifact still survives a 30-second challenge.
- **2** — Origin claim leans on a real artifact but stretches it (e.g., "operator experience" was an advisor role; "research" was a customer interview round); fails an analyst's second question.
- **3** — Origin claim has no underlying artifact, or the artifact contradicts the claim, or the claim is a category-creation assertion ("we're the only ones thinking about this") with no supporting evidence.

### 2. Differentiation Claim Anchor Strength
**What it measures**: Whether the primary differentiation claim is anchored to a measurable capability gap (feature, dataset, integration, methodology, time-to-value) versus adjectives.
**Evidence required**: Differentiation claim verbatim plus the proof anchor — a benchmark, a feature comparison, a customer-time-to-value number, or a competitor admission. If no anchor exists, score 3.

- **0** — Differentiation claim cites a quantified anchor (benchmark, integration count, time-to-value gap, named methodology) that competitors cannot produce or cannot match within 12 months.
- **1** — Differentiation claim cites a real anchor but the anchor is partially matched by one competitor; the lead is real but narrowing.
- **2** — Differentiation claim uses an anchor that is either old (>12 months without refresh) or matched by 2+ competitors; the claim is no longer load-bearing.
- **3** — Differentiation claim is adjective-only ("enterprise-grade," "AI-powered," "best-in-class") with no underlying capability gap, or the claimed anchor has been publicly matched by a competitor.

### 3. Proof Claim Freshness
**What it measures**: Recency and variety of customer proof points (case studies, named logos, outcome data) supporting the narrative — and whether the proof is concentrated in one customer cohort or spread across ICP.
**Evidence required**: Inventory of published proof artifacts with publish dates and customer segments represented. If no inventory exists, score 3.

- **0** — 5+ published proof points in the last 12 months across at least 3 ICP segments; outcomes are quantified and recent.
- **1** — 3-4 published proof points in the last 12 months; outcomes mostly quantified; ICP coverage adequate but uneven.
- **2** — 1-2 proof points refreshed in the last 12 months OR a stack of older case studies (>12 months) that no longer cite current product/pricing; ICP coverage thin.
- **3** — No proof point refreshed inside 12 months, or all proof points come from a single customer / single segment, or proof claims rest on logos without quantified outcomes.

### 4. Outcome Claim Methodology
**What it measures**: Whether the outcome claim (ROI, time-to-value, productivity gain, revenue lift) is backed by a published, repeatable methodology versus a single customer anecdote extrapolated to a market claim.
**Evidence required**: Published methodology document, third-party validator, or auditable measurement framework. Customer-testimonial sourcing alone scores 2 minimum.

- **0** — Outcome claim has a published methodology with named formula, baseline definition, and a third-party validator (analyst, audit firm, customer-side CFO sign-off).
- **1** — Outcome methodology is documented internally and used consistently across 5+ customer rollouts; no third-party validator yet, but reproducibility is real.
- **2** — Outcome claim cites real customers but no formal methodology — the number comes from sales-recap math that varies deal to deal; analyst would push back on the calculation basis.
- **3** — Outcome claim has no methodology — the number is a single customer's reported result generalized to all customers, or the calculation basis is not disclosable.

### 5. Future Claim Survivability
**What it measures**: Whether the future/vision claim survives the buyer's "okay, but how" question — does it have a near-term proof point, or is it pure aspiration?
**Evidence required**: Future claim verbatim plus the named-and-dated near-term proof commitment (next-quarter release, named partnership, signed pilot, public roadmap milestone). If no proof commitment exists, score 3.

- **0** — Future claim is anchored to a near-term, named proof commitment (dated release, signed customer pilot, named partnership) that the buyer can verify in 90 days.
- **1** — Future claim has a directional proof commitment (roadmap published, but no signed pilot or named partnership yet); survives one "how" but not two.
- **2** — Future claim is aspirational with a soft commitment (e.g., "we're investing heavily here") and collapses on the second buyer question; analyst would skip it in coverage.
- **3** — Future claim is pure category prediction ("the future of X is Y") with no proof commitment, OR a competitor has made the same future claim with a stronger proof anchor (signed pilot, named launch).

### 6. Competitor Verbatim Coverage
**What it measures**: Whether the team has captured competitor language verbatim — homepage hero, pricing page, sales-deck claims, analyst-briefing transcripts — for the top 3 competitors.
**Evidence required**: Verbatim capture document with quotes from each top-3 competitor's website, sales materials, and most recent analyst-cycle positioning. Paraphrased summaries do not qualify.

- **0** — Verbatim language captured for top 3 competitors across at least 3 surfaces each (homepage, pricing, sales deck OR analyst briefing); dated within 90 days.
- **1** — Verbatim captured for top 3 but only across 1-2 surfaces; or capture is 90-180 days old.
- **2** — Verbatim captured for top 1-2 competitors only, or the team relies on paraphrased summaries for the rest; capture is >6 months old.
- **3** — No verbatim capture exists, or competitor positioning is reconstructed from sales-rep memory. Team cannot quote a competitor's homepage hero claim.

### 7. 60-Second Oral Delivery
**What it measures**: Whether the CEO (or designated narrator) can deliver the current narrative cold, on a timer, under cross-examination pressure, without notes — and whether the version delivered matches the version on the website.
**Evidence required**: Recorded 60-second oral delivery with facilitator interruption at 30 seconds. Compared line-by-line to the published narrative. Live test in the room.

- **0** — Delivery is fluent, finishes inside 60 seconds, recovers from interruption, and matches the published narrative within paraphrase tolerance.
- **1** — Delivery is fluent but stumbles on one layer (usually outcome or future); recovers; minor drift from the published version.
- **2** — Delivery exceeds 60 seconds OR diverges meaningfully from the published narrative on at least one layer; CEO is selling a different story than the website.
- **3** — CEO cannot deliver the narrative without notes, or delivery contradicts the published version on a load-bearing layer (origin, differentiation, outcome), or the team narrates three different stories when asked individually.

### 8. Sales Enablement Penetration
**What it measures**: Whether sales reps actually use the official narrative in live pipeline conversations — or whether they freelance because the official version doesn't fit deal reality.
**Evidence required**: Review of 5 recent recorded sales calls (Gong, Chorus, internal recordings) checking whether reps lead with the official narrative or with a freelanced version. Plus rep-side admission of which version they trust.

- **0** — 4 of 5 sampled calls open with the official narrative; reps cite specific official talking points; freelance language is rare and addresses an edge-case ICP.
- **1** — 3 of 5 sampled calls use the official narrative; reps blend official + freelance language; freelance versions are convergent (reps freelance the same way).
- **2** — 2 of 5 calls use the official narrative; reps freelance widely; freelance versions are divergent (no two reps say the same thing); enablement materials are stale (>90 days).
- **3** — Reps abandon the official narrative entirely in live calls, or there is no sales-enablement material tied to the current narrative, or marketing and sales narrate different stories to the same prospect inside the same week.

### 9. Analyst Language Match
**What it measures**: Whether the current narrative uses the language that category analysts are using this cycle — or whether you're still using last cycle's vocabulary while analysts have moved on.
**Evidence required**: Two most recent analyst notes / category reports covering your space, compared line-by-line against your homepage hero, pitch deck, and sales narrative. Note the analyst's current framing terms vs. yours.

- **0** — Current narrative uses 80%+ of the analyst's current framing vocabulary; your category descriptor matches the analyst's category descriptor; analysts have quoted your narrative in coverage.
- **1** — Vocabulary overlap is 50-79%; one major framing term is out of date; analyst has covered you but not quoted your narrative directly.
- **2** — Vocabulary overlap is 25-49%; your category descriptor is one cycle behind (e.g., still "platform" while analysts have moved to "agent layer"); analyst coverage describes you using competitor framing.
- **3** — Vocabulary overlap is <25%, or analysts describe you in a category you do not claim, or the team cannot name the current analyst framing for your space.

### 10. Reconstruction Discipline
**What it measures**: Whether the company stress-tests and rebuilds its narrative on a documented cadence — or whether the current narrative is the same one that was written when the website was redesigned 18 months ago.
**Evidence required**: Written stress-test history with dates, scored layers, and rebuild decisions over the last 24 months. If no history exists, score 3.

- **0** — Narrative has been stress-tested at least twice in the last 24 months, with documented score deltas and a rebuild log per layer; next stress test is on the calendar.
- **1** — Stress-tested once in 24 months; rebuild was partial; no next test scheduled.
- **2** — Stress-test history is informal (one workshop, no scoring, no per-layer rebuild log); the narrative is treated as a marketing asset rather than a competitive instrument.
- **3** — Never stress-tested; current narrative dates to a marketing redesign, a fundraise, or a launch and has not been rebuilt since competitive landscape shifted.

## Worked Example: Tessera
**Retail analytics SaaS, $14M ARR, 6-8 months from Series B. Three competitors active: NewEntrant (agent-layer pitch, well-funded), LegacyVendor (enterprise sales motion, 10-year incumbency), and InHouse (customer-side build teams). Attack-Surface Durability pre-engagement (5-Layer scoring from the parent skill): Origin 5/5 (strong), Differentiation 2/5 (weak), Proof 3/5 (mid), Outcome 2/5 (weak), Future 1/5 (collapsed). Composite 13/25 — Fragile, borderline Collapsed.**

The audit was triggered by three consecutive losses to NewEntrant in deals where Tessera had been the incumbent vendor. Lost-deal post-mortems quoted the prospect saying: "Tessera's dashboards are waiting to be agents — NewEntrant is already there." That verbatim is the entire trigger.

| # | Dimension | Score | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Origin Claim Defensibility | 1 | Founders are ex-retail-ops operators at two named brands; origin claim is defensible. One small stretch ("we ran the analytics function" — actually one founder ran ops, the other was a data lead) but the underlying artifact survives a 30-second challenge. |
| 2 | Differentiation Claim Anchor Strength | 2 | Claim is "purpose-built for retail" with an anchor in retail-specific data integrations (24 named retail systems). Real anchor, but NewEntrant has matched 18 of 24 in the last 9 months. Lead is real but narrowing inside 12 months. |
| 3 | Proof Claim Freshness | 2 | Two case studies refreshed in the last 12 months; both are mid-market brands. Five older case studies (>12 months) cite a prior product version and prior pricing. ICP coverage is thin — no enterprise case study, no apparel-vertical case study despite 30% of pipeline. |
| 4 | Outcome Claim Methodology | 3 | Outcome claim is "average 28% lift in promotional ROI." Number comes from one customer's reported result extrapolated to all customers. No published methodology, no third-party validator, no consistent calculation basis across customer rollouts. Analyst would push back on the extrapolation. |
| 5 | Future Claim Survivability | 3 | Future claim is "Tessera is building the data layer retail intelligence will run on." No near-term proof commitment — no signed pilot, no named partnership, no dated milestone. NewEntrant has made the equivalent claim with a signed pilot at a top-5 specialty retailer announced last quarter. Future claim has been beaten on its own ground. |
| 6 | Competitor Verbatim Coverage | 1 | Verbatim captured for NewEntrant (homepage, recent fundraise announcement, conference keynote transcript) across 3 surfaces, dated within 60 days. LegacyVendor coverage is one surface, dated 4 months ago. InHouse coverage is paraphrased — no actual buyer-side language captured. |
| 7 | 60-Second Oral Delivery | 2 | CEO delivered the narrative in 78 seconds (exceeded the timer). Stumbled on the outcome layer (recovered) and diverged from the website on the future claim — said "we're becoming an agent platform" while the website still leads with "the leading retail analytics platform." CEO is selling a different story than the website. |
| 8 | Sales Enablement Penetration | 1 | 3 of 5 sampled calls used the official narrative; reps blended official differentiation language with freelanced outcome language; freelance versions were convergent (reps consistently said "roughly 25-30% lift" rather than the official 28%). Enablement deck was last refreshed 4 months ago. |
| 9 | Analyst Language Match | 2 | Two recent analyst notes (Forrester, IDC) describe the space as "agent-enabled retail data layer." Tessera's homepage still leads with "AI-powered retail analytics platform." Vocabulary overlap roughly 35%; category descriptor is one cycle behind. Analysts have covered Tessera using NewEntrant's framing. |
| 10 | Reconstruction Discipline | 0 | No prior stress test; current narrative dates to the Series A fundraise narrative deck 19 months ago. The absence of reconstruction discipline is the trigger that this engagement exists — re-score honestly at 90 days. |

**Math check**: 1 + 2 + 2 + 3 + 3 + 1 + 2 + 1 + 2 + 0 = 17.

**Total: 17 / 30**
**Tier: Fragile, borderline Collapsed — rebuild required before next competitive cycle.**

The composite hides the more aggressive truth: dimensions 4, 5, and 9 — outcome methodology, future claim, and analyst language match — are the three layers NewEntrant is winning on. The competitor isn't beating Tessera on differentiation (Tessera still has the retail integrations lead); it's beating Tessera on the framing of the future and the methodology of the present. Tessera's narrative has not been rebuilt since the analyst category vocabulary moved, and the outcome claim has not been hardened against diligence-grade methodology challenge.

**Facilitator convention on dimension 10**: When a company enters the engagement without a documented reconstruction history (which is most of them), dimension 10 is scored 0 at intake, not 3. The absence of the artifact is the trigger that the engagement itself is the repair — counting it against the company would double-charge a finding that is already implicit in the other nine dimensions. Re-score dimension 10 honestly at the 90-day re-score: at that point, the company either has the documented rebuild log (0-1) or has not done the work (3).

## Composite Score → Tier Mapping

| Composite | Tier | Interpretation | Competitive Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-9 | **Holding** | Story survives competitor's best attack across all 5 layers. | Maintain; keep quarterly re-score and analyst-cycle vocabulary refresh. |
| 10-20 | **Fragile** | Cracks under pressure; specific layers are losing deals. | Rebuild before next competitive cycle or analyst briefing window. |
| 21-30 | **Collapsed** | Narrative actively losing deals; multiple layers fail trailing test. | Emergency rebuild — assume continued deal losses and analyst downgrades until reconstructed narrative is in market. |

**Borderline rule**: A composite within 2 points of the next tier band, with any one of dimensions 2, 4, 5, or 9 at score 3, classifies at the higher (more collapsed) tier. Differentiation, outcome, future, and analyst language are the load-bearing layers in any competitive narrative — a single 3 on any of those is more diagnostic than a clean composite.

**Concentration override**: If any three of dimensions 2, 4, 5, 6, and 9 are all 3, classify Collapsed minimum regardless of composite. Those five dimensions measure whether the differentiation anchor holds, whether the outcome methodology is defensible, whether the future claim has proof, whether competitor language is captured, and whether the narrative matches analyst vocabulary. Three failing simultaneously is the signature of a collapsed competitive story, not a fragile one.

## Notes for Facilitators

- **Anchor every score to a verbatim artifact.** A rubric without verbatim competitor language or lost-deal quotes is theater. The competitor is the only one who decides whether your narrative survives — score using their words, not yours.
- **Score conservatively when artifacts are missing.** Absent artifacts are themselves a Fragile signal: a competitive function that cannot quote its competitors is, by construction, narrating a story it cannot defend in the deal.
- **Refuse to round down on founder attachment.** When a CEO pushes back on the origin or differentiation score ("we know what we built"), ask for the analyst quote or competitor verbatim that would lower the score. If they cannot produce it, the score holds. Founder conviction is not evidence.
- **Score in the room with sales leader present.** A score without the sales leader is a marketing narrative. The sales leader is the only one who knows which version reps actually use and why prospects actually walked.
- **Tier classification overrides composite when load-bearing dimensions fail.** The concentration override is not a tiebreaker — it is the primary instruction when 2, 4, 5, or 9 are at 3.
- **Re-score 90 days after engagement.** A one-time rubric is a diagnostic. A re-scored rubric is a competitive-narrative governance capability. Companies that hold tier under competitive pressure repeat the score.
- **The rubric is not the deliverable.** The deliverable is the reconstructed narrative, the per-competitor card set, and the 60-second oral delivery the rubric justifies. If you finish scoring and the CEO cannot deliver the reconstructed narrative cold under cross-examination, you stopped too early.
