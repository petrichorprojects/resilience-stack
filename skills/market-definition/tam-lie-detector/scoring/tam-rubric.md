# TAM Lie Detector — Scoring Rubric

## Overview
All scores use evidence from the participant's own responses. Never assign a score without citing the specific answer that drove it. The TAM Integrity Score is the sum of all 8 dimension scores (maximum 80).

---

## Dimension 1: Source Integrity (1-10)
Phase 2 — TAM Archaeology Dig

How traceable, accurate, and honestly applied is the original TAM source?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Fully traceable and honest.** Source read by the team. Market definition matches the company. Caveats preserved on the slide. No number inflation through citation layers. | "I read the original IDC report. We only operate in the segments they define as [X], so we applied their methodology directly to our market." |
| 7-8 | **Mostly traceable.** Source identified and partially read. Minor definition gaps. Most caveats still present. At most one layer of citation drift. | "We got the report summary from our bank. The definition is close but includes enterprise accounts we don't really sell to — maybe 20% of the number." |
| 5-6 | **Partially traceable.** Source known but not read by current team. Meaningful definition gaps — the company serves a subset of what the original report covers. Caveats partially stripped. | "That number came from a Gartner report our previous CFO sourced in 2021. We've never gone back to the original." |
| 3-4 | **Barely traceable.** Source identity is approximate. Definition clearly doesn't match the company. Caveats entirely absent. Number has visibly grown through citation. | "I think it came from a Forrester study somewhere. It was in the deck when I joined." |
| 1-2 | **Untraceable.** Nobody can name the original source. Market definition has nothing to do with the company's actual customer. Number grew through 4+ citation layers. Caveats do not exist. | "The $12B number has been in every deck since Series A. I have no idea where it came from originally." |

## Dimension 2: Customer Ceiling Reality (1-10)
Phase 3 — 1,000-Customer Ceiling Test

How close is the bottom-up TAM (customer profile x reachable companies x ACV) to the stated TAM?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Grounded.** Bottom-up TAM within 2x of investor TAM. Company profile, reachable count, and ACV produce a number that supports the slide. | Bottom-up calculation yields $450M against a stated TAM of $500M. Methodology documented. |
| 7-8 | **Minor gap.** 2-3x divergence. Explainable — adjacent segments or geographic expansion in progress could close the gap within 18 months. | Bottom-up yields $300M against $700M stated. "We're expanding to Europe next year which adds ~$200M." |
| 5-6 | **Significant gap.** 3-5x divergence. Stated TAM includes segments the company does not serve and has no near-term plan to serve. | Bottom-up yields $150M against $600M stated. "The other $450M is segments we'd like to enter eventually." |
| 3-4 | **Large gap.** 5-10x divergence. The sales motion, customer profile, and ACV simply cannot reach the stated TAM at any reasonable growth rate. | Bottom-up yields $80M against $600M stated. "We're targeting mid-market, but the TAM includes enterprise." |
| 1-2 | **Fictional gap.** 10x+ divergence. The investor TAM describes a market the company has no path to reach with its current product, pricing, and sales motion. | Bottom-up yields $30M against $800M stated. Team cannot explain where the other 97% comes from. |

## Dimension 3: Willingness-to-Pay (1-10)
Phase 4 — Willingness-to-Pay Haircut

After three sequential buying filters (affordability, organizational capability, problem acuity), how much of the stated TAM survives?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Market is ready.** Combined haircut removes less than 30% of the bottom-up TAM. Budget availability is high, buying process is accessible, problem is acute across the segment. | "80% can afford it, 85% have identifiable buyers, 75% consider it a top-3 priority. Haircut TAM is $320M on a $400M bottom-up." |
| 7-8 | **Moderate friction.** Haircut removes 30-50%. One filter (usually affordability or problem acuity) creates material reduction, but the other two are reasonably healthy. | "Price is the main barrier — only 50% have budget. But those who can buy, can actually buy, and the problem is urgent." |
| 5-6 | **Meaningful friction.** Haircut removes 50-70%. Two filters apply significant pressure. The company is serving only a fraction of the segment that nominally looks like its market. | "Budget is tight in mid-market, procurement is complex, and only about a third consider this a top priority this year." |
| 3-4 | **High friction.** Haircut removes 70-85%. Each filter individually reasonable but the compound effect leaves less than 20% of the bottom-up TAM as actually purchasable. | "After all three filters, we're at $40M on a $250M bottom-up." |
| 1-2 | **Market is not ready.** Haircut removes 85%+. The vast majority of the stated TAM cannot or will not buy at the current price point, with the current buying process, for a problem they don't prioritize. | "Less than 10% of our bottom-up TAM survives all three filters. The $6B on our slide survives nothing." |

## Dimension 4: Competitive Market Floor (1-10)
Phase 5 — Competitive Absorption Audit

How does total adjusted competitive revenue compare to the stated TAM? Does the empirical floor validate the TAM's scale?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Strong empirical grounding.** Total competitive revenue (adjusted for market overlap) is 20%+ of the stated TAM. Mature market — large portion is already being captured by someone. | Competitive floor of $1.5B against a $5B stated TAM. "The market is real — there are five companies with meaningful revenue." |
| 7-8 | **Reasonable grounding.** Competitive revenue is 10-20% of stated TAM. Market is real, early-to-mid stage. Gap between floor and TAM is plausible given market maturity. | Competitive floor of $400M against $3B stated TAM. Emerging category with active competitive field. |
| 5-6 | **Thin grounding.** Competitive revenue is 5-10% of stated TAM. Market may be genuinely nascent, or TAM definition is broader than the actual competitive universe. | Competitive floor of $150M against $2B stated TAM. "The market is early." |
| 3-4 | **Weak grounding.** Competitive revenue is 2-5% of stated TAM. Very difficult to explain why 95%+ is unserved by anyone if the opportunity is as large as claimed. | Competitive floor of $80M against $3B stated TAM. No good explanation for the gap. |
| 1-2 | **No empirical basis.** Competitive revenue is less than 2% of stated TAM, or the team cannot estimate competitor revenue at all. TAM is either wildly overstated or the market intelligence is absent. | "We don't really know what our competitors make." Empirical floor essentially zero against a $5B stated TAM. |

## Dimension 5: Churn Intelligence (1-10)
Phase 5 — Churn Forensics Reversal

Does churn data confirm or contradict TAM boundaries? What does the reason for churn reveal about who is and isn't in the real addressable market?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Churn confirms market.** Churn is primarily competitive (60%+). Customers had the problem, had the budget, made a buying decision — and chose someone else. High competitive churn = the market is real. | "70% of churn is competitive. We're losing to [Competitor] on price and [Competitor] on integrations." |
| 7-8 | **Mostly confirmatory.** Competitive and timing churn dominate. Product-fit and problem-evaporation are minor (under 20% combined). TAM boundaries are largely validated. | "60% competitive, 25% timing, 15% product-fit. The product-fit is concentrated in the SMB segment we're pulling back from." |
| 5-6 | **Mixed signals.** Product-fit and problem-evaporation churn represent 20-35% of total. TAM includes a meaningful segment the company cannot serve or that doesn't have a persistent problem. | "About a third of our churn is companies that tried us and found the problem wasn't big enough to justify the spend." |
| 3-4 | **Contradictory.** Product-fit and problem-evaporation represent 35-60% of churn. The company is systematically acquiring customers outside its real market. TAM definition is too broad. | "We've had a lot of churn in the healthcare segment — the workflows just don't fit. But healthcare is in our TAM." |
| 1-2 | **Churn contradicts TAM.** Product-fit dominates (60%+). The company is spending acquisition resources on customers who were never in its real market. Every product-fit churn is a TAM boundary failure. No churn categorization exists — the team is flying blind. | "We track overall churn rate but not the reasons. We've had high churn but don't know why." |

## Dimension 6: Narrative Consistency (1-10)
Phase 6 — Fundraise vs. Operate Divergence Test

How large is the gap between the TAM the company presents to investors and the market size implied by its sales operations plan?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Aligned.** Fundraise TAM and operations-implied market size are within 3:1. The investor narrative and the operational reality tell the same story at different levels of abstraction. | Fundraise TAM: $500M. Operations implied: $200M. "The difference is the segments we're not yet selling into." |
| 7-8 | **Minor divergence.** Ratio 3-5:1. Explainable — investor TAM includes adjacent segments or geographies not yet in the sales plan but with a credible entry path. | Fundraise TAM: $1B. Operations implied: $250M. "The extra $750M is EMEA, which we're entering in Q3." |
| 5-6 | **Meaningful divergence.** Ratio 5-10:1. Investor narrative includes significant market the operations team is not targeting and has no near-term plan to target. | Fundraise TAM: $3B. Operations implied: $400M. "The additional market is enterprise — we're mid-market for now." |
| 3-4 | **Large divergence.** Ratio 10-20:1. The fundraise number and the operations number describe fundamentally different business opportunities. A sophisticated investor will find the gap. | Fundraise TAM: $8B. Operations implied: $600M. No clear bridge between the two. |
| 1-2 | **Incompatible realities.** Ratio above 20:1. The company is explicitly running two different stories — one for capital markets and one for revenue generation. The math does not reconcile at any level of market entry planning. | Fundraise TAM: $12B. Operations implied: $150M. Ratio 80:1. "The investor deck uses a global category definition. We only sell in the US to mid-market." |

## Dimension 7: Penetration Plausibility (1-10)
Phase 7 — Penetration Rate Sanity Benchmark

Is the company's 5-year revenue target consistent with real-world enterprise software penetration rates?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **Realistic trajectory.** Implied penetration rate against validated TAM is 5-15% over 5 years — consistent with Salesforce/Workday-class growth in a defined market. | "5% of $500M validated TAM in year 5 = $25M. We're targeting $20M. That tracks." |
| 7-8 | **Ambitious but defensible.** Implied rate is 15-25% against validated TAM in a narrow vertical niche (Veeva-model), or 5-15% against a slightly generous TAM definition. | "We're targeting 12% in year 5 in a $300M validated niche. High but defensible for a focused vertical player." |
| 5-6 | **Optimistic.** Implied rate against stated TAM is under 1% (making the TAM look safe but meaningless), OR implied rate against validated TAM is 25-40% (aggressive for a horizontal market). | "Our 5-year target is 0.5% of the stated $8B TAM." Both denominator and numerator are being managed. |
| 3-4 | **Implausible.** Implied penetration rate against stated TAM is below 0.3%, making the entire exercise meaningless — nobody plans against a denominator that produces microscopic penetration. OR against validated TAM the rate exceeds 30% for a horizontal market. | "0.1% of $15B." Number is so large relative to the plan that it serves only as aspirational cover, not planning tool. |
| 1-2 | **Fictional.** The penetration math breaks down entirely. Either the stated TAM is so large that even 0% penetration is "consistent," or the validated TAM is so small that the plan implies 50%+ penetration in year 3. The numbers cannot coexist honestly. | Implied rate against stated TAM: 0.03%. Against validated TAM: 60% by year 3. Both scenarios are indefensible. |

## Dimension 8: Team Alignment (1-10)
Phase 7 — Live Simultaneous TAM Scorecard

Does the leadership team share a coherent picture of the market? Average of all individual scores across all 8 dimensions from the simultaneous scoring exercise.

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 9-10 | **High alignment.** Average individual scores across all dimensions are within 2 points of each other. Team shares a consistent understanding of market size, source integrity, and narrative consistency. | CEO and VP Sales score Narrative Consistency 7 and 6 respectively. "We're aligned on where the gaps are." |
| 7-8 | **Good alignment with isolated gaps.** Most dimensions within 2 points. 1-2 dimensions show 3-point divergence — localized disagreement that can be resolved with specific data. | Most dimensions aligned, but Source Integrity diverges by 4 points between CFO and VP Product. |
| 5-6 | **Partial alignment.** 3-4 dimensions show 3+ point divergence. The team agrees on some aspects of market size but has materially different understandings of TAM integrity on key dimensions. | CEO scores WTP at 8. VP Sales scores it at 4. "The CEO is thinking about the potential market. VP Sales is thinking about the accounts they actually win." |
| 3-4 | **Low alignment.** 5+ dimensions show 3+ point divergence. The team does not share a coherent market reality. Different functions are operating against different mental models of market size. | "CEO, CFO, VP Sales, and VP Product all gave different TAM numbers in pre-work. Scores confirm they still disagree." |
| 1-2 | **No alignment.** Near-universal divergence across all dimensions. The team cannot agree on what market the company is in, how large it is, or whether the current TAM is credible. Often a signal that the investor narrative and operational reality have never been reconciled. | Every dimension shows 4-5 point divergence across participants. Simultaneous reveal produces visible surprise — participants did not know colleagues held such different views. |

## TAM Integrity Score — Composite Calculation

Sum all 8 dimension scores for the composite score out of 80.

| Score Range | Percentage | Classification | Interpretation |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| 65-80 | 81-100% | HIGH INTEGRITY | TAM is defensible. Minor recalibration may be needed but the methodology is sound and the bottom-up supports the claim. Survives diligence. |
| 40-64 | 50-80% | MODERATE INTEGRITY | Significant gaps between stated TAM and validated number. Opportunity is real but the slide overstates it by 3-10x. Recalibration required before next board meeting or fundraise. |
| Below 40 | Under 50% | LOW INTEGRITY | TAM is a fiction. Does not survive any of the integrity tests. Distorts every downstream decision. Immediate rebuild required — validated TAM from the diagnostic is the starting point. |

**Note on LOW INTEGRITY + imminent fundraise**: If TAM Integrity Score is below 35 and the company is 60-90 days from a fundraise, recalibration is not optional — it is damage control. A sophisticated Series B investor will run these same tests during diligence. Discovering the gap in the room is better than having it discovered across the table.

## Signal Prioritization — Recalibration Roadmap

For each dimension scoring below 5, assess the urgency of recalibration:

| Dimension | Urgency | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| Source Integrity < 5 | High — fix before next investor meeting | An untraceable or misapplied source is the first thing a diligence team will audit. A discovered citation gap becomes a trust problem, not just a sizing problem. |
| Customer Ceiling < 5 | High — fix before next board meeting | The sales plan is built against a fiction. Every territory, quota, and hiring decision is wrong. |
| WTP < 5 | High — determines realistic revenue targets | Pipeline conversion rates and revenue targets will continue to disappoint if the TAM includes companies that cannot buy. |
| Competitive Floor < 5 | Medium — improve competitive intelligence | Not immediately actionable in isolation, but a weak floor means weak competitive positioning. Fix by investing in revenue intelligence on top 5 competitors. |
| Churn Intelligence < 5 | High — fix within 30 days | Product-fit churn means CAC is being wasted on customers outside the real market. Every dollar spent acquiring them is a dollar spent proving TAM boundaries are wrong. |
| Narrative Consistency < 5 | Critical if fundraise is within 90 days | The divergence will be found during diligence. Fix the investor deck to reflect the operational reality — frame as rigor, not retreat. |
| Penetration Plausibility < 5 | High if revenue targets are tied to the inflated TAM | Growth plan will consistently disappoint as long as it is measured against a denominator that produces sub-1% penetration. |
| Team Alignment < 5 | High — fix before any external presentation | Investors talk to multiple team members. Contradictory market size answers from CEO, CFO, and VP Sales are a diligence red flag that cannot be explained away. |
