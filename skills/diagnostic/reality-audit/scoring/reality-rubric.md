# Reality Audit — Scoring Rubric

## Overview
All scores are derived from the participant's own responses. Never assign a score without citing the specific answer that drove it. The goal is a diagnostic, not a performance — evidence beats conviction.

---

## Phase 2: Belief-Truth Gap

### Belief Integrity (1-10)
How well-supported by external evidence are the company's core beliefs?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | **Well-evidenced.** Core beliefs are grounded in external data, customer behavior, and competitive signals. Contradicting evidence has been actively sought and addressed. | Participant cites specific data sources, customer research, or market signals for each belief. Can name evidence that challenged the belief and explain how they addressed it. |
| 3-4 | **Mostly evidenced.** Most beliefs have external support, but one or two rely primarily on internal narrative or leadership conviction. | "We have data on most of this, but [Belief X] is more of a read on the market from our sales team." |
| 5-6 | **Mixed.** Some beliefs are evidence-grounded, others are conviction-based. The team conflates strong internal agreement with external validation. | Participant can point to data for some beliefs but defaults to "we believe" or "we know this market" for others. |
| 7-8 | **Primarily narrative-driven.** Most beliefs rest on internal conviction, historical experience, or leadership authority rather than external evidence. | "We have been doing this for 10 years, we know what the market wants." No external data cited. Contradicting evidence has not been sought. |
| 9-10 | **Fully narrative-driven.** Beliefs are treated as axioms, not hypotheses. External evidence that contradicts them is dismissed or not tracked. | Participant cannot name a single external data source. When challenged, defends belief on authority or experience. "That is just how this market works." |

### Belief-Consequence Exposure (1-10)
If the most dangerous belief is wrong, what is the strategic impact?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | **Contained.** The riskiest false belief would require a tactical adjustment, not a strategic rebuild. | "If we are wrong about [X], we would need to shift our go-to-market approach, but the core product and business model hold." |
| 3-4 | **Moderate.** A false belief would require meaningful strategy changes but the core model survives. | "If [X] is wrong, we would need to rethink our pricing and ICP — significant work but recoverable." |
| 5-6 | **Significant.** A false belief would materially change the strategy and require leadership alignment on a new direction. | "If [X] is wrong, the 18-month roadmap is probably wrong. That affects investment, hiring, and board narrative." |
| 7-8 | **Major.** A false belief would require rebuilding core elements of the strategy — positioning, product direction, or revenue model. | "If that assumption is wrong, honestly, the whole current strategy does not work. We would be starting over on the go-to-market." |
| 9-10 | **Existential.** A false belief would require abandoning the current strategy entirely. The company's trajectory depends on this belief being true. | "If [X] is wrong, the business case for our next funding round falls apart." |

### Public-Private Divergence (1-5)
Gap between what the team privately believes and what they publicly state.

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Aligned.** Private confidence matches public statements. What the team says in board meetings is what they actually believe. | Participant's answers sound consistent — no hedging, no "between us" qualifications. Confidence scores match public narrative. |
| 2 | **Minor gap.** One or two beliefs are stated publicly with slightly more confidence than privately felt. | "We present this to the board as settled, but honestly we are still watching to see if it holds." |
| 3 | **Noticeable gap.** Multiple beliefs are publicly performed with more confidence than privately held. The team is aware of the gap. | "If I am being honest with you, I think [Belief X] is shakier than we let on to the board." |
| 4 | **Material divergence.** The team routinely performs confidence they do not hold. There is an unspoken shared understanding that certain beliefs are not to be challenged publicly. | "That is one of those things where everyone knows it might not be true, but we do not say it out loud." |
| 5 | **Systemic performance.** The gap is structural — the company has developed a culture of performing certainty regardless of internal doubt. The private answer and the public answer have become two different languages. | Participant lowers their voice or hedges significantly when honest. "I probably should not say this, but..." followed by the real belief. |

## Phase 3: Assumption-Validation Gap

### Validation Coverage (1-10)
What proportion of strategy-driving assumptions have been tested with real external data?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | **Well-validated.** Most key assumptions have been tested with customers, market data, or controlled experiments. Kill criteria exist. | Participant can describe specific tests, what was learned, and how assumptions were revised based on results. |
| 3-4 | **Mostly validated.** The majority of assumptions have some external evidence, with one or two still partially tested. | "We have done customer research on most of these — [Assumption X] we are still gathering data on." |
| 5-6 | **Mixed.** Some assumptions validated, others untested. The team often conflates internal alignment with external validation. | "We validated this with our sales team and a few customers — but we have not run formal research." |
| 7-8 | **Largely untested.** Most assumptions driving the strategy have not been tested with external data. Validation is equated with internal discussion. | Participant describes customer conversations as validation without specifics on methodology or sample size. Cannot distinguish proven from assumed. |
| 9-10 | **Untested.** Core assumptions have never been validated externally. The team is executing against hypotheses they believe are facts. | "We have not formally tested it — it is based on our read of the market." Multiple high-consequence assumptions in this category. |

### Forecast Assumption Dependency (1-5)
How many forecast line items are directly dependent on untested assumptions?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Low dependency.** Fewer than 2 forecast assumptions are untested. Revenue model is grounded in validated behavior. |
| 2 | **Minor.** 2-3 untested assumptions in the forecast, none individually catastrophic. |
| 3 | **Moderate.** 3-4 untested assumptions, one of which drives a significant forecast line. |
| 4 | **High.** 4-5 untested assumptions. The forecast would materially change if any one of these is wrong. |
| 5 | **Critical.** The primary revenue forecast depends on assumptions that have never been tested. The forecast is structurally a guess. |

## Phase 4: Forecast-Reality Gap

### Forecast Accuracy Pattern (1-10)
How consistent and directional are forecast misses across the last 4 quarters?

| Score | Criteria | Evidence Pattern |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | **Accurate.** Forecasts are generally met. Variance is minor and non-directional. | "We were within 5-8% in 3 of 4 quarters. The one miss was a specific deal that slipped." |
| 3-4 | **Minor pattern.** Some misses, but variance is small and causes are diverse. No single assumption is repeatedly failing. | "We have been a bit optimistic on timing, but we are close on the numbers overall." |
| 5-6 | **Moderate pattern.** Consistent optimism bias or a specific assumption type appears in multiple misses. | "We tend to forecast deals closing a quarter earlier than they actually do." |
| 7-8 | **Clear pattern.** The same underlying assumption is driving misses across multiple quarters. The team adjusts numbers quarterly without addressing the root assumption. | "We have been off on [metric] for three straight quarters, but we think we have it right this time." |
| 9-10 | **Systemic failure.** The forecast is consistently and significantly wrong. Stated reasons do not match actual root causes. The team is managing board perception rather than managing assumptions. | Material variance every quarter. Official explanations are external factors while internal root causes go unaddressed. |

### Root Cause Honesty (1-5)
How accurately does the team's stated reason for forecast misses reflect the actual root cause?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Fully honest.** Stated board reasons match actual root causes. The team takes direct ownership of assumption failures. |
| 2 | **Mostly honest.** Minor softening of root cause language, but the substance is accurate. |
| 3 | **Partially honest.** Some root causes are stated accurately; others are attributed to external factors or execution when the real cause is a false assumption. |
| 4 | **Managed.** Stated reasons are consistently framed to minimize assumption accountability. The gap between stated and actual is understood internally but not surfaced. |
| 5 | **Perception-managed.** Stated reasons are systematically different from actual root causes. The board is being given a narrative, not a diagnosis. This is the highest-risk posture — it delays the reckoning while compounding the trust exposure. |

## Phase 5: Trust-Reality Gap

### Trust Exposure by Stakeholder (1-5 per group)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Minimal.** Stakeholder's belief about the company is fully grounded in current reality. Exposure of current gaps would not damage the relationship. |
| 2 | **Minor.** Stakeholder holds a slightly idealized view. Exposure would prompt questions but not damage trust. |
| 3 | **Significant.** Stakeholder's trust is based on a narrative that is partially outdated or partially unvalidated. Exposure would require deliberate repair work. |
| 4 | **Severe.** Stakeholder's trust is based on beliefs that have been materially contradicted by gaps found in the audit. Exposure would cause a credibility crisis in this relationship. |
| 5 | **Existential.** Stakeholder relationship is built on a false narrative. Exposure would likely trigger a board action, customer departure, or key talent exit. This is not a future risk — it is a deferred consequence. |

### Trust Basis Validity
For each stakeholder group, classify the validity of their current trust basis:

| Classification | Meaning |
|---------------|---------|
| **Valid** | The belief the stakeholder holds about the company is accurate given current evidence. |
| **Partially Valid** | The belief is grounded in some truth but contains meaningful inaccuracies or outdated assumptions. |
| **Invalid** | The belief the stakeholder holds does not match current reality. The trust is built on a narrative that has been contradicted by the audit. |

## Phase 6: Gap Severity Classification

### Overall Reality Gap Score
Average of Gap 1 through Gap 4 severity scores.

| Classification | Score Range | Interpretation |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Controlled** | 1.0–3.9 | Gaps exist but are contained. Structured monitoring and targeted testing is appropriate. No immediate crisis, but complacency is the risk. |
| **Accelerating** | 4.0–6.9 | Gaps are widening. Without structured validation and revision within 90 days, this moves toward Critical. Trust exposure is building. |
| **Critical** | 7.0–10.0 | Gaps are compounding. Trust exposure is high across multiple stakeholder groups. The strategy requires immediate reality testing. Inaction is not a neutral choice — it is active risk accumulation. |

### When to Escalate Classification
Regardless of average score, escalate to Critical if ANY of these conditions are true:
- A Gap 4 (Trust-Reality) score of 5 exists for board/investors or top customers
- Three or more quarters of forecast misses with the same root assumption
- Public-Private Divergence score of 5 (systemic performance of confidence)
- An untested assumption directly drives more than 30% of current revenue forecast

## Phase 7: Decision Quality

### Reconciliation Completeness (1-5)
How thoroughly did the team engage with the Defend/Revise/Abandon decisions?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | **Thorough.** Every major gap received a clear decision with named owner, specific timeline, evidence plan, and kill criteria. |
| 2 | **Mostly complete.** Most gaps decided with owners and timelines. One or two without kill criteria. |
| 3 | **Partial.** Decisions made but several lack named owners or specific timelines. Risk of decisions becoming intentions. |
| 4 | **Superficial.** Decisions are vague. Owners are committees or roles, not people. Timelines are quarters, not dates. |
| 5 | **Not actionable.** Decisions have been made in name but not in substance. No named owners, no dates, no kill criteria. The reconciliation will not drive action. |

### Signal Priority Scoring
For each gap classified as requiring action in the 90-day roadmap:

| Dimension | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|-----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Consequence** | Minor — adjustable without strategic impact | Noticeable — affects one strategy component | Significant — drives multiple components | Major — central to the current positioning gap | Critical — if unaddressed, accelerates to next classification |
| **Response Speed** | 12+ months to act meaningfully | 6-12 months | 3-6 months | 1-3 months | Can act this month |
| **Trust Exposure Cost** | No change if delayed 3 months | Minor — gap widens slightly | Moderate — stakeholder trust erodes measurably | High — window of credibility closing | Critical — 3-month delay = irreversible trust damage |

**Priority tiers:**
- **Tier 1 (First 30 days)**: Total score 12-15
- **Tier 2 (Days 31-60)**: Total score 9-11
- **Tier 3 (Days 61-90)**: Total score 6-8
- **Park**: Total score under 6
