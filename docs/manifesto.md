# Why Frameworks Decay (And What To Do About It)

## 1. The framework that quietly stopped working

In 1970, Bruce Henderson's Boston Consulting Group introduced the Growth-Share Matrix — Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs. Within a decade it was on every MBA syllabus and inside every Fortune 500 strategy deck. By the late 1990s, BCG itself was quietly distancing the firm from it. Henderson's own colleagues admitted in print that the framework's assumptions — stable market share as a proxy for cost position, predictable growth as a proxy for opportunity, the experience curve as a universal cost mechanic — had not survived the shift to knowledge-intensive industries, network effects, and platform economics.

Here is the uncomfortable part. Most companies stopped getting value from the Growth-Share Matrix sometime between 1985 and 1995. But the matrix kept appearing in board decks until at least 2015. I have personally seen it inside a strategy refresh deck written in 2023 by a top-ten global firm. Three full decades after it stopped working, executives were still being asked to sort their portfolio into Stars and Dogs.

That gap — between the moment a framework stops describing reality and the moment people stop using it — is what this essay is about. It is not a story about one bad model. The Growth-Share Matrix is healthier than most of what currently sits inside your strategy operating system. The point is darker: **every framework you rely on right now is decaying, and the discipline of noticing that decay is the only durable strategic moat left.**

## 2. The decay mechanism

A strategy framework is not a theorem. It is a snapshot. It is calibrated to a specific moment in a specific market with a specific buyer mental model and a specific competitive set. The minute that snapshot is taken, the world keeps moving and the framework starts aging.

Four forces drive the aging.

**Markets shift.** The five-forces analysis that worked when distribution was scarce stops working when distribution is infinite. The "blue ocean" you found in 2019 is a red ocean by 2023 because every podcast guest told a hundred founders to copy you. The Total Addressable Market your board signed off on was sized using a category that no longer exists in the form you described.

**Buyer mental models shift.** In 2018, "AI-powered" was a credibility signal in B2B SaaS. By 2024, it had become noise — buyers had heard it eight times before lunch. The exact same three words moved from differentiator to filler in roughly 36 months. If your messaging framework was tuned for the 2018 buyer, the 2024 buyer is reading right past you.

**Competitive sets shift.** The category you defined yourself against in your seed deck contains different companies than it did at Series B, and different companies again at Series C. Three of your five "main competitors" probably did not exist when your positioning was written. Yet most companies refresh competitive positioning roughly once per funding round, which means it lags reality by 18 to 24 months on average.

**Language drifts.** This is the one most people miss. The verbatim words customers use to describe their problem migrate constantly. Categories rename themselves. "Big data" became "data infrastructure" became "the modern data stack" became "data platforms" in less than a decade. Companies that anchored their positioning to the old vocabulary kept showing up in search results nobody was running anymore.

A framework that does not account for these four forces is not a strategy artifact. It is a fossil with a logo on it. The most expensive sentence in modern strategy work is *"we already did our positioning."* What it usually means is *"we already did our positioning in 2021 and have not looked at it since."*

The honest version of every framework should carry a freshness date the way milk does. Most do not, which is why most have spoiled in the fridge of your strategy deck.

## 3. The three stages of framework decay — Drift, Disconnect, Displacement

Frameworks do not break overnight. They decay in three measurable stages, each with observable signals. This is the same model the relevancy-audit skill applies to positioning, but it generalizes to every strategic framework you own — pricing, ICP definition, category narrative, sales motion, even your internal operating principles.

### Stage 1 — Drift

**Definition.** The framework still mostly works, but the world has moved 5–15% away from the assumptions baked into it. Most of the time you do not notice, because the framework is still producing recognizable outputs and most decisions made with it are still defensible.

**Measurable signals of Drift:**
- Win-rate on inbound is down 10–20% year-over-year but no single deal explains why.
- Of the top 12 terms your buyers use to describe their problem in discovery calls, 2–4 do not appear anywhere in your positioning.
- Your competitive set in your internal battlecards differs from the competitive set named in 50%+ of recent lost-deal reviews.
- Your ICP definition is 18+ months old and was last edited by someone no longer at the company.

Drift is the most dangerous stage because it does not feel like a problem. You can run an entire business on a drifting framework for two or three years and only notice you are losing in retrospect.

> A framework in Drift is a framework that still wins arguments inside the building and loses deals outside it.

### Stage 2 — Disconnect

**Definition.** The framework now actively misrepresents the market. Decisions made from it produce outcomes the framework did not predict. Internal alignment around the framework is becoming the thing that prevents you from seeing the gap.

**Measurable signals of Disconnect:**
- Of the top 12 verbatim terms buyers use, 5–8 do not appear in your positioning. ([Anonymized Petrichor case study, 2025: a Series B fintech had this exact gap — twelve buyer terms, zero matches.](https://github.com/petrichorprojects/resilience-stack))
- Sales is regularly winning deals "off-script" — the deals you close no longer match the playbook you have written down.
- Your pricing page generates objections your pricing rationale was specifically designed to neutralize.
- New hires read your strategy doc and quietly ignore it within 60 days.
- Analyst conversations require you to "translate" your positioning into the analyst's category language before they understand you.

Disconnect is when the framework starts costing real money. Each individual loss is explainable as a "weird deal" or a "tough quarter." In aggregate they are a structural signal that the model is no longer load-bearing.

> A framework in Disconnect is a framework you defend in meetings and override in deals.

### Stage 3 — Displacement

**Definition.** The market has moved to a successor framework — implicit or explicit, named or unnamed — and your model is now actively repelling buyers who already operate in the new one.

**Measurable signals of Displacement:**
- 9+ of the top 12 buyer verbatim terms do not appear in your positioning.
- Buyers describe your category using a noun you do not use, or refuse the noun you do use.
- Your category page on G2 / Capterra / Gartner sits inside a taxonomy you did not write and would not have chosen.
- Press coverage of your space defines the category in language your founder publicly disagrees with.
- Your strongest competitors have stopped trying to win the language war and have started reporting from inside the new one.

Displacement is terminal if untreated. The companies that fail to refresh in time do not lose to a better product; they lose to a better description of the same problem. The product can be superior and still lose the deal because the buyer cannot map it onto the mental model they already operate in.

> A framework in Displacement is a framework whose strongest defender is the founder, and whose strongest opponent is the market.

The three-stage model has one operational use: it forces a quarterly question. Of every strategic framework we operate, which stage is each one in? You should be able to point at every framework you own and assign it a stage with evidence.

## 4. Why most consultants miss this

If decay is so observable, why do most strategy firms not build their work around it? Three reasons, and none of them are flattering.

**Reason one — economic incentive.** A consultant who tells you your framework decays in 18 months sells one engagement. A consultant who tells you your framework is "the strategic foundation for the next decade" sells the same engagement at 4x the price and gets re-hired to defend the framework instead of replace it. The longer a framework can be sold as timeless, the longer the consultant can charge for being the keeper of its interpretation.

**Reason two — no measurement layer.** Most strategy work ships as a deck. A deck is not instrumented. It does not have a refresh trigger. It does not know when its inputs have drifted. The strategy team produces a 60-page artifact, the executive team gets briefed, and then everyone goes back to operating — at which point the deck enters the same lifecycle as a milk carton in the back of the fridge. Nobody is watching the expiration date because the artifact was never built to have one.

**Reason three — confusion between framework and finding.** The framework is the *method.* The finding is the *output.* Both decay, but at different rates and for different reasons. Most consultants ship the finding ("here is your positioning") and not the method ("here is how you re-derive your positioning in 90 days when the inputs have moved"). When the finding goes stale, the client has no instrument to refresh it without buying another engagement.

The result is a strategy industry optimized for selling the artifact and structurally unable to ship the maintenance system. This is not malicious. It is the gravity of how the work has been priced and sold for forty years.

> Most strategy consulting is the sale of a snapshot dressed up as a clock.

What this misses is that the snapshot is the easy part. The hard part — the part that takes discipline, instrumentation, and a measurement system — is detecting when the snapshot has stopped being true. That is the work most firms do not ship, because shipping it would shorten the engagement cycle and make the client less dependent.

## 5. What Petrichor does differently

Petrichor Projects starts every engagement from the opposite assumption. Every framework we ship is presumed to be decaying from the day it is delivered. The work is not to produce a timeless strategic truth; the work is to produce a strategy operating system that detects its own decay and tells you when it is time to refresh.

Three operational consequences follow.

**One — every framework ships with a refresh trigger.** When we deliver a positioning artifact, an ICP definition, or a category narrative, it ships with the specific measurable conditions under which it should be re-derived. Not "review annually." Specific conditions: when X% of top-12 buyer verbatim terms drift out of the positioning, refresh. When the named competitive set in 30% of lost-deal reviews diverges from the named set in your battlecards, refresh. When inbound win-rate moves more than 15 points without a clean attribution, refresh. The refresh trigger is part of the deliverable, not a footnote.

**Two — external evidence is the only evidence.** Internal opinion is excluded from refresh decisions. The only data that counts is external verbatim and external behavior — what buyers actually said in interviews, what prospects actually typed into the search bar, what lost-deal contacts actually wrote in their decision rationale. This is not because internal opinion is dumb. It is because internal opinion is exactly what calcifies the original framework past its decay window. The team that built the framework is the worst possible team to judge whether it has expired.

**Three — the discipline is the deliverable.** We do not sell wisdom. We sell the operating discipline of replacing wisdom on schedule. The framework you receive is less important than the cadence of refresh you receive. A merely-okay framework refreshed every nine months will out-execute a brilliant framework left frozen for three years. Compounding belongs to the refreshers, not to the originators.

> Reputation engineering is not the act of being right once. It is the discipline of staying right while the world keeps moving.

This is why Petrichor's flagship engagements are priced as systems, not as artifacts. The $45,000 reputation engineering engagement is not for the deck. It is for the instrument that detects when the deck has expired and re-derives it before the market notices.
